## Narrative Description

**"The River Wolves"**

Where the Zvezdan range across open steppes, the Yaroslav navigate the vast river networks that cut through northern forests and marshlands. They are river nomads, traveling in fortified boats, trading and raiding along waterways that connect distant territories. The Yaroslav are merchants and pirates, explorers and conquerors, a people who treat rivers as roads and boats as homes.

Yaroslav society revolves around **fleets**—family groups who share boats, trade goods, and military obligations. A fleet might include three to thirty vessels, from small river boats to large fortified trading ships. Leadership follows a combination of merit and lineage; the best navigator, the shrewdest trader, or the fiercest warrior might command a fleet, but hereditary claims matter when skills are equal. Fleets form larger **river coalitions** for mutual protection and major trading expeditions, but these coalitions are temporary and constantly shifting.

The Yaroslav are named for a legendary river king who supposedly united all northern waterways under his rule—a unification that lasted exactly one generation before fragmenting into competing fleets. Modern Yaroslav invoke Yaroslav's name while pursuing intensely local and factional interests, a contradiction they find unremarkable.

**Military Character**: Yaroslav warriors use distinctive pavise shields—large shields that can be planted like walls, turning their boats into floating fortresses. Their preferred tactic is amphibious assault: approach by river, establish a beachhead using pavise walls, and then deploy warriors to raid inland or besiege river fortifications. A Yaroslav fleet can appear hundreds of miles upriver, strike decisively, and escape downriver before land-based forces respond.

On land, Yaroslav fight as disciplined infantry using their oversized shields to form defensive walls. They're not light skirmishers like the Zvezdan but heavy shock troops who seize and hold key positions. Combined with their mobility, this makes them extraordinarily dangerous—they choose when and where to fight, then fight from prepared positions using superior equipment.

**Expansionism**: Yaroslav expansionism (2.3) is the highest among Slavic cultures, reflecting their unique capabilities. They expand along waterways, establishing trading posts that become fortified settlements that become new fleet bases. Over generations, Yaroslav presence pushes inland from rivers, integrating conquered peoples into their river-based culture. Former enemies become fleet members; former enemies' children become Yaroslav warriors.

**Economic Networks**: The Yaroslav control river trade across vast territories, moving furs, amber, slaves, and exotic goods from northern regions to southern markets and back. They speak multiple languages, maintain trading contacts across cultural boundaries, and operate as intermediaries between peoples who won't trade directly. A Yaroslav merchant at a Solemnium market is unremarkable; a Yaroslav fleet appearing in your river is terrifying.

**Cultural Values**: The Yaroslav celebrate **adaptability and pragmatism**. The river changes with seasons and years; survival means adapting to change, not resisting it. They honor warriors but also merchants, navigators, and diplomats—anyone who contributes to fleet success. Yaroslav have few absolute rules; instead, they make pragmatic decisions based on current circumstances.

This creates a culture outsiders find ethically flexible. The same Yaroslav who trades peacefully today might raid tomorrow, depending on opportunity and risk. The same fleet that honors treaties might break them if circumstances change. Yaroslav don't see this as hypocrisy but as responding appropriately to reality—rivers change course, why shouldn't people?

**Religious Practice**: The Yaroslav honor river spirits and practice divination by reading water patterns. Their shamans claim to predict floods, droughts, and propitious times for travel or warfare. Whether this represents genuine insight or sophisticated observation of natural patterns is debated, but Yaroslav shamans are rarely wrong enough to lose credibility.

**Internal Dynamics**: Yaroslav politics involves constant competition between fleets for control of profitable river routes and trading posts. This competition rarely becomes open warfare—destroying another fleet reduces overall Yaroslav power—but takes forms ranging from economic undercutting to strategic marriages to occasional "accidents" at river crossings. The system creates a dynamic equilibrium where ambitious fleets rise, overextend, and fragment while new fleets form from the pieces.

**Gender Relations**: Yaroslav culture is less egalitarian than Zvezdan but more so than settled cultures. Women commonly serve as navigators, traders, and healers, though combat roles are predominantly male. Fleet leadership is theoretically open to women but practically male-dominated, though notable exceptions exist and are celebrated in Yaroslav sagas.

**Relationship to the Strategists**: The Yaroslav's river-based networks make them invaluable for Strategist manipulation. Their trade routes move information as well as goods; their raids can destabilize targeted regions; their settlements can establish footholds in strategic territories. Some Strategists use Yaroslav fleets as mobile agents, influencing where they trade and raid to produce desired board states. Whether the Yaroslav recognize this influence is unclear—they might attribute Strategist manipulation to their own cleverness or to favorable spirits.
