## Narrative Description

**"The Endless Marchers"**

The Eldermark are what happens when Germanic organizational capacity meets territorial ambition. Occupying the rolling plains and coastal regions where the Germanic template meets the Latin south, they have built a society structured entirely around expansion. An Eldermark child learns to ride before learning to read; Eldermark marriages are often celebrated in newly conquered territories; Eldermark heroes are measured by the lands they claimed, not the enemies they slew.

Their name derives from the "Elder March"—a legendary migration four generations past when the proto-Eldermark traveled over a thousand miles in a single generation, absorbing or displacing everything in their path. Modern Eldermark retain that restless energy, but channel it through institutions rather than chaos. They practice **systematic conquest**: scouts identify targets, diplomats offer terms, armies secure territory, and administrators integrate the conquered. Within a generation, former enemies are sending their sons to serve in Eldermark armies, and within two generations, those sons are leading new conquests.

The Eldermark do not merely conquer—they **transform**. An Eldermark army marching through foreign territory leaves roads behind it. Eldermark occupation means new towns built on grid patterns, new laws replacing old customs, new temples erected to Eldermark gods (though often with conquered deities carefully syncretized into the pantheon). Some call this cultural genocide; the Eldermark call it civilization.

**Military Character**: The Eldermark field cavalry-heavy armies built around heavy lancers supported by mounted archers and mobile infantry. Their heater shields are lighter than Sturmgaard designs, favoring maneuverability over formation fighting. More importantly, they have perfected the art of **strategic mobility**—Eldermark armies can march twenty-five miles in a day, arrive in fighting condition, and be ready to march again the next morning. They win battles through positioning and logistics, often forcing enemies to fight on ground of Eldermark choosing.

**Internal Structure**: Eldermark society is organized around military districts called **Marklands**, each led by a Margrave who holds civil and military authority. Margraves are appointed by the High King but selected from local military nobility—a system that balances central authority with local autonomy. The tension between ambitious Margraves and royal oversight drives much Eldermark politics, with new conquests often serving as safety valves for internal pressures.

**Cultural Philosophy**: The Eldermark believe in **manifest destiny**—that their expansion is historically and divinely ordained. They see themselves as bringing order to chaos, replacing petty tribal conflicts with organized governance. This is not entirely propaganda; Eldermark-ruled territories generally experience reduced warfare, increased trade, and better infrastructure. Whether this justifies conquest is, naturally, a matter of perspective.

**Relationship to the Strategists**: The Eldermark are among the most frequently manipulated cultures in the game. Their high expansionism and organized military make them excellent instruments for Strategists seeking to disrupt the board. Some scholars theorize that the Elder March itself was Strategist-driven—a calculated migration that reshaped the entire northern political landscape. If so, the Eldermark remain unaware, attributing their success to their own virtue and martial prowess.
