## Narrative Description

**"The Covenant Cities"**

In the wake of the old empire's collapse, the coastal cities of what became Solemnium chose an unusual path: they federated. The Covenant of Solemnium binds thirteen independent city-states into a mutual defense pact, commercial union, and religious confederation—an arrangement that has survived two centuries of internal tensions and external pressures through a combination of shared interest, cultural identity, and genuine political innovation.

Each Solemnium city governs itself according to local custom—some are merchant oligarchies, others have guilds that rotate leadership, a few experiment with limited democracy. What unites them is the **Covenant Council**, which meets quarterly in rotating host cities to arbitrate disputes, coordinate defense, and manage the collective treasury. The Council operates by consensus when possible, supermajority when necessary, and has provisions for expelling member cities that violate core principles (provisions invoked three times in Covenant history, each nearly triggering civil war).

Solemnium culture celebrates **diversity within unity**. Their cities compete economically but cooperate militarily. Their merchants are cutthroat rivals who will unite instantly against foreign competition. Their artists develop distinct local styles that nonetheless share certain aesthetic commitments. The Solemnium wear their city's colors proudly but consider an insult to any Covenant city an insult to all.

**Economic Power**: The Solemnium control the richest maritime trade routes in the Latin world. Their ships—painted with horsehead prows in distinctive local styles—carry goods from the Berber coast to the Germanic north. More importantly, they've developed **banking institutions** that allow merchants to deposit funds in one city and withdraw them in another, facilitating trade across vast distances. Solemnium letters of credit are accepted in markets that refuse foreign coinage.

**Military Character**: The Covenant maintains no standing army, but each city must provide troops according to a formula based on population and wealth. In practice, this means Solemnium forces are a patchwork—some cities send disciplined infantry, others send marines, a few contribute cavalry. Coordination is managed by rotating military councils that plan campaigns months in advance. The system is inefficient but resilient; destroying one city's military capacity barely affects the whole.

More importantly, the Solemnium have developed **naval supremacy**. Their war galleys, built in standardized designs but customized by city, dominate coastal waters. Solemnium marines practice amphibious assault, Solemnium fire-ships carry alchemical weapons, and Solemnium admirals study currents and winds with the dedication other cultures reserve for theology.

**Internal Tensions**: The Covenant's greatest weakness is its own structure. Wealthy cities resent subsidizing poorer ones. Inland cities feel underrepresented compared to maritime powers. Religious differences (the Covenant includes cities that worship different aspects of the same gods) create periodic friction. Every Council meeting is a minor miracle of compromise; every major crisis threatens to shatter the whole arrangement.

**Cultural Identity**: The Solemnium see themselves as pragmatic, commercial, and civilized without being imperial. They mock Veridian pretensions while copying Veridian architecture. They trade with everyone while trusting no one. They value contracts over honor, results over tradition, and profit over glory—though they'll throw extravagant festivals to celebrate all three.

**Relationship to the Strategists**: The Covenant's federated structure makes it a fascinating puzzle for Strategists. Influencing one city doesn't control the whole; influencing the whole requires coordinating across multiple centers of power. Some Strategists work to exploit internal divisions, backing particular cities against others. Others use the Covenant as a stabilizing force, a counterweight to more volatile cultures. A few theorize that the Covenant itself might be a Strategist creation—an experiment in alternative governance structures—though evidence for this remains circumstantial.
