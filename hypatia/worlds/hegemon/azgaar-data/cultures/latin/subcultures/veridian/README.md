## Narrative Description

**"The Eternal Republic"**

The Veridian occupy the heartland of what was once a continent-spanning empire, and they have never forgotten it. Though the empire collapsed three centuries ago, Veridian culture remains defined by its republican institutions, its legal traditions, and its conviction that all other cultures are, at best, interesting variations on barbarism.

Veridian cities are built on ancient foundations—aqueducts still carry water, roads still connect distant provinces, and amphitheaters still host public games (though the gladiatorial combats now face periodic reform movements). The Veridian Senate still meets in the same marble building where it met when emperors ruled, still debates in the same formal Latin that was old when the empire was young, and still issues decrees that much of the known world treats as authoritative even when it lacks means to enforce them.

The Veridian are not an empire but they retain **imperial habits**. They issue citizenship to conquered peoples (eventually). They build infrastructure in allied territories (while extracting resources). They send governors to client states (who often stay for decades). They intervene in foreign conflicts (citing ancient treaty obligations). Their moderate-high expansionism (1.6) reflects not uncontrolled aggression but calculated opportunism—Veridian expansion happens through legal mechanisms, treaty obligations, and strategic marriages as often as through military conquest.

**Political Structure**: The Veridian Senate represents the landowning nobility—families who trace their ancestry to the empire's founding. Below them, the Assembly represents citizens (a category that expanded significantly after the empire's fall, when desperate senators granted citizenship in exchange for military service). Tension between Senate and Assembly drives Veridian politics: the Senate holds traditional authority and wealth, the Assembly holds numerical superiority and military power.

This tension produces **populares** and **optimates**—political factions that cut across class lines. Populares champion Assembly power, land reform, and expansion of citizenship; optimates defend senatorial prerogatives, traditional law, and restricted citizenship. The conflict is rarely violent (the fall of the empire taught the Veridian the cost of civil war) but perpetually intense, with every political question becoming a proxy battle in the larger struggle.

**Military Character**: The Veridian legion remains the gold standard for heavy infantry. Rectangular roman shields, short swords, heavy javelins, and iron discipline allow Veridian forces to defeat numerically superior enemies through superior cohesion and training. But Veridian military power is as much institutional as martial—their logistics system can supply armies deep in foreign territory, their engineering corps can fortify a camp in hours, and their medical corps ensures wounded soldiers survive to fight again.

**Cultural Contributions**: Veridian remains the diplomatic language of the Latin world and much beyond. Veridian law provides the template for legal systems across multiple cultures. Veridian architectural styles are copied (often badly) from the far north to the southern coasts. Veridian scholars preserve ancient texts, compile encyclopedias, and maintain the closest thing the world has to a universal library. The Veridian see this cultural dominance as proof of their superiority; others see it as the fading afterglow of a dead empire.

**Relationship to the Strategists**: The Veridian are frustratingly difficult pieces to manipulate. Their institutions provide stability that resists sudden shifts; their political culture incorporates debate and opposition, making it difficult to push them in any single direction. Some Strategists work through the populares-optimates divide, backing factions to create controlled chaos. Others use Veridian cultural prestige to influence neighboring peoples, making the Veridian an indirect instrument rather than a direct one.
