# Grytnes

**Type:** River | **Population:** 3.33 | **Culture:** Valthir

Grytnes controls the ford where the Gryt River—Lurland's primary inland waterway—can be safely crossed at low water. This makes it a natural chokepoint, and Grytnes has been fortified accordingly. A stone bridge spans the river (one of only three stone bridges in the entire Grand Duchy), and both approaches are commanded by squat fortified towers.

The city's economy is simple: **tolls**. Every merchant traveling between interior Lurland and the coast must either pay to cross at Grytnes or take a three-day detour. The toll is reasonable, the bridge is well-maintained, and violence against travelers is punished by immediate execution—Grytnes has learned that reputation matters more than squeezing maximum revenue from each crossing.

What makes Grytnes unusual is its **cultural position**. The city sits at the boundary between coastal Valthir culture (more open to change, influenced by foreign contact) and interior Valthir culture (deeply traditional, suspicious of outsiders). Grytnes itself is split—families on the western bank trace ancestry to coastal settlers, while eastern bank families descend from interior clans. This creates a permanent low-level tension that occasionally erupts into violence, usually during marriage negotiations or inheritance disputes.

**The Bridge Keeper:** Grytnes is governed by a hereditary Bridge Keeper, a position that alternates between western and eastern bank families every generation. The current Bridge Keeper is Astrid Grytborn, a western-banker who has held the position for twenty-three years by the simple expedient of refusing to die. She is 71, blind in one eye, and runs Grytnes with an iron fist that has kept the peace longer than anyone expected.

**Strategic Vulnerability:** Grytnes is the obvious target for anyone seeking to disrupt Lurland's internal trade. Destroy the bridge and the Grand Duchy's economy suffers immediately. Astrid Grytborn is acutely aware of this and maintains a permanent garrison of forty soldiers—enormous for a city of barely three hundred permanent residents.
