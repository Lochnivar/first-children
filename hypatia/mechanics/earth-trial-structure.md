# Earth Trial Structure

**Status:** Framework Element - Earth-Specific  
**Category:** Mechanics / World Operations  
**Related:** [Trials](trials.md), [Worlds README](../worlds/README.md)

## Overview

Earth undergoes a world-to-dungeon transformation during its trial. The structure below defines how roads, walls, and spaces create the dungeon topology. See [Trials](trials.md) for the general framework.

## Timing & Origin

Walls appear during the **Year of Preparation**—one year before the trial begins. They are not human built; how they arrive is unknown.

## The Basic Structure

Roads create corridors with walls on either side. The spaces between corridors are rooms. Rooms are not defined by county lines—they're defined organically by whatever corridor walls bound them. County lines are irrelevant to the physical structure.

## The Corridor Hierarchy

In general, any major, arterial, primary or secondary roadway (or international equivalent) qualifies as corridor material. US examples:
- **Interstates** — major corridors, primary arteries
- **US Highways** — secondary corridors
- **State Highways** — tertiary corridors, subdivide larger spaces into smaller rooms

All three tiers create walled corridors. County roads and local streets do not. **Rails do not count as corridors**; they are cut by walls but can be torn up and rerouted.

## Corridor Space

Medians and service plazas, when existing, are included as part of the highway. This provides room within corridors rather than thin, narrow roadways; they can develop into their own communities if needed.

## Plazas (Gaps)

Where two or more qualifying highways intersect, the corridor walls have gaps—openings that allow access between rooms and corridors. These aren't literal plazas but simply breaks in the walls, approximately half-kilometer radius around the intersection. Major surface roads with interchanges and limited access provide ready gateways; state highways (and international equivalents) do not typically have these interchanges. A T-junction creates a three-mouth gap; a full intersection creates four mouths.

**Preserved structures:** Overpasses and underpasses are preserved at intersections of major roadways. Bridges over major waterways are preserved; minor waterways probably preserved for uniformity.

## Visibility

You cannot see through walls or across them. Corridors are walled canyons. You can hear but not see into adjacent spaces. Walls are physical barriers with all the limitations that implies.

## Wall Extent

Walls extend underground—severing sewers, power, and data lines—and extend high enough to block aircraft and balloons. Exact height undetermined.

## Waterways

Rivers pass through tunnels in the walls where the walls bisect them, creating chokepoints. Where a river runs parallel to a corridor boundary the wall follows mid-channel or bankside. Rivers remain navigable and serve as escape valves and emergency passages, not primary corridors.

## What This Creates

- Urban areas get shredded into complex multi-room networks
- Small towns get bisected with one modest plaza if they're lucky
- Rural areas have large open rooms with minimal subdivision
- Parallel highways create stranded strips dependent on river access or distant plazas
- Regional map awareness becomes a critical survival skill
