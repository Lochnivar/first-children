# Human Adjacency Index

**Status:** Framework Element — Authorial Reference  
**Category:** Races / Visual Reference  
**Related:** [Races README](README.md), [Genetic Frameworks](genetic-frameworks.md)

## Purpose

A consistent authorial reference for two independent axes of species presentation: how human-presenting each species is visually (HAI), and how far their anatomical configuration has moved from animal baseline toward human-equivalent on the axis of secondary sexual characteristics and intimate anatomy (ConfAlpha).

Use this when writing descriptions, establishing scene tone, understanding social friction between species, or calibrating how a character registers in a crowd or in closer contact.

Neither index implies value hierarchy. The numbers describe presentation and configuration, not personhood.

---

## The HAI Scale

**1 — Pure animal form plus sapience.** The animal shape is entirely dominant. Bipedal function may be absent or marginal. The sapience is the surprise.

**5 — The midpoint.** You read "person" and "animal" in roughly equal measure simultaneously. The species identity and the personhood register together.

**10 — Human.** The baseline against which all other scores are measured.

---

## Band Descriptions

### Band 1–3 | Animal-Primary
*Identifiable at 200+ feet. The animal reads before the person does. Clothing and posture eventually signal sapience — the baseline assumption from a distance is animal.*

The sapience is something you discover, not something you assume. First-encounter scenes with Band 1–3 species almost always involve a moment of recalibration. Non-sapient animals exist in the same worlds; the initial read is not automatically wrong.

### Band 4–5 | Person-Plus-Animal
*Identifiable at 50–100 feet. Clearly a person at closer range; clearly their species at any range. The humanoid frame and posture read as sapient, but the species identity is always in the foreground.*

No recalibration moment, but no ambiguity either. In mixed-species settings these species are immediately legible. Their distinctive anatomy drives scene description — you write the shell, the beak, the trunk, then the person wearing it.

### Band 6–7 | The Working Middle
*Identifiable at 10–30 feet. Humanoid enough that clothing and posture read naturally; species features concentrated and legible. At significant distance, in dim light, or in crowds, might briefly pass for human before the markers register.*

The most versatile band for narrative. Enough humanity to carry scenes without constant anatomical description; enough distinctiveness to remain visually present. You establish the species once and return to it as needed rather than maintaining it continuously.

### Band 8–9 | Humanoid-First
*Identifiable at conversation distance. The "person" registers before the species does. Species markers are the accent, not the headline.*

In a crowd, these species might not immediately draw the eye as non-human. The specific markers (pointed ears, green-grey skin, unusual height) require attention to notice. Passing for human in dim light or from behind is plausible. Social friction based on species identity requires closer contact to trigger.

### Band 10 | Human
*The baseline. Not identifiable as non-human without specific knowledge.*

## Configuration Alpha (ConfAlpha)

ConfAlpha describes how far the anthro process moved a species from their animal baseline on the specific axis of secondary sexual characteristics and intimate anatomy. It is independent of HAI — a species can present as very human visually while retaining animal-baseline configuration, or present as clearly non-human while having humanoid-equivalent anatomy.

### What ConfAlpha Covers

**Female:** Placement and configuration of mammary anatomy; secondary sexual characteristics.  
**Male:** Scale and proportion toward human-functional range.  
**Both:** Overall intimate anatomical configuration.

### What ConfAlpha Does Not Cover

**Reproductive cycles** (egg-laying vs. live birth, mating frequency) — narrative texture noted in individual species entries where relevant, not indexed here.  
**Cross-species reproduction** — not possible regardless of ConfAlpha rating. ConfAlpha governs intimate compatibility, not reproductive outcome.  
**General body plan** (digitigrade legs, tails, fur, shells, feathers) — HAI territory.

### The Three Pressures

ConfAlpha is the product of three pressures acting on a species:

**Social niche** — species whose identity requires cross-species appeal or integration have strong pressure toward E. The Felari's entire function depends on being compelling across species lines; retaining animal-baseline configuration would undermine that at a structural level.

**Body plan constraints** — existing anatomy sets a ceiling on what changes are physically coherent. A shell, full feather covering, or complete scale coverage limits what can change and still make sense. Chelonari ConfAlpha A isn't a failure of the anthro process — it's the shell enforcing a body plan where certain changes would be structurally nonsensical.

**FC design intent** — Staff species engineered for human-adjacent operational environments cluster at D–E by design. Social compatibility was a feature specification, not an accident.

### The Scale

**A** — Original animal baseline. Configuration matches or closely approximates the non-sapient ancestor. No social integration pressure or body plan logic required change.

**B** — Animal-primary with adaptation. Upright posture and sapient development modified some characteristics; the animal baseline dominates. Cross-species intimate compatibility is limited.

**C** — Transitional. Meaningful blend of animal and humanoid configuration. Secondary characteristics may serve social display functions beyond purely biological ones.

**D** — Humanoid-primary. Predominantly human configuration with remaining species markers — texture, proportion variation, species-typical features. Cross-species intimate compatibility is functional. Kitsune is the type example: the fox is still present; it isn't the first thing encountered.

**E** — Human-equivalent. Configuration functionally indistinguishable from human baseline. FC Staff species cluster here by deliberate design. Felari is the panspermia type example: social function required it; cultural selection and the anthro process delivered it.

## Authorial Notes

### HAI and ConfAlpha Are Independent Axes
The most important thing to internalize about the two-index system is that they don't correlate. A species can be HAI 7 (reads as nearly human at a distance) and ConfAlpha B (animal-baseline configuration up close). A species can be HAI 4 (unmistakably non-human at any range) and ConfAlpha C. The visual read and the intimate read are different experiences and produce different social dynamics. Write them separately.

### The Gap Between HAI Band 4–5 and Band 8–9
The most socially significant gap in the HAI index. In mixed-species settings, Band 4–5 species carry their species identity in every scene they enter — there is no moment where their presence is not legible as "non-human." Band 8–9 species can pass, briefly, before they're read. This asymmetry produces different social dynamics: Band 4–5 species have never experienced ambiguity about how they're perceived; Band 8–9 species sometimes have. Neither experience is categorically better, but they are different, and characters built from each reflect that difference.

### The ConfAlpha Gap: Felari vs. Nekari
The sharpest ConfAlpha contrast in the index is between species sharing an identical biological baseline. Nekari and Felari are both Felis — same genus, same non-sapient ancestor. Their HAI scores are close. Their ConfAlpha scores are not. Nekari at B–C: precision work, solitary, no cross-species intimacy niche, no cultural pressure toward humanization. Felari at E: social presentation is identity; cross-species appeal is the function. Same body; different answer to the question of what to do with it.

### Body Plan Ceilings
Some ConfAlpha ratings are constrained by anatomy rather than social pressure. Chelonari and Aubrei at A isn't a comment on their social integration — it's the shell. There is nowhere for humanoid secondary characteristics to coherently land. Corvani at A is the feather covering and avian baseline; the body plan sets the ceiling regardless of social niche. When writing these species in intimate or social contexts, the body plan constraints are the scene, not a problem to route around.

### FC Staff Clustering
The FC Staff species (Homo genus) cluster at ConfAlpha D–E by design. Operational social compatibility in human-adjacent environments was a specification. Elves in particular were engineered for maximum cross-species appeal as PR staff — their E rating is a deliberate feature. The panspermia species arrive at their ConfAlpha through the combination of biological baseline, body plan, and social niche pressure rather than FC intent.

### Clothing, Anatomy, and Social Norms
HAI Band 4–5 and lower species require tailored clothing — standard humanoid garments don't fit a shell, a trunk, or a hippo frame. ConfAlpha intersects with this: a Band 6 species at ConfAlpha A may have different clothing requirements and norms than a Band 6 species at ConfAlpha E, even at identical HAI scores. When a society's tailoring tradition only serves the upper HAI bands, that's information about the society. When its social nudity norms only account for high ConfAlpha species, that's also information.

### The Fae Exception
Ilvari score HAI 8–9 not because they look human but because the wrongness is registered socially and emotionally rather than visually. You would pass an Ilvari on a street and register "person" — the recalibration happens later, in conversation, when something doesn't quite map. Their ConfAlpha E follows the same logic: they present as human-equivalent because they are substrate-direct expressions that coalesced into person-shaped forms. Whether that equivalence is biological in the conventional sense is an open question the index doesn't resolve.
