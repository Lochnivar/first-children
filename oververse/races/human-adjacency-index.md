# Human Adjacency Index

**Status:** Framework Element — Authorial Reference  
**Category:** Races / Visual Reference  
**Related:** [Races README](README.md), [Genetic Frameworks](genetic-frameworks.md)

## Purpose

A consistent authorial reference for how human-presenting each species is. Use this when writing descriptions, establishing scene tone, understanding social friction between species, or calibrating how a character registers in a crowd.

The index does not imply value hierarchy. A 3 is not lesser than an 8 — the number describes visual presentation, not personhood. A Chelonari is as fully sapient and socially functional as an Elf. They look completely different doing it.

---

## The Scale

**1 — Pure animal form plus sapience.** The animal shape is entirely dominant. Bipedal function may be absent or marginal. The sapience is the surprise.

**5 — The midpoint.** You read "person" and "animal" in roughly equal measure simultaneously. The species identity and the personhood register together.

**10 — Human.** The baseline against which all other scores are measured.

---

## Band Descriptions

### Band 1–3 | Animal-Primary
*Identifiable at 200+ feet. The animal reads before the person does. Clothing and posture eventually signal sapience — the baseline assumption from a distance is animal.*

The sapience is something you discover, not something you assume. First-encounter scenes with Band 1–3 species almost always involve a moment of recalibration. Non-sapient animals exist in the same worlds; the initial read is not automatically wrong.

### Band 4–5 | Person-Plus-Animal
*Identifiable at 50–100 feet. Clearly a person at closer range; clearly their species at any range. The humanoid frame and posture read as sapient, but the species identity is always in the foreground.*

No recalibration moment, but no ambiguity either. In mixed-species settings these species are immediately legible. Their distinctive anatomy drives scene description — you write the shell, the beak, the trunk, then the person wearing it.

### Band 6–7 | The Working Middle
*Identifiable at 10–30 feet. Humanoid enough that clothing and posture read naturally; species features concentrated and legible. At significant distance, in dim light, or in crowds, might briefly pass for human before the markers register.*

The most versatile band for narrative. Enough humanity to carry scenes without constant anatomical description; enough distinctiveness to remain visually present. You establish the species once and return to it as needed rather than maintaining it continuously.

### Band 8–9 | Humanoid-First
*Identifiable at conversation distance. The "person" registers before the species does. Species markers are the accent, not the headline.*

In a crowd, these species might not immediately draw the eye as non-human. The specific markers (pointed ears, green-grey skin, unusual height) require attention to notice. Passing for human in dim light or from behind is plausible. Social friction based on species identity requires closer contact to trigger.

### Band 10 | Human
*The baseline. Not identifiable as non-human without specific knowledge.*

## Authorial Notes

### The Gap Between Band 4–5 and Band 8–9
The most socially significant gap in the index. In mixed-species settings, Band 4–5 species carry their species identity in every scene they enter — there is no moment where their presence is not legible as "non-human." Band 8–9 species can pass, briefly, before they're read. This asymmetry produces different social dynamics: Band 4–5 species have never experienced ambiguity about how they're perceived; Band 8–9 species sometimes have. Neither experience is categorically better, but they are different, and characters built from each reflect that difference.

### Clothing and Anatomy
Band 4–5 and lower species require tailored clothing — standard humanoid garments don't fit a shell, a trunk, or a hippo frame. The fact that every society has developed tailoring traditions for the full range of anatomies present is a quiet indicator of genuine integration. When a society *hasn't* — when the tailors only serve Band 7+ species — that's legible information about the society.

### The Fae Exception
Ilvari score 8–9 not because they look human but because the wrongness is registered socially and emotionally rather than visually. You would pass an Ilvari on a street and register "person" — the recalibration happens later, in conversation, when something doesn't quite map. This makes them the only high-band species whose HAI score understates their actual otherness.

### FC Staff vs. Panspermia
The FC Staff species (Homo genus) cluster at Band 7–10 by design — they were built to function in environments where humans are the majority baseline and social friction costs operational efficiency. The panspermia species distribute across the full range because they were not built for human environments; they were built for ecological and behavioral niches.
