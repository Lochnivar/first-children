# Bandari (Raccoon People)

**Status:** Framework Element - Universal Race  
**Category:** Races / Species  
**Related:** [Genus Procyon](genus.md), [Races README](../README.md), [Genetic Frameworks](../genetic-frameworks.md), [Geliss](../gekko/geliss.md), [Kitsune](../vulpes/kitsune.md), [Kibo](../hippopotamus/kibo.md)

## One Line Summary

Think Browncoats who never lost a war because they never fought one — just kept moving, kept working, and kept out of everyone else's business.

---

## Overview

Bandari (*Procyon callidus*) are raccoon-derived anthropomorphic people seeded through FC panspermia. They occupy the gray areas between formal civilization and complete wilderness — the frontier settlements, the salvage routes, the back channels that keep the little guy moving when the big systems don't reach that far or don't care enough to try.

They aren't rebels. They aren't revolutionaries. They don't have a manifesto. They just want to do their work, take care of their crew, and not have to ask anyone's permission to exist. "The Man" rotates — could be an FC this week, a Bruin marshal next week, a Cayminus banking house the week after. The Bandari's position doesn't change. Whoever is currently trying to tell them what to do, extract a cut they didn't agree to, or make them fill out a form that serves someone else's interests is The Man. The Bandari would like The Man to mind his own business.

They are not heroes. They are not villains. They are the people who keep things moving when the big systems break down — usually with a workaround, a salvaged part, and someone in the crew who knows a guy.

**Singular/Plural:** Bandari

### Truism
*"Ain't no such thing as trash. Just stuff that ain't been useful yet."*

## Author's Narrative

The Bandari aren't anti any specific power. They're anti-whoever is currently trying to extract something from them they didn't agree to give. The FC, the Bruin marshal, the Cayminus banking house, the settlement council with the new tariff — The Man rotates. The Bandari's position doesn't.

They occupy the space the formal economy doesn't reach and doesn't particularly want to. The Kitsune connects worlds through contracts and relationships and Legara's network. The Bandari connects the settlement at the end of the dirt road to the rest of the world through salvage, side deals, and the stubborn refusal to accept that something can't be done just because the proper channels say so.

They're not against the Bruin. Law is fine as long as it stays in its lane. They're not against the Cayminus. Business is fine as long as the terms are fair. They're not against the FC. The FC can do whatever the FC does as long as it doesn't land on them specifically.

The crew is the thing. Not the ideology, not the politics, not the grand cause. The specific people who chose to stick together and look after each other when the big systems made clear they weren't going to do it. Once you're crew you're crew. The Bandari who'd sell out their crew for any amount of money or safety or ideological alignment hasn't been encountered yet and would not be recognized as Bandari by anyone who knew them.

The FC barely registers them. This is the highest possible compliment from the Bandari perspective.

## Universal Characteristics

### Physical Attributes
- **Height/Size:** 5'2"–6'0" — medium build, highly agile
- **Build:** Lean and wiry with surprising strength in hands and forearms; excellent balance and climbing ability; built for getting into places and getting back out
- **Lifespan:** 150–200 years — enough time to build real reputation on the frontier without becoming an institution
- **Distinctive:** Raccoon mask facial markings; highly dexterous hands with sensitive pads; ringed tail; keen night vision; sensitive whiskers; short dense fur in gray-brown with black accents

### Cognitive & Behavioral Traits

**Resourceful curiosity.** The default Bandari thought process when encountering anything is *"what can this become?"* Not just objects — situations, relationships, problems. Everything has potential application. The Bandari who looks at a broken transit gate and sees scrap is less experienced than the one who sees three solutions and a trade opportunity.

**Pragmatic adaptability.** They read situations and people quickly and find the path of least resistance — or most opportunity, which is sometimes the same path. Not as sophisticated as Kitsune relational intelligence. More opportunistic, less surgical. The Kitsune reads the room. The Bandari reads the exits and the loose panels.

**Anti-authoritarian by default.** Not ideologically. Just constitutionally. Whoever is currently representing institutional authority is someone the Bandari is instinctively calibrating around — not necessarily avoiding, just not automatically deferring to. *"What do they want, what do they cost, and do I need them?"* The answer determines the interaction.

**Crew loyalty.** Once someone is crew the Bandari is ride-or-die. Not performed loyalty — actual loyalty. The friendly scavenger who'll trade cheerfully with anyone disappears when the crew is threatened. What replaces it is cold, coordinated, and dangerous. Other species sometimes underestimate this transition. They tend not to make that mistake twice.

**Dry humor aimed at pretension and inefficiency.** Not the Gnoll's satirical precision. More situational — the Bandari finds the gap between how things are supposed to work and how they actually work consistently funny. Bureaucracy, formal hierarchies, elaborate protocols that exist to serve the protocol rather than the people — all reliable comedy. Self-deprecating too. They know what they are.

## Social Characteristics

### The Crew

The crew is the Bandari social unit and the only institutional loyalty that consistently holds. Not species loyalty — crew loyalty. A Bandari will look after another Bandari who isn't crew because that's just decent. They'll die for crew because that's just what crew means.

The crew is voluntary. You join because you chose to. You stay because you keep choosing to. You leave when you can't stomach the direction anymore — *"vote with your feet"* is the ultimate check on bad leadership. What you don't do is leave in the middle of a job. That's the one line that doesn't move.

**The Captain** earns the role through competence, fairness, and the demonstrated ability to keep the crew safe and functional. The Captain's word is law during a job — not because of authority but because someone has to make the call and the crew chose this person to make it. The authority is real. It is not permanent. It is not absolute outside the job context.

A Captain rumored to be coercing crew members finds themselves isolated quickly. Word spreads. Other Bandari crews refuse work, refuse docking, refuse the small courtesies that make frontier life functional. No formal enforcement. Just the collective withdrawal of a community that knows what crew is supposed to mean.

**The three things every Bandari understands without being told:**
- Follow the Captain or move on
- Don't leave in the middle of a job
- Live and let live

That's it. That's the whole code. Everything else is situational.

### On Authority

The Bandari relationship with authority is calibrated rather than oppositional. They're not picking fights with The Man. They're assessing The Man's cost-benefit ratio in real time and acting accordingly.

Bruin marshal keeping predators off the settlement — useful, worth cooperating with, jurisdiction acknowledged.

Same Bruin marshal asking questions about the cargo manifest — suddenly the Bandari has somewhere else to be.

This isn't dishonesty. It's the practical application of *"your systems are your business, our business is our business."* The Bandari knows exactly where their line is. They're just not going to tell you in advance.

### The Gray Area Economy

The Bandari occupy the economic space formal systems don't reach. Not the black market exactly — that implies deliberate illegality. More like the informal economy that exists because the formal economy has a minimum viable transaction size and a lot of people fall below it.

The settlement too small for a Kitsune contract. The salvage job that doesn't need documentation. The thing that needs moving between two places quietly. The repair that needs doing with whatever parts are available rather than whatever parts are specified.

The Bandari is the last mile. The Kitsune connects worlds. The Bandari connects the settlement at the end of the dirt road to the rest of the world.

This puts them in natural proximity to the Bruin — both operating at the frontier, both filling gaps the formal systems leave. The relationship is wary mutual tolerance built on the pragmatic recognition that the frontier needs both of them.

*"You stay out of my cargo, I'll stay out of your jurisdiction."*

Unspoken. Understood. Usually honored.

## Origin

FC panspermia-seeded from raccoon genetic template. No patron FC. No designated purpose. Left to develop independently after the seeding project moved on — which suits the Bandari fine. An FC patron would be The Man by definition.

They survived and spread not through strength but through adaptability. The raccoon base is worth noting: an opportunistic generalist that thrives in marginal environments, has highly dexterous hands, and demonstrates remarkable problem-solving capability. The Bandari inherited all of this and added sapience and the crew bond.

They didn't need a patron. They needed each other.

## Natural Gravitation

Resourceful curiosity + dexterous hands + pragmatic adaptability + crew loyalty + anti-authoritarian calibration →

- **Salvage and repair** — turning what others discard into what someone needs; the truism in action
- **Frontier trade** — moving goods between places formal trade networks don't reach
- **Information brokerage at street level** — not Kitsune sophistication; more "I know a guy and the guy knows a guy"
- **Fixers** — the person who knows how to make the thing work with the parts available rather than the parts specified
- **Smuggling** — not always, not exclusively, but the capability and the inclination exist and the frontier provides regular opportunity
- **Unconventional exploration** — going where formal expeditions don't bother because the profit margin isn't there

The Bruin foil is natural here. The Bruin sees the frontier as a place where law needs to be established. The Bandari sees the frontier as a place where they can operate without someone else's law telling them what to do. Same geography. Completely different relationship with it.

## Relationships

**Bruin:** The most textured relationship in the Bandari roster. Both occupy frontier space. Both fill gaps formal systems leave. The Bruin is law at the edge. The Bandari is economy at the edge. They need each other whether they'd admit it or not. The wary mutual tolerance is real and usually holds — until the Bruin's jurisdiction and the Bandari's cargo manifest intersect, at which point things get situationally complicated. Neither holds a grudge. It's just the nature of adjacent niches.

**Kitsune:** Natural but wary. Both read situations quickly. The Kitsune does it with surgical relational intelligence across formal networks. The Bandari does it opportunistically in informal spaces. They occasionally work the same territory from different angles. The Kitsune finds the Bandari useful for last-mile reach. The Bandari finds the Kitsune expensive but occasionally worth it for the bigger jobs.

**Cayminus:** Amusing friction. The Cayminus finds the Bandari unpredictable but commercially legible — the Bandari always wants fair terms and independence, which is a consistent negotiating position. The Bandari finds the Cayminus entertaining and worth watching carefully. Both are operating in the acquisition space. Different methods. Different scales. Occasional overlap.

**Gnoll:** Natural affinity. The Gnoll finds the Bandari's ability to turn any situation into an opportunity genuinely funny and genuinely impressive. The Bandari appreciates the Gnoll's complete lack of pretension and their accurate read on when things are actually serious. Good crew material if the Gnoll can handle the Captain's authority structure — which is situational.

**Kethi:** Surprisingly compatible. The Kethi holds the line. The Bandari finds the workaround that means the line doesn't need to be held as hard. Complementary problem-solving approaches. The Bandari finds Kethi anxiety responses exhausting in enclosed spaces. The Kethi finds the Bandari's casual relationship with authority alarming. Both find the other useful enough to manage the friction.

**Geliss:** Natural respect. The Geliss understands every formal system the Bandari operates around — therric knowledge of institutional contexts the Bandari navigates by instinct. The Bandari finds the Geliss useful for understanding what they're actually dealing with. The Geliss finds the Bandari's gray area operation interesting research. When official curiosity gets too specific, having a Geliss nearby is often useful — and vice versa.

**Kibo:** The Bandari finds the Kibo's enthusiastic commitment to the job genuinely refreshing — a species that does what it says it'll do and brings full energy to it is extremely useful crew material. The Kibo finds the Bandari's resourcefulness complementary to their own directness. Good working relationship. The Kibo handles the thing. The Bandari figures out what the thing actually is.

**FC generally:** The Man, potentially. The FC that leaves them alone is fine. The FC that takes an interest is a problem to be navigated around. The Bandari has no theological relationship with the FC and no interest in developing one.

## Governance & Politics

None formal. The crew structure is the only governance that consistently holds and it's entirely voluntary. External governance is The Man until proven otherwise — not automatically opposed, just automatically assessed for cost and necessity.

Bandari communities where they exist are loose affiliations of crews with shared understanding rather than formal institutions. The Captain network enforces the three tenets through collective social pressure rather than anything resembling law. It works well enough.

---

## Legal Code

The three tenets. Everything else is situational.

Formal legal systems are external impositions to be navigated rather than internalized frameworks. The Bandari honors agreements because breaking agreements is bad for business and bad for the crew — not because of reverence for law. The distinction matters to them even when it doesn't show externally.

---

## Theology

Minimal formal theology. The FC as patron concept is actively unappealing — a patron is The Man with extra steps. Any theology that has emerged centers on the crew bond and the road rather than creator figures.

The closest thing to a Bandari theological position is the truism itself: *"Ain't no such thing as trash. Just stuff that ain't been useful yet."* Applied broadly enough this becomes a cosmology — nothing is wasted, everything has potential application, the universe is full of things waiting to become something else. The Bandari who finds a use for what everyone else discarded isn't just being resourceful. They're being correct about how things work.

The FC origin question gets the same treatment as everything else institutional. Something seeded them. Fine. That something is not currently relevant to the job.

## Variations Across Worlds

Crew loyalty and anti-authoritarian calibration are consistent. Specific expressions vary — urban scavengers, frontier salvagers, wandering fixers, information brokers, unconventional traders. The gray area economy takes different shapes in different environments. The Bandari finds it regardless.

## Naming Conventions

### Philosophy

Bandari names are crew-given and carried. No family names. No formal registry names distinct from the working name. No elaborate dual systems. Just the name the crew saw fit to give you based on something they noticed — and then it's yours.

The name travels with you when you move crews because it's *yours* now, not the old crew's name for you. New crew picks it up because that's what you're called. The only reason a name changes is collision — there's already a Maro in this crew and two Maros creates operational confusion the Bandari finds inefficient. In that case a new name emerges from the new crew observing something, the same way the first one did. No grief about it. The old name served its purpose. Something else fits now.

A Bandari who has kept the same name across multiple crews has either been lucky about collisions or has a name distinctive enough that it doesn't come up. Either way the name has weight — not institutional weight, just the weight of having been yours for a long time across different contexts.

The Hobgoblin registry, when it needs to document a Bandari, gets a single name and a blank stare when it asks for the family name field.

*"Just the one."*
*"We need a family designation for the—"*
*"Yep. Just the one."*

### On Collision Resolution

When a new crew already has the name you're carrying the resolution is practical and quick. The crew observes something. A name emerges. You take it. The old name is noted — *"used to be Maro, now Kell"* — and that's the extent of the record.

The Bandari who has gone through several names has moved a lot or been unlucky about collisions or both. The name history is part of the personal history. Not shameful. Just true.

*"What do they call you?"*
*"Senna. Was Tella before, but there was already a Tella."*
*"Before that?"*
*"Rovi."*
*"Long road."*
*"Yep."*

### Example Profiles

**Tella**
- Crew-given: the crew noticed she always knew where things were even when nobody else did
- Carried across: two crew changes; no collision yet
- Current status: three crews in, same name; starting to feel like it fits all the way down

**Maro**
- Crew-given: first crew called him this; nobody remembers exactly why; it just fit
- Carried across: one crew change; collision on the second; became Kell
- Current status: goes by Kell now; still answers to Maro from people who knew him before; doesn't correct them

**Senna**
- Crew-given: the smell of something she was cooking when she joined; the crew ate well that night; the name stuck
- Carried across: four crew changes; no collision
- Current status: the name has been hers long enough that it doesn't feel crew-given anymore; just hers

**Voss**
- Crew-given: short for something longer that nobody uses anymore
- Carried across: multiple crews; one collision resolved by becoming Fell for two years before reverting
- Current status: Voss again; the Fell period is noted in conversation occasionally; Voss finds this faintly amusing

**Dalla**
- Crew-given: new crew; name arrived three days in after something the crew observed
- Previous name: Carra; collision on joining
- Current status: still getting used to Dalla; Carra comes out occasionally under pressure; the crew corrects her gently
