# Tavern Faite
*The common man's game. Fast, loud, and brutal.*

---

## The Deck

A standard 52-card deck plus two Jokers. Suits exist to differentiate cards of the same rank — they carry no strategic weight in Tavern Faite. Rank is everything.

**Ranks low to high:** 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, Jack, Queen, King, Ace, Joker

**The Jokers outrank everything, sitting above Aces.** They are never played as trick cards — they are spent as activated abilities. Joker rank exists solely to determine Sideboard positioning.

**The Jokers:**
- **Seelie (Bright Fool)** — marked or agreed upon before play
- **Unseelie (Shadow Fool)** — the other one

---

## Setup

1. Before shuffling, remove two random cards and place them **face-up in the center** — the **Sideboard**. All players can see them throughout the round. The Sideboard is always stocked regardless of how many cards are in the deck — even if a Standing Gridlock has locked one or both Jokers out of circulation.
2. Shuffle the remaining deck and deal **5 cards** to each player.
3. All players **ante** into the pot.
4. The player to the dealer's left goes first. Play proceeds clockwise from there.

---

## Playing a Round

A round consists of a minimum of **5 tricks**. Each trick, the next player clockwise plays first, followed by all other players in clockwise order. There is no lead restriction — players may play any card they choose regardless of what has been played before them.

**Each trick:**
1. The first player in clockwise order plays one card face-up.
2. All other players play one card face-up in clockwise turn.
3. **Highest rank wins the trick.** The winner takes all played cards into their personal pile.

Suits do not affect winning or losing. A Seven of Hearts and a Seven of Spades are equal in rank.

After Trick 5, if all Gridlocks are resolved, the round ends and the pot is settled. If a Gridlock is still active, play continues into Trick 6 and beyond until it breaks or the deck exhausts. See **End of Round — Scoring** for full resolution.

---

## Cascading Ties — The Gridlock

If two or more players tie for the highest rank in a trick:

- No one wins. All played cards are frozen in the center — the **Gridlock**.
- Play continues clockwise as normal into the next trick.
- The winner of the next trick claims it **plus all frozen Gridlock cards**.
- If that trick also ties, everything freezes again and cascades further.
- Ties can cascade across multiple tricks.

**Strategic note:** Players may deliberately play low into a cascade, sacrificing a trick to conserve high cards. Letting a cascade grow and breaking it late is a legitimate comeback strategy. Feeding a cascade is not weakness — it may be patience. A player who is behind on card count may find that growing the cascade and breaking it late is their only path back.

## The Jokers

### Seelie Joker — The Bright Fool

The Seelie may be played **at any point during the round** — on your turn, between other players' plays, even mid-trick before you've played your trick card.

**Effect:** Discard the Seelie and immediately take **either Sideboard card** into your hand. The Seelie is retired from the round.

What you do with the acquired card is normal play — it may be played immediately as your trick card or held for later.

**Notes:**
- The Seelie's value depends entirely on what is sitting on the Sideboard. A weak Sideboard makes the Bright Fool a poor trade.
- Because the Sideboard is visible to all players, everyone at the table knows exactly how good or bad that trade is — including the player holding the Bright Fool.
- If the Unseelie has already been played, one Sideboard card is already gone. The Seelie player takes whichever remains.
- When the Seelie is played, it is discarded into the trick pile of the round it was activated in. The Sideboard card is taken into hand. The winner of that trick claims the Seelie as part of their card count.

### Unseelie Joker — The Shadow Fool

The Unseelie may be played **at the start of any trick**, before any cards are played.

**Effect:**
1. The Unseelie player places the Shadow Fool face-up (all players see who triggered it) and retires it.
2. All players — including the Unseelie player — play their trick card **face-down**.
3. A **round of betting** occurs before the reveal. Players may raise, call, or fold.
4. All face-down cards are **flipped simultaneously** and resolved normally. Highest rank wins. Ties cascade as usual.

**The cost:** The Unseelie player plays two cards this trick — the Joker and their normal play card. After the trick resolves, the Unseelie player takes the **lower ranked Sideboard card** into their hand. Hand size is restored.

**Notes:**
- The Shadow Fool announces itself. It does not sneak. Everyone knows who flipped the table.
- Players who fold forfeit any claim to the trick — their card is still flipped with the rest and goes to the winner's pile.
- Played against a growing Gridlock, the Unseelie blinds the table at exactly the wrong moment for anyone with a plan.
- The Unseelie player always gets the worse end of the Sideboard as the price of chaos. If the Seelie has already been played, one Sideboard card is already gone and the Unseelie player takes whichever remains.
- Because Jokers outrank Aces, the Unseelie can never claim a Joker from the Sideboard.
- When the Unseelie is played, it goes into the pile of the trick in which it was activated. The winner of that trick claims it as part of their card count. The Shadow Fool is part of the card economy — it does not retire to a neutral zone.

## Sideboard Joker Scenarios

Since Jokers outrank all standard cards, their appearance on the Sideboard creates special situations:

**Seelie on the Sideboard:** The Bright Fool is locked away for the entire round. No player can reach it — the Unseelie cannot claim it (it is the higher card), and there is only one Seelie in the deck. It watches from the table and does nothing.

**Unseelie on the Sideboard:** The Seelie player may choose to claim it, converting the Bright Fool into the Shadow Fool mid-round. A rare and dangerous trade.

**Both Jokers on the Sideboard:** Neither can be claimed. The Fools are watching. Players settle into a clean card game underneath two grinning witnesses. At many tables this is considered an omen — something significant is about to happen in the world outside the cards.

## End of Round — Scoring

A round consists of a **minimum of 5 tricks**. The round does not end at trick five — it ends when all Gridlocks are resolved and a winner has claimed every frozen pile.

**If Trick Five results in a Gridlock:** Play continues clockwise into Trick Six, Seven, and beyond using whatever cards remain in players' hands. The round ends only when the Gridlock is broken and all frozen cards are claimed.

**If the draw deck is exhausted and a Gridlock remains unresolved:** The frozen pile stays exactly where it is — untouched, visible, in the center of the table. This is a **Standing Gridlock**. All cards not in the frozen pile are gathered, reshuffled, and new hands are dealt. No new ante. The round continues — the next trick resolves the Standing Gridlock, and the winner claims those trick cards plus the entire frozen pile.

**Jokers in a Standing Gridlock:** If either Joker was played into the trick that became the Standing Gridlock, it is locked inside the frozen pile. The reshuffle proceeds without it — the deck is one or two cards lighter and everyone knows why.

**Jokers not yet played:** If a Joker was still in a player's hand when the deck exhausted, it returns to the reshuffle with all other non-frozen cards. A player may draw a Joker with a Standing Gridlock already on the table. That player has an immediate weapon pointed at the next trick.

A Standing Gridlock is a physical presence — the frozen pile sits in the center of the table, untouched, for everyone to see. No tokens, no written counts, no memory required. The cards are the record. They sit there until someone wins them.

The Sideboard is always stocked on reshuffle — two cards pulled before dealing regardless of how many cards remain in circulation. If both Jokers are locked in the Standing Gridlock, the reshuffle plays with a lighter deck, no Jokers in anyone's hand, no Jokers on the Sideboard. Pure card game until someone breaks the freeze and the Fools come back.

A Standing Gridlock that survives a reshuffle is the kind of thing people remember. The pot keeps growing. The pressure keeps building. Someone is going to break it eventually.

Once all Gridlocks are resolved, each player counts **every card in their collected pile** — all trick cards won throughout the round, including any Jokers collected as trick winnings.

**Most cards wins the pot.**

A new round then begins: collect all cards, shuffle, set a new Sideboard, deal fresh hands, and ante again.

## Strategic Notes

The economy of Tavern Faite is **cards, not tricks**. Every trick you win transfers those cards to your pile. A Gridlock cascade isn't just a big moment — it's a massive card count swing. The whole table can see the frozen pile growing and calculate exactly what breaking it means.

**The core decisions:**
- **Break the cascade** — spend a high card, claim the pile, take the count lead.
- **Feed the cascade** — play low, let someone else break it, conserve your high cards.
- **Starve your opponent** — if you're behind, growing the cascade and breaking it late may be your only path back.

A player who keeps playing low into a cascade is either very confident or very scared. Reading which one is the real game.

Sometimes the cards are just the cards. A dominant hand — three Aces, a King and a Queen — may simply win. The Jokers are powerful tools but they are not miracles. A blowout hand is part of the game.

## The Sideboard

Two cards removed before the round are visible to all players throughout. The Sideboard serves three purposes:

1. It is the only source of cards either **Joker** can access.
2. It signals to all players what **isn't** in the deck — information with real strategic weight.
3. It creates a live tension element: a strong Sideboard card means whoever holds the Bright Fool has a decision to make, and whoever holds the Shadow Fool knows exactly what they're settling for.

The Sideboard is read-only for all players except the Joker holders. No other player may access it under any circumstances.

---

## A Note on How People Actually Play

The rules above describe strict Tavern Faite — clockwise, one card at a time, every play observed.

In practice, casual players throw their cards down simultaneously, laugh at the chaos, and argue about who played what. Nobody's counting cards seriously. Nobody cares who goes first. It's a social game and the rules are furniture.

Strict turn-by-turn play means something. When you walk into a tavern and see a Faite table playing by the rules — really playing, one card at a time, nobody talking, eyes on every hand — you know the stakes are real. Sit down carefully or don't sit down at all.

The way people play tells you everything about the table before a word is spoken.
