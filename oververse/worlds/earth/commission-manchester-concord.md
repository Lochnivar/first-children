## AI Primer — Minimum Context

> **If anything below is unclear or you need more depth on a concept, ask before proceeding.** The canonical detail lives in `trial-mechanics.md`, `manchester-nh.md`, and `world-building/institutions/corp.md`. Do not develop ideas in a vacuum when source material is available.

**The situation in one sentence:** The FC (First Children — the governing body of the wider multiverse) activated a signal called the QWAN on Earth as a sandbox experiment, accidentally triggered a physical cascade that destroyed combustion infrastructure worldwide, got results they didn't anticipate, and walked away. A Commission of multiverse staff came through a terminus that opened in Manchester NH, built walls dividing Earth into rooms using existing administrative boundaries, invented a "humanity is being evaluated for integration" trial narrative to give the situation meaning, and has been managing the mess ever since.

**Key terms:**
- **FC (First Children):** The multiverse's governing body. Ran the QWAN experiment, walked away. Not present on Earth.
- **QWAN:** The signal the FC activated. Still running. Interacts with combustion chemistry — explosives unstable, engines non-functional. Also activated human pineal glands as biological toll hardware.
- **The Commission:** The administrative body that came through the Amoskeag terminus to manage the post-QWAN chaos. Primarily Homo genus staffers (elves, hobgoblins, dwarves, halflings). No enforcement force. Runs on toll revenue.
- **The trial narrative:** The Commission's official story — humanity is being evaluated for integration into the wider multiverse. Entirely Commission-constructed; no FC authorization. Most non-humans on Earth know this. All of them signed an NDA at Amoskeag customs promising not to tell humans.
- **Amoskeag terminus:** The only known point of entry to Earth from the wider multiverse. Located in Manchester NH. The reason everything happens here.
- **Rooms:** The wall system's primary zones — defined by administrative boundaries (town lines, city limits, county lines). Movement between rooms requires passing through a Gatelock and paying a toll.
- **Gatelocks:** Controlled thresholds where roads cross room boundaries. Impose a toll. The toll is friction — makes crossings deliberate, generates data for the integration metric.
- **Integration metric:** The Commission's measure of genuine cross-species cooperation on each wall segment. Builds toward wall dissolution (~90% = walls dissolve), collapses back toward wall reformation (~50% = walls reform). Progress is not permanent.
- **CORP (Commission Outreach Resource Personnel):** Paid human employees — recruited and trained in Manchester — who do community outreach on behalf of the Commission. Called *Corpses* by Earth Front.
- **Earth Front:** Human resistance movement. Four wings — principled opposition, pragmatic, political (rebuilding human governance), hardline (collaborator accusers). Not unified. Kernels formed during the Summer of Chaos.
- **The Summer of Chaos:** The period between QWAN activation and the Fall — no walls yet, no alien presence, humans organizing on their own. The founding wound. Earth Front's legitimacy comes from here.
- **The Fall:** When the Amoskeag terminus opened and the Commission came through into a world mid-chaos. The walls came from them. The trial narrative was assembled alongside the walls.
- **Manchester city government:** The elected city government of Manchester NH. Still functioning. Has procedural friction with the Commission but genuine mutual respect. Answers to constituents who distrust the Commission.
- **Concord:** New Hampshire's state capital, 18 miles up I-93 from Manchester — four townships away, a direct corridor shot. Seat of NH state government. Travel is horse-drawn wagon pace, so a same-day trip with planning, not a quick visit. Close enough for regular contact and firmly within Manchester's sphere of influence. Has zero formal standing in the Commission's wall framework despite that proximity.

## Scope Note

**The federal government is off-stage and out of scope.** Washington DC has its own regional situation and is not attending to NH politics. There is no federal cavalry, no federal court to appeal to, no higher human authority above the state level that any party in this triangle can invoke. This is deliberate. The NH state government is therefore the highest surviving human institutional structure with any reach in this region — which increases its motivation to assert itself against the Commission and sets Concord as the hard ceiling for Earth Front's political wing.

Do not develop federal angles. If a scenario requires escalation above the state level, it goes nowhere. That dead end is the point.

## The Structural Gap

The Commission built its room system on administrative boundaries — but at the local level. Town lines. City limits. County boundaries. The state of New Hampshire exists, has a governor, presumably has a legislature still meeting in Concord, and has **zero formal standing in the Commission's architecture.**

That is not an accident and not an oversight. The Commission used the finest-grained administrative infrastructure available because fine-grained creates signal. State boundaries are coarse — too coarse to measure integration at the neighborhood level where the metric actually lives.

But the Commission never had to explain this to the governor. They just built what they built.

## The Three Relationships

### Commission vs. NH State (Concord)

The Commission's posture toward the Manchester/Concord political tension is essentially: **not our problem.** They have a term for it internally — Normal Human BS. They've seen planetary politics before. A city government and a state government arguing over jurisdiction and institutional legitimacy while walls divide their county lines is recognizable noise. The integration metric will reflect whatever happens. The Commission does not intervene in human governance disputes.

This aloofness has two distinct components that are easy to conflate but shouldn't be:

**Pattern recognition:** They've seen this before. It reads as normal institutional friction in a first-contact situation. It will likely resolve or it won't. The metric will show it either way.

**Deliberate non-partisanship:** Even when they have a read on what's happening, they cannot take sides. If the Commission visibly backs Manchester city against the state, they become a political actor. That hands Earth Front the occupation narrative on a plate — alien institution picking human winners — and tanks the integration metric on every segment they touch. If they validate the state's claims, they delegitimize Manchester city's working relationship and undercut everything CORP has built. Any move the Commission makes in human governance disputes is a political move. Staying out is not just indifference. It is policy.

The two components reinforce each other but they're not the same thing. The Commission would stay out even if they cared deeply about the outcome. The aloofness is partly a trap they're deliberately staying inside.

The Commission has two tripwires and ignores everything else:
- **Infrastructure:** If the Gatelocks, terminus, corridor walls, or Cayminus payment systems are physically interfered with, the Commission responds.
- **Alien safety:** If alien contractors are harassed, threatened, or accosted, the Commission responds.

Short of those two conditions, the NH state legislature can pass resolutions, hold hearings, and declare the Commission's presence an occupation under state law. The Commission will file the hearing notes and move on. The governor can make the corridor run to Manchester and stand on the Millyard steps. The Commission will send someone to accept the letter.

This aloofness is not contempt — it is institutional pattern recognition. They have seen this before. They are watching the metric. If the political noise tanks the integration score on Manchester-adjacent segments, that's data. They will note it. They will not involve themselves in the cause.

**The specific frustration this creates:** The state cannot get the Commission's attention through normal political pressure. There is no negotiating with something that isn't paying attention. The governor has an audience — NH's "Live Free or Die" cultural DNA makes the grievance legible everywhere outside Manchester — and no one to perform for. The Commission is not watching the performance. It filed the press release.

**The only way to get Commission attention** is to cross a tripwire. Earth Front's hardline wing probably knows this and is tempted. Earth Front's political wing knows that crossing a tripwire would be catastrophic for every project they're trying to build. That tension runs through the coalition.

### Manchester City vs. NH State

Concord has bigger problems than Manchester — and the biggest problem is that New Hampshire as a functioning state is being shattered by the wall system itself.

**The physical reality:** Every town boundary is a Gatelock crossing with a toll. Every county line is another. Moving from Berlin in Coos County to Concord at horse-drawn wagon pace, through multiple county walls and town walls, is a multi-day expedition. A state representative from the North Country isn't commuting to the legislature. They may not be coming at all. Rooms that were already remote before the walls are now effectively unreachable on any practical governance timescale. The state doesn't govern them. It has a legal claim over them that nobody is enforcing.

**What NH actually is now:** A cluster of rooms along the I-93 and I-89 corridor spines that can still maintain enough contact to call themselves a state. Concord. Manchester. The seacoast, maybe. The Merrimack Valley towns within corridor reach. Everything else is fragmenting — not through dramatic rupture but through attrition.

The corridor is why some rooms hold and others don't. Entry to a corridor costs one Gatelock toll, then free movement. A room with a direct corridor interchange is reachable with a single crossing — Concord can send someone regularly, get a response, follow up. The frequency is there. The governance relationship is maintainable.

One room off the corridor is already harder. Two crossings, two tolls, at wagon pace. Doable but an expedition. Contact drops from weekly to monthly. The relationship thins.

Beyond that it doesn't stick. Not because those rooms are necessarily hostile — though some are — but because the friction makes contact too sporadic to sustain anything functional. Concord probably tried. Sent representatives north and west. The visits were rare enough that by the time they came back the room had made its own arrangements and the state's position in it had become theoretical. No dramatic break. The relationship required maintenance Concord couldn't provide at that distance, and it quietly dissolved.

Coos County might as well be a different world. Carroll County, the North Country, the rural west — those rooms have developed their own post-wall arrangements. Whatever survived the Summer of Chaos, whatever Earth Front presence established itself, whatever informal authority emerged. Concord's claim over them is a legal position without operational meaning.

The wall system didn't intend to fragment state governments. It's a side effect of designing corridors for movement efficiency and integration measurement. The Commission built what they built for their own reasons. The governance consequences at the state level were nobody's problem to solve.

**The legislature:** Whether it achieves quorum is a real question. Representatives from corridor-connected rooms can still make the trip. Representatives from distant rooms may have stopped coming, or may come rarely enough to not be reliable votes on anything. The body that meets in Concord is probably a rump of the rooms the state can actually reach — which means the rooms that are easiest to reach have disproportionate influence over what the state does, which means the legislature is increasingly a Manchester-area institution whether it wants to be or not.

**This reframes Manchester's value to Concord entirely.** Manchester isn't just the largest city — it's one of the few significant rooms the governor can still plausibly claim to govern, and it's a gateway node. I-93 south terminates at Manchester before Route 101 branches east toward Portsmouth and the seacoast. Concord doesn't sit on Route 101 directly. The seacoast connection runs through Manchester. Lose Manchester's cooperation and Concord loses not just the city but its cleanest route to the eastern corridor. What's left is I-93 north and I-89 — Merrimack County and a strip heading toward Vermont. That's a very small state.

Losing Manchester's cooperation doesn't mean political embarrassment. It means the governor's effective territory contracts to something much smaller and harder to defend as a state.

The benign neglect of Manchester isn't just the governor having other priorities. It's a governor who knows — at some level, maybe not consciously — that Manchester is one of the load-bearing walls of whatever NH still is. The arrangement where Manchester stays quietly functional and Concord stays usefully distracted is not just convenient. It may be structural.

**What changes it** is Manchester becoming a problem Concord can no longer ignore. An Earth Front escalation visible enough to demand a state response. A Commission action that embarrasses Concord by association. Something that makes "stable and loyal" no longer a defensible description. When that happens, the governor doesn't just have a political problem. They have a territory problem — because the response options all risk pushing Manchester further from Concord's effective reach, in a state that is already losing rooms faster than it can hold them.

Until then, the I-93 corridor runs between them and neither government is discussing what it means that this is the corridor keeping the state together.

**The fracture within the delegation:** Manchester representatives in the state legislature make the corridor run to Concord for sessions. They have pragmatic Commission relationships while representatives from distant rooms — when those representatives still come — do not. A Manchester state rep who quietly uses their Cayminus account is voting in the same chamber as a rep from the rural north who introduced a bill criminalizing CORP outreach operations. They rode up I-93 together. The Manchester rep is not picking that fight today. The rural rep got here by wagon and it took four days.

### NH State and Earth Front's Political Wing

This is the generative edge — and it has two distinct Earth Front operations that shouldn't be conflated.

**The political wing** is trying to build human governance structures capable of genuine negotiation with the Commission. They want standing, institutional capacity, and the discipline to not blow up working relationships. The state legislature is the surviving human governance structure and they should be natural allies. They may not be. The political wing may view the state as a liability — a megaphone attached to a legitimate claim, incapable of converting it into leverage. The state may view the political wing as upstarts using their legitimacy without their authority. If they work together, the political wing risks being absorbed into grievance politics rather than building something new.

**The Coos County operation** is a different project entirely — and a strategically clever one.

The North Country rooms have effectively slipped from Concord's practical reach. They organized during the Summer of Chaos, built their own arrangements, and Earth Front was present for that founding. These are the rooms where the founding grievance — *we built something ourselves and then the Commission arrived to administer it* — is most viscerally felt, because the Commission has almost no presence there and CORP hasn't reached them. Earth Front didn't displace anything in Coos County. They were there first.

What those rooms still have is nominal NH identity. They haven't formally seceded. They still call themselves New Hampshire. That identity costs them nothing to maintain — Concord can't reach them to collect on it — but it is a political asset Earth Front can use.

**The leverage move:** Earth Front organizes the Coos County rooms as a coalition of NH constituents with Commission grievances and presents them to Concord as a constituency demanding action. The pressure chain: *these are your citizens, they are suffering under Commission infrastructure they never consented to, what are you going to do about it.*

Concord can't easily ignore it. A coalition of NH rooms — even unreachable ones — claiming to speak as NH citizens demanding state action is a constituency claim the governor has to respond to. The options are all bad:
- **Acknowledge and respond:** Means taking a visible position against the Commission that Concord has been carefully avoiding. Breaks the benign neglect.
- **Ignore:** Visibly abandons NH citizens to Earth Front's framing. Confirms that Concord doesn't speak for the rooms it claims.
- **Reassert governance:** Not practically achievable. The corridor friction that made these rooms drift isn't solvable by political will.

The Coos County coalition doesn't have to win the argument. It just has to make Concord respond — and any response routes pressure toward Manchester, which is the only channel Concord has to the Commission.

**The irony for the political wing:** The Coos County operation is weaponizing the shell of existing state authority — the nominal NH identity — as a pressure tool. The political wing is trying to build something new that can function within the Commission framework. These are competing projects using adjacent language. Earth Front claims both. They are not the same thing, and the Commission's aloofness means neither one gets a response from the institution they're ostensibly targeting.

## When Concord Has a Grievance

The benign neglect holds until Concord has a specific complaint — a Gatelock placement affecting a town, wall geometry cutting off a county road, something the governor is getting constituent pressure about. At that point Concord has to decide how to raise it, and there are two channels. Both are uncomfortable.

**Direct pressure on the Commission** runs into the aloofness immediately. The Commission receives the complaint with institutional courtesy. The Kitsune arbitrators — the Commission's human-interface layer — respond. The response is: *if you have a specific boundary concern, the mechanism is a Wall Commission petition filed at the relevant interchange plaza.* Here is the form number. Here is the process. The Commission does not negotiate wall geometry with state governments outside that framework. The framework was not designed for state governments, and nobody is going to say that directly, but the experience of being redirected into a petition process as though you were a private citizen will communicate it.

The governor has an audience. The Commission has a filing system. These do not produce a useful exchange.

**Indirect pressure through Manchester** is more insidious. If Concord contacts Manchester and says *do something about this Gatelock situation* — or even implies it — they are treating Manchester as the responsible party for Commission behavior. The framing is: Manchester has a relationship with the Commission, Manchester should use it. That Manchester has zero authority over the Commission is technically true and politically irrelevant. The expectation lands anyway.

Manchester cannot fulfill it. Manchester also cannot publicly say *we have no control over the Commission* — because that validates Earth Front's argument that Manchester has surrendered sovereignty to an alien institution. So the city absorbs the expectation, relays concerns through whatever informal Commission contacts exist, and takes the credit or blame for outcomes it didn't determine.

**The "pet aliens" problem:** The implicit version of the indirect pressure is that Manchester is accountable for the Commission's presence in a way other NH cities aren't — because Manchester accommodated it, built working relationships with it, made itself legible to it. That accommodation looks like responsibility from the outside. Concord treating Manchester as the Commission's local manager validates the collaborator framing from an unexpected direction. Earth Front says Manchester is a collaborator. The governor's behavior, even without saying it, implies something similar — that Manchester speaks for the Commission or at minimum answers for it.

Manchester is in the position of having built the only functional human-Commission relationship in the state, and being held responsible for everything that relationship touches.

## The Lever That Isn't Being Thought About

The state's only real lever over Manchester is indirect — withhold funding, make Commission cooperation a political liability, force the city to choose visibly. That doesn't touch the Commission. It freezes CORP. The informal MPD relationships go cold. The metric deteriorates. The Commission files it as Normal Human BS and adjusts deployment.

But Concord is not thinking about that lever. Manchester is not on the list of problems that requires a lever. The governor's attention is on the rooms that are actually in crisis.

The lever becomes relevant only if Manchester stops being the city that can be argued stable and loyal. At that point it is not a lever being carefully deployed — it is a governor reacting to a situation that just got complicated, with tools that will hurt Manchester more than they hurt the Commission, in a context where nobody planned for this to be the fight they were having today.

That is a worse scenario than deliberate pressure. Deliberate pressure is calculated. A governor reacting to an unexpected Manchester problem, under pressure from the rest of the state, with a lever they haven't thought carefully about — that's where the damage happens.

## Open Questions

**The governor:**
What does the governor actually want? Removal (not achievable). Formal recognition as humanity's negotiator (no mechanism). Something smaller and achievable — say, a Commission acknowledgment that CORP operates under state law when in NH rooms? That last one is a real ask with real implications: it would give the state regulatory standing over CORP operations, which is exactly the kind of procedural concession Earth Front's political wing would fight over internally.

**Internal state fractures:**
Is the state government unified? Or does Manchester's delegation hold enough seats to block the most hostile legislation, while being outvoted on the symbolic stuff? The symbolic votes are what the press covers. The blocked legislation is what actually matters.

**Earth Front's relationship with Concord:**
Has the political wing made common cause with the state or not? If yes: what did they give up to do it? If no: are they competing for the same legitimacy claim, and what does that competition look like on the ground?

**The CORP problem:**
CORP is Commission staff operating inside city limits, doing work that looks like policing from a distance. The state could try to require CORP personnel to be licensed under NH law, bonded, subject to state oversight. That's not a resolution bill — that's a regulatory move with teeth. Does the Commission comply, contest, or route around it?

**The corridor:**
I-93 runs through Manchester. The corridor is Commission-adjacent infrastructure, technically. Does the state claim jurisdiction over the corridor within NH? The corridor is not a room. It is its own thing. State highway jurisdiction meets Commission corridor architecture at every interchange.

## The Breaking Point — Manchester Stops Absorbing It

The benign neglect arrangement has a load limit.

Manchester has been the Commission's de facto whipping boy for every Gatelock complaint, wall geometry grievance, and constituent pressure Concord couldn't route anywhere else. The city absorbed it because the arrangement was worth maintaining — Concord not looking closely was Manchester's autonomy. Every redirected grievance was the price of being left alone.

At some point the price stops being worth it. The same complaint comes back a third time. Or Concord blames Manchester publicly for a Commission decision Manchester had no part in. Or the mayor is fielding Earth Front accusations of collaboration from one direction and state demands to control the Commission from the other, and both are pointing at the same institution Manchester has no authority over.

**The breaking point is Manchester finally saying it out loud: we don't control the Commission.**

It is a simple statement of fact. It is also the thing neither side has been willing to say because of what it costs both of them:
- **For Manchester:** saying it validates Earth Front's argument — you have no sovereignty, you surrendered it when you let the Commission set up in the Millyard
- **For Concord:** hearing it means the lever doesn't exist. Their constituents' Commission grievances have nowhere to go. The governor has been routing complaints to an address that was never actually connected

Once Manchester says it, the benign neglect is over. Concord has to look directly at Manchester — and when Concord looks directly at Manchester, they see everything that was fine when nobody was watching. The Commission in the Millyard. CORP in the neighborhoods. The informal MPD relationships. Things that could be quietly tolerated become things that require a position.

**What it costs Manchester:** The autonomy that made absorbing the pressure worthwhile disappears the moment they stop absorbing it. They traded the whipping boy role for the freedom to not be examined. Refusing the role ends the trade.

**What the Commission does:** Notes the metric deterioration. Files it as Normal Human BS with downstream effects. Does not intervene. Manchester just blew up its most valuable political arrangement and the Commission is watching the integration score on I-93 segments respond.

**What it does to CORP:** The protagonist's working relationships are built on Manchester city's quiet tolerance — the city not looking directly at what CORP is doing in the neighborhoods. If Manchester and Concord are suddenly in open friction over Commission accountability, CORP is the most visible piece of the Commission's Manchester presence. They become the evidence in an argument neither side wanted to have.

## What This Triangle Produces

The Commission is aloof. The state has a legitimate grievance and no lever that reaches the Commission directly. Manchester city is caught between them, absorbing pressure from both directions without backup from either — until it stops.

The protagonist is the only Commission-adjacent actor actually watching the political drama accumulate. They can see what the expectation is doing to Manchester. They can see what it's going to do to the metric when it breaks. They cannot get the Commission to care, because the Commission has correctly categorized it as Normal Human BS.

The state's grievance doesn't reach the Millyard. It reaches the relationships the protagonist has spent months building. The Commission will note the metric deterioration when it appears. By then the damage is already done on the ground, in rooms, between people.

That gap — between what the Commission measures and what the protagonist watches happening in real time — is where the story lives.
