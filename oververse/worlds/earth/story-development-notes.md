## Genre / Tone Clarification

**Not:** Post-apocalypse dystopia. Not "humans overcome flaws to join the galactic community." Not a story where alien institutions are the villains.

**Is:** A bureaucratic moral thriller set inside an alien administrative mistake. The post-apoc elements (combustion failure, collapsed infrastructure, RV wagons, the Summer) are present but serve a specific function: they make the bureaucracy *necessary and inescapable*. In normal times you just don't cross the Gatelock. In these conditions the Gatelock is the only way to reach the next town.

The horror isn't that the bureaucracy is evil. It's that it's functional and genuine and still wrong. The FC were careless, not malevolent. The Commission is sympathetic damage control. The trial narrative is constructed but not cynical. Nobody is lying. Nobody is even particularly malevolent in the obvious sense.

**The walls are weather.** They shape everything without being the point. The story is not about bringing the walls down — that's a civilization-scale question that swallows any human-sized story placed next to it. The walls will or won't come down well outside the scope of this story.

## The Protagonist's Job — Cayminus Adoption

The protagonist does human outreach for the Commission. The specific friction point: **Cayminus account adoption**.

Humans are already enrolled. They didn't choose it. The Commission and Cayminus gave every human an account with a starting balance — generously, without being asked. The protagonist's job is not to sign people up. It is to get people to **use what they already have**.

The problem: humans refuse. Non-human species won't accept anything else.

---

## The Core Friction — Primal Resistance to Abstract Exchange

This isn't irrational stubbornness. It's ancient.

Physical exchange (paper, metal bits, barter goods) is:
- **Complete in itself** — the thing changed hands, the transaction is done
- **Private** — nobody logged it, nobody measured it
- **Sovereign** — exists between two people, not mediated by an alien system
- **Tangible** — you can hold it, count it, know exactly what you have

A Cayminus account transaction is none of those things. It's a record on a QWAN network, logged as a data point in an integration metric that measures whether you're cooperating correctly with the species that walled off your county.

The starting balance makes it worse, not better. It was given without consent, pre-enrolled. Every use generates data for a system that's evaluating you. The paper in your pocket doesn't do that.

**The Summer of Chaos legitimizes the resistance.** People built barter economies that worked, without help, under genuine crisis. Their distrust of alien systems is earned. CDS is a wrong response to a legitimate grievance — not a wrong response to nothing.

The argument that isn't coercion and isn't condescension probably doesn't exist as a clean pitch. It's closer to: *I know why you don't want to. Here's what it's actually costing you.* And then being honest enough that the human in front of them can decide.

## Thematic Frame — CDS (Author Framing Only)

**Commission Derangement Syndrome.** Author diagnostic lens — this label does not appear in the story. The story shows what CDS does; it never names it. Modeled on TDS but directed at the Commission rather than a political figure.

The Commission has the same structure as a viable derangement target: genuinely present, real authority over daily movement, and whether you think they're the problem or the solution depends almost entirely on what you lost during the Summer versus what you gained since The Fall.

**The derangement isn't in the grievance.** Earth Front isn't wrong that the walls went up without consent, that the trial narrative is constructed, that humans are being measured without being asked. The derangement is in what it does to judgment. The hardline wing encouraging barter refusal is making the walls stronger. The person who won't let their kid cross a Gatelock on principle is punishing their kid.

**The story's question:** What does CDS cost humanity internally? Not whether the Commission is right or wrong. What does it cost the Hendersons on Elm Street, and who pays that bill?

The cost lands on the people with CDS — not on observers. The protagonist watches. That's not a comfortable position.

## Two Flavors of Malevolence

**Alien malevolence within the rules.** Aliens have Cayminus accounts, terminus access, species reputations, institutional accountability. So they work through the system. Extractive contracting that's technically compliant. Information asymmetry exploited in ways a human couldn't name without proving they'd been told something they weren't supposed to know. Plausibly deniable. Whether the Cayminus fee structure incidentally suppresses integration metrics while staying technically sound is internal Cayminus analysis — they're not volunteering it. Is that malevolence or just good business? Probably yes.

**Human malevolence without rules.** The hardline Earth Front knows the Commission can't enforce against them. An armed Commission response collapses every integration metric it touches — the Commission's no-enforcement posture is a deliberate, correct policy choice. So the protection vacuum exists and Earth Front fills it. They can threaten, intimidate, burn a merchant's cart — and the Commission's options are limited to hoping the metric reflects it and asking the Bruin to handle it in county remainder.

The protagonist stands between: alien malevolence that works inside the rules so cleanly the rules can't catch it, and human malevolence that works outside the rules because the Commission can't enforce against its own natives without becoming the thing it's accused of being.

## Cayminus Calibration

**Not evil bankers.** Warm, generous, serve Nummus, love the game. The truism is *"Sound books, steady hands, and a great deal of patience."*

Practical in ways that *can come across as evil* — but the distinction matters. They designed the fee structure to balance the books. The suppressive effect on integration metrics is a Commission KPI problem, not a banking problem. A Cayminus enrollment officer looking at your numbers would say: *the fees are disclosed, the terms are on page forty-seven, the account structure is sound.* Genuinely helpful. Probably finds humans fascinating — novel species, unprecedented conditions, genuinely interesting financial chaos.

They're not going to restructure their fee model because it's hard for humans right now. That's not their problem. The protagonist's Cayminus counterpart is probably warm, charming, genuinely invested in the protagonist succeeding — and operating within an institutional structure the protagonist can't move and the counterpart doesn't see as a problem.

## Earth Front — Fractured Beyond the Claim

Earth Front claims bipartisan unity. "Bipartisan" is already a tell — the language of a coalition that needs to claim unity it doesn't have.

More fractured than a jigsaw puzzle. Different factions have different relationships to the Cayminus accounts:
- **Hardline wing:** Refusal as litmus test. Collaborator accusation is personal and directed.
- **Pragmatic wing:** May quietly use their accounts while publicly denouncing the system.
- **Political wing:** Trying to build human-controlled banking alternatives — not refusing Cayminus so much as building something to replace it.
- **Fringe:** Has theories about what the starting balance is actually tracking.

The fractures matter as much as the unified face. CDS isn't just directed outward at the Commission — it's eating the coalition from inside. Factions that agree on the grievance and agree on nothing else, held together by a shared enemy, falling apart the moment they have to agree on what to actually do.

The collaborator accusation doesn't come from one coherent voice. It comes from six directions simultaneously, each with a slightly different definition of what collaboration means.

**The fault line that matters:** The one that cracks Earth Front visibly during the story — TBD.

## Guardrails Against Condescension

The setup has all the ingredients for an "enlightened outsider watches backwards people hurt themselves" story. That has to be actively resisted.

1. **The Summer legitimizes the resistance.** The people refusing aren't ignorant. They built something that worked.
2. **Earth Front's best version must be genuinely compelling.** The hardline wing needs someone inside it who is smart, principled, and making a coherent argument — even if self-defeating. No strawmen.
3. **The protagonist can't be obviously right.** Their advantage is access to information others don't have, not superior judgment. They know the Commission narrative has seams. The people refusing would have a point if they knew what the protagonist knows.
4. **The cost lands on the people with CDS.** The protagonist watches. That's the position.
