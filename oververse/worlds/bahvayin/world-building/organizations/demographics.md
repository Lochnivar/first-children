# Demographics & Settlement — Bahvayin

**Category:** Organizations & Institutions  
**Related:** [Political Hierarchy](political-hierarchy.md), [The Empire](empire.md), [World Overview](../geography/overview.md)

## Population Overview

**Total population:** Approximately 8 to 12 million across the entire atoll.

Bahvayin is medieval sparse. The Death of Magic was a civilizational reset — magical infrastructure that supported food production, water management, construction, and logistics collapsed over a generation. The carrying capacity of the land dropped sharply before mortal-technology replacements could fill the gap. Generations of rebuilding have not fully recovered the mageocracy's population levels.

Additional pressure: Orcus leakage from sealed dungeons, outer ocean dangers limiting maritime expansion, and the ordinary medieval reality that disease and famine do most of the work that monsters don't.

The result: a world that feels genuinely underpopulated relative to its geography. Emptied land is common. Reclaimed forest covers former agricultural territory. Old mageocracy roads run straight through areas nobody officially governs.

## Frontier Homesteads

Single or double family settlements, maximum 15 people, typically on the frontier edge of settled territory. Palisaded — a defensive perimeter is not optional when you are beyond reliable help.

**What they are:** Families who pushed out beyond the last village and found a defensible rise, a reliable water source, or a piece of land the Death of Magic left viable but the empire hasn't resettled. Not on any map. By the time a cartographer would bother, a homestead has either grown into a hamlet or been abandoned.

**Their relationship with ruins:** Homesteaders and mageocracy ruins coexist in ways that settled villages don't. A homestead built inside the outer wall of a ruined structure gets free stonework. The family that has been salvaging dressed stone from the tower on the ridge for two generations knows that ruin better than any scholar. They also know when something inside it changes.

**Their relationship with the political structure:** Informal. The nearest Baron may not know they exist until they appear asking for help or stop paying the unofficial tithe that constitutes their relationship with authority. They are a feature of the landscape — like the ruins, like the reclaimed forest, like the old mageocracy road that runs straight through territory nobody officially governs anymore.

**Story register:** Frontier homesteads are where the world's danger is most immediate and most personal. A leaking dungeon two miles away is not an abstract political problem. It is the reason the palisade got an extra course of logs last autumn.

## What Medieval Sparse Feels Like

A character traveling between kingdom capitals crosses stretches where there is nothing for half a day's ride. Forests that were cleared during the mageocracy have partially reclaimed agricultural land. Some towns marked on old maps no longer exist — the Death of Magic took the infrastructure that made them viable and they emptied over two generations.

The ruins are everywhere partly because the mageocracy built extensively and partly because there aren't enough people to tear them down and reuse the stone. A sealed dungeon on the edge of a Baron's territory isn't a distant abstraction — it's a named feature of the landscape his grandchildren grow up knowing to avoid.

The world is not post-apocalyptic. It is *recovering.* The difference matters. People are not surviving — they are building. Slowly, carefully, with long memories of what happened when the wrong kind of power organized everything.
