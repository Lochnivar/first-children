# The Succession Claimants

**Category:** Organizations & Institutions — The Succession Crisis  
**Related:** [Emperor Vaelor IX](emperor-vaelor.md), [Political Hierarchy](political-hierarchy.md), [Client Kingdoms](client-kingdoms.md), [Empire Decline](../history-events/empire-decline.md)

## Overview

Five main contenders for the Sheltering Hand. Each has a legal claim Dinath's scales have not dismissed. Each is wrong for the throne in a different way. If any of them were obviously right, the scales would have tipped already — that they haven't is the theological and political crisis simultaneously.

They have been working the five client kingdoms, the Grand Duchies, and the imperial court for two years. None has broken through. None has been eliminated. The balancing act Vaelor maintains with such serene skill keeps all of them in hopeful anxiety and none of them in power.

Running alongside the five main contenders — not a claimant, not a faction, but a sentiment — is the sixth force that none of them know how to address.

---

## Why Vaelor Cannot Simply Name a Successor

The succession law requires ratification by the five client kings — a concession extracted during a moment of imperial weakness, probably two or three reigns back, when a king needed to be bought and the price was constitutional. The emperor proposes. Three of five kings confirm. The named heir has no legal standing until the threshold is met.

In stable times this is a formality — the kings confirm because the empire's legitimacy benefits them and blocking succession costs more than accepting it. In the current crisis it is an absolute structural trap:

- Vaelor can name a successor. He may have done so in private correspondence. It doesn't matter.
- Three of five kings ratifying requires a coalition. No coalition currently exists.
- Two kings declaring for a claimant and three withholding produces a named heir with no legal confirmation — worse than the current ambiguity because it forces everyone to take a position on a claim that hasn't cleared the threshold.
- The minimum winning coalition is exactly three. A fourth ratification is redundant. A second is insufficient. Every claimant's campaign is a race to three kings.

**San Tavar's position:** If the Queen is the third ratification for any claimant, she is the deciding vote. Every claimant has understood this for two years. She knows every claimant knows this. The race to three is, in practice, a race to San Tavar — because the first two kings a claimant secures are probably achievable through direct leverage, and the third requires the Queen's read of which wave is worth riding.

**Kol Idic's position:** The original ratification agreement is in the mageocracy-era documentation Kol Idic has been quietly acquiring. The precedents for how the threshold has been met historically — under what circumstances kings have withheld, what "ratification" legally requires, whether a king under duress can ratify validly — are in Zafaran's Archives. The coffeehouse archivists have opinions. The credentialing process takes four months.

**The dissolution provision:** The succession law contains a separate mechanism specifically for a childless emperor with no confirmed heir — the exact failure mode the framers understood they needed to address. Unanimous vote of all five kings formally dissolves the empire. Each kingdom returns to full independence, constitutionally clean, no treason involved.

**The treason clause:** A dissolution vote that fails — that does not achieve unanimity — treats every king who voted in favor as a traitor to the empire. The defecting king or kings who voted against dissolution have backed a claimant by default, and the winning claimant inherits the treason charges against the others as a prize. Failed dissolution doesn't just mean the empire continues. It means the kings who voted for it lose everything.

**The geometry this creates:**

- All five vote dissolution: empire ends, all five independent, constitutional and clean
- Four vote dissolution, one defects: four kings are traitors, defector is kingmaker with four kingdoms as the prize
- Three vote dissolution, two defect: three traitors, two kingmakers splitting the reward and the resulting tension
- Any split: the defectors win, the dissolution voters lose catastrophically

**Why this makes coordination nearly impossible:** Kings need absolute certainty of each other before any of them moves. One defector doesn't just win the succession — they win four kingdoms. The downside of being wrong isn't missing the kingmaker prize. It's treason.

**San Tavar's position sharpens further:** The Queen knows enough about all five kings' calculations to assess whether unanimity is achievable. That assessment is worth more than any claimant's title confirmation offer. She could sell it to a claimant — identify which king is the weakest link in the dissolution coalition, tell the claimant where to apply pressure, and collect the kingmaker prize without ever formally ratifying. She could broker the coordination the dissolution requires. She could do both — broker, confirm unanimity, then defect at the last moment. Nobody outside her private archive knows which option she is running. Possibly she hasn't decided.

## The Five Main Contenders

### Claimant One — The Legitimate Cousin
**Claim basis:** Nearest surviving bloodline to Vaelor. Strongest paper trail. The claim Korrin's legal research keeps returning to when he works through the archive documentation.  
**Character:** Competent administrator. No vision. Would run the empire the way Vaelor runs it minus the theology — pure bureaucratic continuity. Understands process, follows procedure, produces accurate reports. The empire would function under this claimant the way Amud's transit economy functions: reliably, without drama, without inspiration.  
**The problem:** Nobody is excited. The claim is legally strongest and personally least compelling. The five kings are not rushing to back the candidate who represents more of the same, only without the theological framing that at least gave Vaelor a coherent internal logic. The Grand Dukes who need stability might eventually settle here by default if the alternatives deteriorate. The Legitimate Cousin is aware of this and is quietly waiting for the alternatives to deteriorate.  
**Vaelor's handling:** Favored slightly in symbolic gestures, which the Legitimate Cousin reads as encouragement and everyone else reads as Vaelor doing the minimum required by the bloodline proximity. The storm-sapphire clasp has never hummed in this claimant's presence. Nobody mentions this.  
**Species:** Human. Old bloodline, appropriately thin.

### Claimant Two — The General
**Claim basis:** Distant intermarriage connection several generations back. Legally arguable — not dismissible, not strong. The kind of claim that wins a civil war and cannot win a legal argument.  
**Character:** Military record that commands genuine respect. Popular support in the fleet remnants, among the island lords who want stability enforced rather than negotiated, and in the frontier communities that have been protecting themselves for years and would like an emperor who understands what that costs. Direct, decisive, impatient with the succession crisis in ways that read as strength to some and recklessness to others.  
**The problem:** Knows the legal claim is thin. Has been compensating by building relationships with Grand Dukes who need protection more than legitimacy — offering military guarantee of their titles in exchange for declarations of support. The network is growing. It is also the most visible of the five claimant operations, which means it is the one the other four are most actively working to counter.  
**Vaelor's handling:** Receives the General with warm ceremonial courtesy and redirects budget allocations to the dockyards that supply the General's allied fleet immediately afterward. The General has noticed. Has not found a way to address it that doesn't involve threatening the emperor, which would collapse all remaining legal legitimacy.  
**Species:** TBD — the General's background from a specific military culture is held for development.

### Claimant Three — The Architects
**Claim basis:** Strong claim, well-documented, several intermarriage connections across two generations that stack into a cumulative argument. Not the single strongest claim but the most redundantly supported — if one line fails legally, another holds. One of those lines runs through a cross-species political marriage two generations back — legally valid, socially awkward, the detail the other four quietly point to when they want to cast doubt without making a direct accusation. The Architects consider it their most defensible line. The other claimants consider it the most revealing thing about the house.  
**Character:** The house that arranged the boating accident. Unproven. Unprovable. Vaelor suspects. Will never act. The Architects have calculated this correctly and find it useful. Their campaign is the most methodical of the five — five years of sustained Grand Duchy relationship-building, careful placement of house members in administrative positions adjacent to imperial institutions, patient accumulation of leverage that doesn't announce itself. They do not seek declarations; they accumulate debts.  
**The problem:** They are good at this. Being good at this in a way that is visible to the other four claimants and the five kings has created a specific kind of opposition — not "we prefer someone else" but "we cannot allow these particular people." San Tavar's Queen has made no statement. Her information networks have been particularly active in documenting the Architects' operations for two years. Nobody has asked what she plans to do with the documentation.  
**Vaelor's handling:** Held with perfect precision — neither favored nor punished, exactly centered. Every interaction is correct, appropriate, and impossible to read. The Architects find this unnerving in a way they do not admit. They had planned for Vaelor's passive complicity. They had not planned for Vaelor's passive precision.  
**Species:** Human primary line, cross-species cadet branch in the claim documentation.

### Claimant Four — The General's Mirror
**Claim basis:** Mid-tier legally. Supported by a significant faction of the imperial clergy whose backing provides theological legitimacy that paper trails cannot.  
**Character:** Genuinely devout, theologically sophisticated — not performance, genuine conviction. Has spent years developing a theological argument that the succession crisis requires *correct* theological response rather than passive theological observation. Vaelor's piety is framed not as virtue but as abdication: the Sheltering Hand shelters by acting, not by waiting. This argument is the most dangerous challenge to Vaelor's internal logic that any claimant has produced.  
**The engineer-priests are watching this one carefully.** A theologically-driven emperor who accepts the argument that correct ritual requires correct structural preparation — that Tsapadon's Eye cannot find the offering worthy if the offering has been deliberately compromised — is an emperor who would listen to the Corvani analyst's findings. The Senior Council has not made contact. It is thinking about it.  
**Clergy backing:** The specific imperial clergy faction and its interests are TBD pending church hierarchy development. This claimant's full shape depends on that foundation. What is established: the backing is real, significant, and comes from a faction that wants something in return that has not yet been specified.  
**Vaelor's handling:** Receives this claimant with particular warmth — genuine warmth, the warmth of a man who recognizes sincere faith and is reassured by it. Has granted several ceremonial religious honors. Has also ensured that every honor was precisely equivalent to one granted to each other claimant, theologically neutralizing the signal. The claimant finds Vaelor's appreciation for sincere faith genuinely moving and his political precision genuinely maddening.  
**Species:** TBD pending broader development.

### Claimant Five — The Outsider
**Claim basis:** Weakest formal claim of the five. A rim culture marriage connection two generations back, legally present, not legally strong. The kind of claim that requires everything else to go right.  
**Character:** Comes from a rim culture rather than the inner Akhan court. Understands the actual empire — the frontier, the trade routes, the people who aren't nobles — better than any of the others. Has spent time in Tzafan, in the northern rim settlements, in the working ports. Knows what the succession crisis looks like from the bottom of the levy chain. Speaks to the populations the other four address through intermediaries.  
**The problem:** The imperial court finds this claimant alarming in the specific way that competence at the wrong register is alarming. The Outsider is not playing the succession game correctly. Not from ignorance — from a considered decision that the succession game as currently played produces Vaelors. This reads as either the most dangerous claimant or the most naive depending on who is reading it.  
**The five kings:** The Outsider is the claimant they find most interesting. Not most comfortable — most interesting. Tzafan's King has had three private conversations. A'Kavah's King has had two. San Tavar's Queen has had one meeting that lasted four hours and produced a detailed intelligence report filed in her private archive. None of the three has declared.  
**None of the Above dimension:** The Outsider's campaign is the one most closely adjacent to the None of the Above sentiment — close enough that some of the energy feeding that sentiment is potentially convertible, and close enough that the Outsider risks being consumed by it rather than riding it.  
**Vaelor's handling:** Genuine uncertainty. This is the one claimant whose internal logic Vaelor cannot fully model. The others are comprehensible within the framework of court politics. The Outsider is operating from premises that Vaelor's framework does not easily accommodate. He has been courteous and has offered no symbolic favors whatsoever — not from hostility, but from genuine uncertainty about what signal any favor would send. The Outsider has noticed the absence and drawn their own conclusions.  
**Species:** TBD — rim culture origin is held for development.

## The Sixth Force — None of the Above

Not a claimant. Not a faction. A sentiment with two distinct populations that are easily confused and politically very different.

### The Disappointed Majority

The dominant version. Most people running None of the Above aren't nihilists — they're disappointed. They would accept a good emperor. They haven't seen one. The five main contenders haven't given them a reason to believe any of them would be meaningfully different from Vaelor.

This version is passive, expressed as cynicism rather than action. The dock worker in Amud muttering about imperial levies. The frontier thane filing minimum required paperwork and otherwise governing his own territory. The Hobgoblin clerk processing succession correspondence with perfect procedure and zero investment in the outcome. It runs strongest among:
- Working populations of Grimport, Kerath, Baram — the succession crisis as levy spikes and broken fuel contracts, not dynastic politics
- Junior functionaries throughout the imperial bureaucracy
- Communities that have been managing their own affairs for long enough that imperial administration feels theoretical

This population is convertible. The right candidate with the right message could reach them. The Outsider is the only claimant who understands this.

### The Dissolution Minority

Smaller, cooler than it sounds, and concentrated in specific places. This version isn't "let it burn" — it's "let it stop." The empire dissolves with Vaelor's death. The kings return to full independence. The Grand Duchies revert to whatever their local legitimacy supports. The engineer-priests continue maintaining the breakwaters because they were doing that before the empire existed. The Djemari keep running the Desert Road. Grimport keeps fishing. The world continues — without the imperial superstructure extracting levies, redirecting budgets, and keeping everyone in hopeful anxiety.

Not chaos. Not destruction. Procedural dissolution. Each obligation running to the imperial office, and the imperial office becoming vacant.

This version runs among:
- Northern rim communities genuinely self-sufficient for a generation, now asking why they pay imperial taxes for services the empire no longer provides
- People with specific traceable grievances — a breakwater failure, a redirected maintenance budget, a levy that arrived before storm season
- Island lords who have been running their own affairs long enough to have stopped noticing the imperial framework they're technically inside

The grievances are personal, not ideological. That makes this population harder to argue with than a revolutionary faction.

### The Kings' Prisoner's Dilemma

The dissolution provision exists. Every king knows it exists. The constitutional path to independence is real, clean, and available — if all five can coordinate.

The treason clause makes coordination existentially dangerous. Each king is running the same calculation simultaneously:

- *If all five vote dissolution, we all win.*
- *If I vote dissolution and one other defects, I am a traitor and the defector wins my kingdom.*
- *If I defect first and back a claimant, I win big — kingmaker terms plus four kingdoms if the others vote dissolution.*
- *If I defect second, I share the kingmaker prize. If I defect third or later, I get whatever the winner decides to offer people who were slow.*

The five kings don't trust each other enough to coordinate and they know it. The conversation that would need to happen — five kings explicitly agreeing to invoke the dissolution provision — is itself documentable treason if any one of them defects afterward. San Tavar's intelligence networks would have it documented before it finished happening. Kol Idic would have documentation it currently lacks. Any king who proposes the dissolution conversation is handing a weapon to every other king simultaneously.

**San Tavar's position:** The Queen knows enough about all five kings' calculations to assess whether unanimity is achievable. That assessment is the most valuable piece of information in the empire right now. She could sell it to a claimant — identify the weakest link in any potential dissolution coalition, collect kingmaker terms without ever formally invoking the provision. She could broker the coordination the dissolution requires. She could broker it and then defect at the last moment, leaving four kings facing treason charges while she collects maximum terms from the winning claimant. Nobody outside her private archive knows which option she is running. The four-hour meeting with the Outsider was partly about this. What conclusion she reached is not known.

**Tzafan's position:** The King is not running a dissolution calculation. He is running a pragmatic imperial alignment — the succession crisis is inner Akhan politics, Tzafan doesn't care who wins, the transshipment routes need to stay open. He will ratify whoever presents a credible northern policy. This makes him Vaelor's most reliable vote and dissolution's most reliable obstacle. Unanimity requires Tzafan. Tzafan won't vote dissolution while the current King reigns.

Every player running the dissolution calculation knows this. The King knows they know. He is professionally unsentimental about frontier threats and has begun asking privately whether the northern consolidation his scouts have been reporting for two years is a genuine emergence — or a manufactured distraction designed to keep his attention pointed north while something is arranged closer to home. He has not shared this question with anyone. He has increased his personal security.

**Vaelor and the dissolution provision:**
Vaelor is aware the provision exists. He helped draft the legal commentary on it thirty years ago, before the succession crisis was imaginable. He has filed the current situation under "the cosmic bureaucracy's testing of the imperial framework." He has not updated his model. Korrin has noted this with a date.

## Collective Dynamics

The five claimants are not cooperating. They are not fully in conflict either — open conflict would force declarations from the five kings and the Grand Duchies, and none of the five is certain enough of the outcome to want that. The current equilibrium is uncomfortable for everyone and preferable to the alternative.

The Architects are the most patient. The General is the most exposed. The Legitimate Cousin is waiting for attrition. The True Believer is building a theological argument that may matter enormously at the right moment. The Outsider is the variable none of the others have fully modeled.

Vaelor's serene precision keeps them all suspended. When Vaelor dies — and the calculations are running — the equilibrium ends.
