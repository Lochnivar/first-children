# Bahvayin — Geographic Overview

**Category:** Geography  
**Related:** [Storm Gate](storm-gate.md), [Desert Road](desert-road.md), [Southern Way](southern-way.md)

## The Atoll

Bahvayin is an atoll — the inner Akhan Sea approximately 1.75 times the size of the Mediterranean, the atoll rim extending further beyond. Large enough that the Storm Gate and the Southern Way are weeks apart by sail, small enough that imperial communication, trade networks, and naval politics are coherent within a single generation's political memory.

A continuous rim of elevated land encircles the Akhan Sea. From space it is unmistakably an eye: the rim as iris, the inner sea as pupil, the three straits as imperfections in the iris. The mortals have no frame of reference from which to observe this. They live inside the pupil and call it the world.

The three straits — Storm Gate (east), Desert Road (west-northwest), Southern Way (south) — divide the atoll rim into three distinct landmass arcs, each with its own biome character, cultural identity, and name. Boundaries between arcs bleed naturally; the transitions are gradual, not hard lines, and the overlap zones are often the most interesting geography.

## The Three Arcs

### Grenmarch — The Northeast Arc
*Between the Storm Gate and the Desert Road, going over the top*

The furthest north the atoll extends — wide enough that its northern reaches achieve genuine taiga and approach tundra at the extreme rim. Four distinct zones, each with its own name and character. The frontier character runs through all of them: never fully administered, terrain that made conquest not worth the cost. Communities here have been governing themselves for long enough that imperial paperwork feels theoretical.

**Tal Aegis**, the imperial capital, occupies the southern headland of Grenmarch where it meets the Storm Gate — tucked into the lee side of the northern mainland arm, protected from direct surge exposure by the headland itself while remaining undeniably at the Storm Gate. The Sheltering Hand shelters by standing in the path.

The southern edge of Grenmarch bleeds into Dashamar — forest thinning to scattered woodland, then open steppe. No clear line. Communities in the transition zone have complicated answers to which arc they belong to.

#### Kyremark — The Inner Coast
*Akhan-facing, deeply indented, harbor-rich*

The commercial face of Grenmarch. The inner coast is Greek in morphology — or more precisely Dalmatian: deeply indented rias, peninsulas reaching into the Akhan, natural harbors behind every headland, clusters of small islands just offshore breaking any swell that builds. Water penetrates far inland. Every inlet shelters something. The empire did not build this harbor infrastructure — it arrived and found it already excellent.

Kyremark is where Grenmarch's settlements concentrate, where the imperial administrative presence actually operates, where the trade flows in and the timber and furs flow out. The towns here are small but dense, each bay with its own fishing culture, each inlet with its own dialect variation. The coastline's complexity has kept them distinct for longer than any political boundary has.

The Aldwood does not stop at the coast. On the rocky headlands between bays the old forest grows to the cliff edge — roots in the rock face, canopy ending where the drop begins. From the water, Kyremark reads as alternating dark forested headland and bay-gap: you navigate by the break in the tree-cliff. The settlements are in the bays; the forest owns the rock between them. Overland travel from one inlet to the next cuts through Aldwood headland terrain the forest has not made welcoming. These communities are maritime-connected and land-isolated simultaneously — a fact that has shaped their politics for as long as they have had politics.

Tracks do run north through the Aldwood — connecting Kyremark baronies and counties to the Pachi Trail and the Kaldvast settlements beyond. Not roads in any imperial sense: deep Siberian in character, narrow threads beaten into the forest floor by necessity, the old growth pressing in on both sides, the route a thing you know or you don't. They follow waterways and ridgelines where the terrain concedes passage. Summer makes them difficult — mud, soft ground, root tangle, insects. Winter makes them viable: the ground freezes, sleds run where carts cannot, and the Aldwood opens slightly to anyone who knows the track. A Kyremark settlement may be better connected to the Pachi Trail in January by sled than in July by cart. The communities that run those winter routes hold knowledge that doesn't transfer to outsiders and have never been asked to share it.

Where a track meets the Pachi Trail a waystation accumulates — not a town, a clearing with a structure or two, a place to swap goods and news between the coastal circuit and the full atoll circuit. The Pachi know every junction. Each coastal settlement knows its own track and nothing beyond it.

Tzafan sits at Kyremark's northwestern extreme — the last significant harbor before the coast transitions to something wilder on the approach to the Desert Road strait.

#### Icemark — The Outer Coast
*Ocean-facing, glacially carved, exposed*

The hard face of Grenmarch toward the outer ocean. Where the Kyremark is sheltered complexity, the Icemark is exposed drama: glaciers descend from the northern heights to the sea, carving the fjords as they go. The ice explains the morphology — the same indented coastline as Kyremark, but carved by ice rather than water, deeper, steeper-sided, colder. In places the glacier still reaches the shore. In places it recently retreated and the raw rock hasn't yet decided what to do with itself.

Settlement is sparse and purposeful. The Icemark is not a place people live because it is comfortable — it is a place people live because the outer ocean produces things the rest of the atoll will pay for.

**The whaling industry:** The primary reason anyone is here. The outer ocean's cold, nutrient-rich waters support whale populations that the inner Akhan cannot match. Icemark villages are whaling stations first and communities second — built around the deep fjords where rendering operations can run without the smell destroying the settlement, positioned for access to the offshore grounds, sized for the crew rotations the industry requires. The fleet goes out in the open seasons; the village processes what comes back.

What the whaling produces: whale oil for lighting and lubrication across the empire's cities; ambergris that travels south by ice road and eventually reaches Florentia's perfumer houses as one of the rarest fixative ingredients available; rendered blubber for soap and preservative; whalebone for structural and tool applications. The oil is bulk trade. The ambergris is luxury trade. Both leave the Icemark in winter.

**Ice roads and seasonal connection:** The outer ocean in storm season is a genuine threat — the exposed fjord mouths take weather that the inner Kyremark harbors never see. The whaling fleet's calendar is built around the open window between the worst storm seasons, and even that window requires reading the outer ocean's moods correctly. But winter consolidates the overland terrain between the Icemark and the Kaldvast interior. Ice roads form across ground that is marsh and soft earth in summer — the same seasonal inversion that opens the Kaldvast also opens the Icemark's back door. Supply convoys move south in winter by sled, reach the Pachi Trail, and connect to the inner Akhan trade network. The Icemark's most valuable exports travel in the coldest months by the most reliable route available.

The Icemark's people face the outer ocean and the outer ocean has opinions about them. The relationship is old and thoroughly negotiated.

#### Aldwood — South of the Pachi Trail
*Primordial temperate forest, deep and old*

The forest that was here before anything else and intends to remain. Broadleaf and mixed, ancient in the way that means the largest trees predate the empire by centuries and the forest itself predates recorded history. The Aldwood does not feel managed. It is not managed. The timber operations that feed Grenmarch's coastal trade take from the edges; the interior has never been cleared and gives no indication of tolerating the attempt.

The Pachi Trail runs the treeline at the Aldwood's northern edge — the Pachi wouldn't cut a tree when the forest hands them a border to follow. South of the trail the canopy closes and the light changes. North of the trail the sky opens. The boundary is exact and was not drawn by anyone.

The Aldwood is where the predecessor civilization's ruins concentrate in Grenmarch. The forest grew back over what they left. Some of what they left is still there, under the roots, occasionally not as inert as it appears.

#### Kaldvast — North of the Pachi Trail
*Taiga to tundra-fringe, wide and cold*

North of the treeline the forest thins to boreal — spruce, birch, fir, widely spaced, the sky visible through the canopy in ways the Aldwood never allows. Further north the trees thin further: shorter, more wind-shaped, further apart, until the northernmost reaches of the Kaldvast are something that resists being called forest at all. Not quite tundra. Something that a tundra traveler would recognize and a forest traveler would not.

The Kaldvast is wide — Grenmarch extends far enough north that the taiga zone has genuine depth before reaching the rim. This is where the northern consolidation that Tzafan's scouts have been reporting is taking shape. The Kaldvast is large enough to hide significant organized activity from anyone not already in it. Tzafan's scouts go in. Some come back unsettled. Some don't come back.

The Pachi Trail runs year-round along the Kaldvast's southern edge — it is the reliable line, the one that functions in all seasons. But routes between Kaldvast settlements, and between those settlements and the deeper interior, behave differently. Summer in the taiga is soft ground, standing water, and insects in quantities that make open travel miserable. Winter freezes everything and gives it structure: frozen ground, river ice that bears weight, the forest floor firm underfoot. Dog sleds and winter runners reach places that summer pack animals cannot. The communities that live significantly north of the Trail plan their supply runs and heavy transport for the cold season. A Kaldvast settlement's connection to the Trail, and through it to the rest of Grenmarch, is actually strongest in the months that would strand a southern traveler.

The northern rim beyond the Kaldvast is genuinely wild. The Kaldvast is the last zone that can be called administered, in the loose sense Grenmarch applies to that word.

### Dashamar — The South Arc
*Between the Storm Gate and the Southern Way*

Southern steppe — warm, open, rolling grassland. Not the cold brutal expanse of the iconic northern steppe but its warmer cousin: vast, pastoral, the kind of landscape that rewards patience and mobility. The Southern Way's warm current moderates the climate from below, pushing warmth and moisture northward across the arc.

Ranching culture dominates. The steppe supports large herds and the people who move with them. Not wilderness — worked land, well-understood land, land whose rhythms take a generation to learn and reward those who learn them.

Stormbreak sits at the northeastern corner where Dashamar meets the Storm Gate mouth. The **divine office temples** occupy the storm coast along Dashamar's eastern outer face — the gods conduct business from the front line of what they are supposed to be managing. The temple complex is older than the empire and possibly the original reason people settled this coastline.

The western edge of Dashamar climbs into the lower Andalvar foothills before the Archavar asserts itself. Mare Shafel occupies this transition zone at the southern end of Harova — the warm steppe and the sheltered highland valleys meeting to produce exceptional horse country.

### Harova — The West Arc
*Between the Southern Way and the Desert Road*

The most geographically complex of the three arcs. The Archavar mountain range runs as a spine through the interior, creating three distinct zones stacked west to east, each with its own character and name. The Desert Road strait marks the northern boundary; the Southern Way marks the southern.

**The Harovian Desert** — the outer western face. Hyperarid, the weather factory of Bahvayin: thermal dynamics over the desert floor seed the storm systems that cross the outer ocean and become the eastern rim's nightmare. The desert generates what the Storm Gate endures. The Djemari move through it along the Desert Road corridor, their water knowledge a map of where the Archavar's karst forces aquifer water to the surface.

**Andalvar** — the alpine interior. High passes, sheltered valleys, mountain culture that lives between two radically different worlds. The highlands where the Count of Kern's vineyards sit — Kernish Red grown in sheltered valley microclimates that exist nowhere else on Bahvayin. Andalvar communities look both ways: west into the desert they are not, east toward the inner Akhan they can almost see from the passes.

**Mazarvan** — the steep eastern descent to the Akhan shore. The Alborz/Caspian in character: dramatic altitude drop from alpine heights to warm coastal humidity, dense forest on the slopes, terraced agriculture clinging to the gradient, fishing villages at the base. A completely different world from Andalvar despite sharing the same mountain spine — the eastern face receives the Akhan's moisture where the western face receives nothing.

## The Archavar

The mountain range that forms Harova's spine. In mortal theology the Archavar is the **home** of the gods — not their offices, not where mortals petition them, but where they reside. Each major peak or massif is associated with a specific deity, named as their private seat.

**Mount Tsapadon** is the highest peak — storm-exposed, visible from the Mazarvan slopes, visible from the inner Akhan on a clear day. The engineer-priests had a station there. The budget for maintaining it has been redirected.

The distinction between divine home (Archavar) and divine office (storm coast temples) is theologically load-bearing. You do not go to the Archavar to petition a god. You go to stand near something that was not built for you. The journey is its own statement.

### Archavar Karst Hydrology

The mountain peaks and high plateaus develop natural solution basins in the karst limestone — broad shallow depressions where winter rain and snowmelt collect and percolate down rather than running off. These feed a mid-mountain saturated zone moving laterally through the rock, emerging as reliable springs on the Andalvar eastern face.

On the western descent the aquifer moves deeper until it reaches the mountain base — where the geology transitions from porous karst to compressed impermeable bedrock. This forces all remaining water laterally to the surface at the mountain base contact zone. The Desert Road corridor runs along this precise ribbon — the zone where forced aquifer emergence makes water available in otherwise lethal terrain.

The Djemari's water knowledge is essentially a map of where the karst forces water to the surface, built from generations of observation that cannot be quickly replicated by outsiders.

## The Yeorvan

The great river of Harova. Sources in the Archavar peaks — snowmelt and high altitude precipitation — cuts west through the Harovian Desert, and fans into a broad delta in the northern desert where the terrain flattens and the sand cannot maintain defined channels. The delta is the most fertile land in the entire West arc, its agricultural productivity thrown into dramatic relief by the surrounding arid landscape.

*The Yeorvan* — not "the Yeorvan River." The name already contains the word for river in First Speech. The redundancy marks an outsider.

The Yeorvan delta sits in the northern Harovian Desert approaching the Desert Road strait. Zafaran's agricultural base depends on delta productivity more than imperial records officially acknowledge. The Djemari have named the river and its moods in their own language; the imperial name is the worn-down version of something older.

The desert cannot maintain the river's channels as they spread — sand absorbs and disperses, the river loses definition, each distributary deposits silt and builds the alluvial fan wider. This is not a problem. It is how the delta works.

**Cotton production:** The delta's combination of river irrigation, desert sun, and warm temperatures produces the empire's primary cotton crop. The same logic as the great river deltas of the real world — maximum sun exposure, reliable water, well-drained alluvial soil. Yeorvan cotton is the empire's working textile, supplemented at the luxury end by Mazarvan silk. Florentia's dye barrels roll west down the Archavar slopes to reach the delta's cotton finishing operations — the same barrels that roll east to the Mazarvan's silk houses.

## The Pachi Trail

The only road through the interior of the atoll mainland. Not a road in any imperial sense — no construction authority, no maintenance budget, no surveying record. A trail worn into the landscape by generations of Pachi caravans making the full circuit, wide enough in places to pass a loaded wagon and nothing more. The Appalachian Trail's character applied to a continental-scale circumnavigation.

The trail circuits all three arcs through their interiors — not hugging the inner Akhan coast or the outer ocean rim but running through the heartland of each arc, deliberately wending through as many biomes as the terrain allows. The Pachi are not transiting. They are experiencing. The most direct route is actively uninteresting to a Pachi caravan.

**Grenmarch section:** Runs the treeline — the natural boundary where the primordial temperate forest to the south gives way to taiga and open terrain to the north. The Pachi did not cut this path; they found it. A biologically nomadic species that wouldn't fell a tree when the landscape hands them a road for free reads the treeline edge and runs the wagons along it. Forest to the south, open taiga to the north, the seam between two worlds underfoot. The treeline IS the trail in Grenmarch. This is why the treeline marks the cultural boundary between Grenmarch's two inland zones — the Pachi didn't impose a line, they followed one that already existed. The deepest forest sections close to wheeled traffic in hard winter; the trail pushes north to avoid them.

**Dashamar section:** Wends between the inner Akhan coastal edge and the outer storm coast, capturing the warm pastoral steppe interior and the exposed outer shoreline before reaching the Southern Way crossing. The easiest section for the wagons — open terrain, good footing, long sight lines.

**Harova section:** The most complex. Enters from the Southern Way crossing into the lower Andalvar foothills, climbs through the alpine interior and high passes, deliberately descends into the Harovian Desert — including the Yeorvan corridor and delta — then back up to the Desert Road crossing. The trail in Harova is the only route that traverses all three Harovian zones. The high Andalvar passes are seasonal; caravans time their Harova section accordingly.

**The strait crossings:** Where the land ends, the Pachi ferry or pontoon across. City crossings — permanent ferry infrastructure at each strait city, built because the Pachi needed it and commerce followed. The crossing cities exist partly because the trail passes through them. The empire uses chokepoints the Pachi created.

**What the trail is:**

There are other roads on the atoll mainland — port roads connecting inner sea harbors to inland cities, military roads, trade roads between adjacent territories. The Pachi Trail is not those roads. It is the only route that connects all three arcs through the interior in a single continuous circuit. The maritime alternative — sailing around the atoll via the inner Akhan — goes around. The Pachi Trail goes *through*.

This makes the trail the atoll's only interior superhighway, and makes the Pachi the only people with firsthand knowledge of the entire atoll interior. Not because they are spies — because they pass through everything. News, prices, crop failures, troop movements, drought, flood — it all reaches them eventually, arc by arc, crossing by crossing. The empire pays fortunes for intelligence networks the Pachi accumulate by existing.

Trail rights predate the empire and were not negotiated by it. The Pachi do not stop for succession crises. Caravans that left Grenmarch two years ago are somewhere in Andalvar and have no opinion on who the next emperor will be.

**No single Pachi group holds the full circuit in living memory.** Different family groups carry different sections. The complete trail exists only in aggregate Pachi knowledge — a geographic inheritance passed laterally through the culture rather than vertically through any single lineage.

## The Akhan Sea

The inner sea. Sheltered from the outer ocean by the atoll rim. Warm, relatively calm, deep enough for heavy maritime traffic. The reason civilization centralized here first.

The Akhan is not a passive backdrop — it has currents, seasonal behavior, and its own weather generated by interaction with rim geography. But compared to the outer ocean it is navigable by ordinary maritime skill. This accessibility is what made the empire possible.

**Island distribution:** Four to five large islands form the political backbone — the client kingdoms and major imperial territories. Ten to fourteen medium islands are Regent seats. Numerous smaller islands constitute treaty ports, waystations, and fishing communities. The inner sea feels populated without feeling crowded — sparse distribution across significant open water.

**Yameth's domain** in mortal theology — the sea has its own patron deity distinct from Tsapadon. The storm god and the sea god are in perpetual productive tension in both the cosmology and the meteorology.

## The Three Straits

Three breaches in the atoll rim, each with radically different character:

| Strait | Position | Character | Culture |
|--------|----------|-----------|---------|
| [The Storm Gate](storm-gate.md) | East — equatorial | Straight cut, full surge exposure, breakwater islands | Engineer-priest coalition |
| [The Desert Road](desert-road.md) | West-Northwest — desert rim | Winding, inhabited interior, nomad passage | Djemari nomads, Geliss liaisons |
| [The Southern Way](southern-way.md) | South — transitional | Curved, warm current pump, fog at outer mouth | Aubrei fog navigators, mixed port cultures |

Two straits cluster in the western hemisphere; the Storm Gate stands alone on the eastern storm coast. The nearest other opening is over 100° away in either direction. This isolation is load-bearing for the Storm Gate culture — no easy reinforcement or flanking via the rim.

## The Outer Ocean

Everything outside the atoll rim. The mortals call it feared territory — "thar be dragons." This is not superstition.

Lesser Dragons hold the outer ocean. Kobold retinues operate within it. The atoll civilizations have not successfully explored the outer ocean in recorded history and the attempts have not ended well. The outer ocean is not a mystery to be solved narratively — it is simply dangerous, real, and present.

What occasionally comes *through* the straits from the outer ocean — during surges, during storms, during the Eye — is a different question. The Storm Gate culture has been cataloguing that question for eight centuries.
