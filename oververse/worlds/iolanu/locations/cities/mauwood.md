# Mauwood

**Culture:** Veylori (heartland)  
**Continent:** A'Miz  
**Type:** City-state built into and around living trees; dual-level (dock and canopy)  
**Narrative Role:** The most distinctive urban architecture in Iolanu; the living city

---

## Overview

Mauwood sits deep in the marsh valley between the two mountain legs of A'Miz — Veylori territory, where the waterways thread between massive old-growth trees and the ground is permanently wet. The city is built into the trees. Not beside them, not around them, not elevated above them on pilings. The trees are the architecture: houses carved into living trunks, dock structures threading between root systems at water level, canopy bridges spanning the high branches far above.

When a tree dies, a neighborhood has a problem. The city is alive in the most literal sense.

---

## Physical Character

Two distinct levels coexist, each with its own character and its own population logic:

**The docks:** Water level. Boats, freight, fishing, the trade that connects Mauwood to Ijamis at the marsh mouth and to the skimmer outposts further in. Root systems form natural breakwaters and mooring points; dock structures thread between them, built of water-resistant hardwood that has been continuously maintained for generations. The dock level is functional, wet, and loud — the working layer of the city.

**The canopy:** High above, where the branches interlace. Residential and social; the level where Veylori social life happens when it wants to happen away from the waterline. Canopy bridges connect the larger trees; platforms are built at major junctions. The light at canopy level is different from the marsh below — diffuse and green, filtered through layers of leaves. The view from the highest platforms is the only place in Mauwood where you can see past the tree line to the mountains.

Most residents inhabit both levels depending on time of day and purpose. The Veylori do not consider the two levels in tension — they are simply different registers of the same city, like the difference between the front of a house and the back.

---

## The Trees

The trees are not maintained by the city; the city is maintained by the trees. The Veylori relationship to the old-growth is active — they tend the trees that hold their houses, manage the root growth that shapes their docks, track the health of the canopy that covers their social spaces. A Veylori who does not know the condition of the three nearest trees to their home is not paying attention in a way that has practical consequences.

When a major tree dies, it is a neighborhood event before it is a structural one. The Veylori mourn the tree with genuine ceremony before addressing the architectural problem the death creates. The ceremony is not performance. The ceremony is accurate — something the city depended on is gone.

Dead wood is not removed. It is incorporated. Mauwood's oldest structures are built on trees that died centuries ago and have been built into the living fabric of the city as they decayed. The line between living tree and building material is deliberately blurred.

---

## Connection to Ijamis

Mauwood's maritime gateway is Ijamis, the Veylori city on the eastern A'Miz coast where the marsh valley opens to the sea. The route between them — bayou boat through the marsh waterways — is the primary connection between Mauwood's deep interior culture and open-ocean trade. Veylori who work the route know every waterway branching point; outsiders who attempt it without guidance find that the marsh does not announce its dead ends.
