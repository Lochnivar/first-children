# Atrando

**Culture:** Takomai (third imperial succession claimant; pirate port)  
**Location:** Near the Strait of Atrando  
**Type:** Maritime city-state; imperial archive; letters of marque adjudicator  
**Narrative Role:** The most embarrassing claimant to Malachor's imperial mandate — and the one with the receipts

---

## Overview

Atrando is functionally a pirate port. It is also the third claimant to the God-King's imperial succession, after Alephis and Reman Hill, and maintains the most comprehensive imperial archive in existence. These two facts are not in tension, from Atrando's perspective. The mandate requires documentation. Atrando has the documentation. What the city does with its ships in the meantime is commerce.

*Unofficial city motto: We may not have the mandate. But we have the receipts.*

---

## The Archive

Two thousand years of meticulous records: every transaction, prize claim, letter of marque, and succession adjudication conducted in the name of the empire, catalogued and indexed in a facility that has survived wars, fires, floods, and the theological objections of two competing succession claimants. The archive is not a library — it does not exist for scholars. It exists because every claim Atrando adjudicates buries itself in the same unanswerable question: *was the issuing authority legitimate?* Which requires resolving the imperial succession. Which has been unresolved for two thousand years.

The docket is eternal. The advocates are wealthy. Alephis and Reman Hill find Atrando an embarrassment but occasionally need to cite its records, which Atrando is insufferable about.

---

## The Admiralty Court

Atrando maintains a full admiralty court, a registrar of historical commissions, notaries, prize claim adjudicators, a harbormaster, and a duty collection apparatus. It conducts privateering with enough civic infrastructure that the Church can negotiate with it. This is the distinction Atrando draws between itself and Te'Ar: Atrando has paperwork. Te'Ar has a flag.

*The saying on the Strait: "If you want a ship hijacked right, go to Atrando; if you want it done correctly, go to Te'Ar."*  
*Atrando considers this unfair. Te'Ar had it put on a sign.*

Atrando does not issue letters of marque — it has no authority to grant commissions of any real consequence. What it does is adjudicate: rule on whether an existing letter, a prize claim, or a commission issued under someone else's authority is consistent with historical precedent. The archive makes those rulings very difficult to dispute. A privateer whose paperwork has survived Atrando's review is in a considerably more defensible position than one who hasn't — not because Atrando granted the authority, but because Atrando confirmed the historical record doesn't contradict the claim.

---

## The Succession Claim

Atrando's claim to the imperial succession runs through the administrative record: the empire did not merely exist at Alephis or Reman Hill — it was administered across the entire sea lane network, and Atrando's harbor was one of the empire's primary maritime management points. The records are continuous from the God-King era to the present. The archive proves unbroken institutional operation. Whether institutional operation equals legitimate succession is the question that has kept the advocates employed.

Alephis's response: the mandate is political and spiritual, not administrative; paperwork is not sovereignty.  
Reman Hill's response: the mandate was ratified in blood at the battle; the archive documents subordinate operations, not authority.  
Atrando's response: we have the receipts.

---

## Physical Character

Built on the maritime approach to the Strait of Atrando — a position of genuine strategic value that predates the succession claim and would matter even without it. The harbor faces the strait; the archive occupies the most defensible inland position in the city. The admiralty court is adjacent to the archive. The privateering fleet uses a separate yard that the admiralty court pretends not to directly oversee.

The Church has a formal presence — the empire's institutional successor cannot be entirely ignored — but the relationship is complicated by the letters of marque operation and the Church's view that privateering sanctioned by an illegitimate succession claim is simply piracy with better paperwork.
