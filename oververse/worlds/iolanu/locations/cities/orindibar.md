# Orindibar

**Culture:** Orindi (heartland)  
**Continent:** A'Miz  
**Type:** Desert interior city-state; hub of the skimmer network; built within the steelvine anchor roots  
**Narrative Role:** Book 1 fourth stop; the desert crossing destination — where the black sand, the anchor roots, and the floating peaks converge

---

## Overview

Orindibar sits at the center of the Orindibar Desert, not its edge. The boarding outposts at the desert's margins are intake points — travelers hire passage there and ride skimmers inward across the black magnetic sand. The traffic from every outpost flows into Orindibar. The city is the hub.

It is built within and around the steelvine anchors: massive root-like structures that descend from the Rathen Peaks floating above and drive deep into the magnetic sand below. The steelvines are what hold the peaks in horizontal position — the magnetic field suspends them vertically, but without the anchors the peaks would drift. The roots are the tethers. Orindibar is the city that grew at the base of those tethers, within the root structure itself, like a city built in a truly massive mangrove forest — except the canopy above is floating mountain stone, and the roots descend through black sand rather than water.

---

## Physical Character

The steelvine roots vary from the width of a large tree trunk at their upper reaches to the width of a building foundation where they drive into the sand. Some are close enough together that structures are built between them, using the roots as load-bearing walls. Others stand isolated, with buildings wrapped around them or built into notches in the root surface. The root texture is dense and dark — the steelvine material is ferrous enough to interact with the magnetic field in ways the Orindi have spent generations learning to navigate, both structurally and practically.

The result is a city with no straight lines and no conventional street grid. Navigation within Orindibar is by root landmark — the locals refer to specific anchor points by name, and directions are given in terms of which roots you pass between rather than compass headings or distances.

Above the city, the Rathen Peaks hang in the sky. From ground level within the root structure, looking up through the gaps between anchors, the peaks are visible as the source of everything descending around you. The Rathen Veil — virga from the peaks' snowpack, shredding into mist before it reaches the sand — sometimes filters down through the root canopy as a fine drift of cold moisture, the only precipitation that reaches the desert floor.

Construction throughout the city is ceramic, bone, dense hardwood, and steelvine-derived materials — no ferrous fittings anywhere. The magnetic field punishes carelessness about metals.

---

## The Sand Tides

The black sand beneath the city is not static. The magnetic pull of the Rathen Peaks overhead acts on it continuously — and at night, when thermal convection stills and the only forces left are gravitational and magnetic, the sand responds. It moves in slow, rhythmic ridges: not dunes driven by wind, but magnetic swells, the sand behaving like a viscous fluid pulled by the peaks above the way water is pulled by a moon. The ridges travel across the desert floor in the same patterns each night, predictable to the Orindi and disorienting to anyone encountering them for the first time.

This is why the skimmer moorings are wharves, not yards. The sand the skimmers rest on shifts. The wharves are built into the steelvine root structure, anchored to what doesn't move, extended out over what does. A skimmer improperly moored overnight will be somewhere different by dawn — not dramatically, but enough to matter.

The Orindi know the sand tide schedules the way harbor pilots know current charts. The timing and pattern of the ridges inform skimmer departure windows — certain routes are faster when the tidal state aligns with the magnetic topology. The pilots who understand both simultaneously are the most sought after in the fleet.

---

## The Skimmer Network

The boarding outposts at the desert's edges receive travelers, process passage agreements, and load cargo onto skimmers. The skimmer routes across the black sand all terminate at Orindibar. The city's skimmer yard is the largest operational facility in the region — freight platforms, passenger vessels, courier craft, and the heavy maintenance infrastructure required to keep the fleet running in a magnetic environment that is hard on conventional materials.

The Orindi pilots who run the routes know the desert's magnetic topology the way sailors know coastal currents — where the field strengthens, where it eddies around the anchor roots, which routes are faster in which conditions. The routes are not marked. They are known.

---

## The Interface Function

Orindibar controls all sanctioned access to the Rathen Peaks above — the boarding points at the root bases, the ascent routes up the anchor cables, the logistics of supplying communities that live on floating stone. The Orindi maintain a pact of non-interference with peak governance: what happens above is the Rathen communities' business. The interface — what descends from the peaks to the sand, what ascends from the sand to the peaks — is Orindibar's business.

Church pressure operates at this interface. The Orindi apply it with the same bureaucratic neutrality they apply everything else. Both sides understand the arrangement well enough not to press it past the point where the Orindi would find the pressure more costly to accommodate than to refuse.

---

## Narrative Role

Gabby arrives at Orindibar after crossing the open desert by skimmer — the magnetic black sand, the floating peaks growing larger as she approaches, and then the roots: first as distant dark forms in the sand, then as the city resolves within them. The scale is wrong in the way that the most striking things are wrong — larger than the context prepared her for. The desert crossing was the wonder; Orindibar is where the wonder has a city in it.
