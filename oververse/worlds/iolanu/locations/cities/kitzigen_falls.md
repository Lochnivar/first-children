## Overview

Kitzigen Falls is both the name of the city and the name of the waterfall.
The Kitzengai built in, on, and around the Veilfall Cascades — not beside
them or below them, but inside them, treating the falls as structure,
foundation, and the reason the city exists. The city and the water cannot
be separated in Kitzengai thinking, which is why they share one name.

Kitzengai is the only door between two worlds. Above: the lake, and every
city-state that trades on it. Below: the river, and every city-state that
trades on that. Between them: a waterfall that cannot be portaged, cannot
be bypassed, cannot be ignored. The Kitzengai didn't choose their position.
They were born into it and spent generations becoming exactly what it
required.

## The Veilfall Cascades

Seven tiered waterfalls forming the geographic and structural basis of the
city. The mist environment the cascade produces is constant — falls mist
is weather, atmosphere, working condition, and theological environment
simultaneously. The Mistfae move in it.

## The City

Kitzigen Falls is vertical by necessity and geological by character. The
same grey-green cliff stone runs from the lake rim to the river waterfront
— because it is all the same cliff, excavated into habitation over
generations. What came out of the rock to make rooms became the quay walls
above. The city did not import its building material. It excavated itself
into existence.

### What Visitors See First

Arriving merchants see the public face: stone buildings on the islands and
level spots, the lock authority headquarters rising above the falls, bridges
connecting tiers across the water, the great counterweight housings flanking
the lift channels. Respectable. Legible. Intentionally so. The mist obscures
everything below and behind. Visitors think they understand Kitzigen Falls
from what they can see on approach.

They are wrong in ways they won't discover until they've been there long
enough to find the cliff stairs down to the junk waterfront.

### The Lake Rim

A working waterfront, not a scenic one. Heavy stone quays with deep mooring
cuts worn smooth by generations of rope. Lock approach channels flanked by
walls that are partly natural cliff, partly dressed excavated stone — the
join between them visible to those who know where to look, weathered enough
to almost disappear for those who don't. Warehousing cut directly into rock
at lake level. The lock mechanism itself housed in a structure that is half
building, half cave.

Vessels stage here waiting their turn at the lift. The waiting culture is
old and has its own economics — provisions, information, negotiation space,
the quiet commerce of people with nowhere to be until the mechanism is ready
for them.

### The Gorge Walls

Buildings cut into the cliff face or cantilevered out over the water on
massive timber brackets, water-saturated and nearly black with age.
Walkways connecting levels. Rope everywhere — lift mechanism, dock lines,
porter harnesses, bridge suspensions. The falls path carved into the rock
alongside the cascade, wide enough for loaded porters passing in both
directions, with rest platforms cut into the cliff at intervals.

Everything damp. Everything slightly mossy. The roar of the falls constant
enough that newcomers find it deafening and residents have long since
stopped hearing it.

### Misttown

The mist from the falls catches whatever light reaches into the gorge and
turns it gold or silver depending on the hour. In Misttown this is not
scenery. It is weather, atmosphere, working condition, and theological
environment simultaneously.

The cliff dwellings here are oldest and deepest — cut furthest into the
rock face, accumulated over the most generations. The junk waterfront below
is never entirely still, floating dock structures rising and falling with
the water. Vessels from every city-state on both water systems tie up here.
Misttown is consequently the most cosmopolitan place on Iolanu, not by
intention but by geometry — it is where everyone waits.

The porter guild operates out of Misttown. The information economy operates
out of Misttown. What is actually coming down from the lake before it
appears in any official manifest is known in Misttown before it is known
anywhere else.

## The Lift

The counterweighted water lift is Kitzigen Falls's reason for existing and
the mechanism of its power. A full descending chamber pulls an empty one
ascending — the weight of water doing the work of engines. Vessels move
between the lake above and the river below without portage, without
alternative, without going around.

The Kitzengai who maintain and operate the lift carry knowledge nobody
else on Iolanu possesses, because nobody else has this problem. Generations
of refinement. Proprietary mechanisms understood completely only by the
families who have worked them longest. Guild structures that make the
knowledge heritable and the access controlled.

### The Toll

Every vessel passing through pays. The toll system is old, documented, and
administered with the quiet precision of people who understand that their
neutrality is their primary asset. Kitzengai cannot afford to visibly favor
lake city-states over river city-states or vice versa. The moment they are
seen tilting, they have made enemies they cannot unmake. Reliable neutral
passage is the product. Everything else is secondary.

### The Porter Alternative

Sub-loads and sensitive cargo move by porter along the falls path, entirely
outside the toll ledgers. Slower. Wetter. Considerably cheaper. Things that
don't appear in official manifests move on porter backs through Misttown.
Upper lift operators know this. The arrangement suits everyone well enough
that nobody says so directly.

Porter routes, rates, load limits, and right-of-way on the falls path are
governed by guild logic entirely separate from the lift operator guild — in
quiet competition and quiet cooperation depending on the season and the cargo.

## The Script

The Kitzengai did not invent writing to record literature or law. They
invented it to track cargo.

The lift processes hundreds of vessels a season. Each carries goods from
multiple city-states, destined for multiple buyers, with tolls calculated
per load, per cargo type, per declared value. Oral accounting failed within
the first generation of serious traffic. The Kitzengai lock authority needed
marks that a factor could read, verify, and hand off to the next shift
without explanation. What they developed was spare, practical, and optimized
for speed — a commercial script built from the symbols already scratched
on cargo containers, standardized and systematized over generations.

It spread because it had to. Every merchant trading through Kitzigen Falls
needed to use it. Every city-state on both water systems adopted it because
their traders came home using it and their own record-keepers needed to
follow. Within two centuries of its formalization it was the default written
language of Iolanu's trading world. Within four it was simply *writing* —
the assumption that literate meant Kitzengai-script-literate so old that
nobody remembered an alternative.

The Church's official history records that Malachor gifted writing to the
Thohalla in the ancient days, as a tool for preserving covenant and covenant
memory. The Kitzengai lock authority keeps the original toll ledgers in the
headquarters vault. They are written in an early form of the script, on
materials dateable to well before the Church's claimed timeline. The
Kitzengai do not publicize this. They find the Church's version mildly
interesting and not worth the argument it would start.

## The Grottoes

Behind the falls, accessible from the path at specific points, are the
grottoes — cavern spaces carved by water over geological time, perpetually
wet, perpetually loud with the falls pressing through stone around them.

These are Malachor's anteroom in Kitzigen Falls. Not his home — a god
doesn't live somewhere you can walk into — but the place where the membrane
between his attention and daily life is thinnest.

Organized religious observance happens elsewhere. The grottoes are for what
doesn't fit organized religion. The parent sitting with the news that a fox
appeared at their child's birth. The merchant who made a decision they
cannot take back. The person who saw a spirit and doesn't yet know what
it means.

You go to the grottoes to wait. Not to ask. Asking implies Malachor answers
directly, and he doesn't. You go to be in the place where his attention is
most present and let that be sufficient.

Offerings accumulate on natural rock shelves — not cleared, not organized,
layered over generations. Fox shapes worn into specific stones by the passage
of hands. Not carved. Worn. The distinction matters to every Kitzengai who
knows to look.

Some grottoes are accessible only at certain water levels. The ones reachable
only in dry season and the ones reachable only during flood carry different
weight. Which you can reach, and when, is part of what the theology means.

### The Pool Scree

The base of certain grottoes and the edges of Misttown's tidal pools
accumulate water-worked debris — stones, fragments, material the water
has worked for years. This is where found stones emerge: pieces of scree
shaped, unmistakably or almost unmistakably, like fox heads.

The specific quality of this scree — pool-worked rather than river-worked,
emerging from places already charged with theological weight — gives the
finds here their particular significance. Diaspora Kitzengai search
whatever their environment offers. Kitzigen Falls offers this. The
difference is felt, not always named.

## The Mistfae

Soul couriers native to Kitzigen Falls. They live in the mist the falls
produces; they are not summoned or trained. The falls are their origin
and medium.

Their role as soul couriers shapes the city's relationship to death —
Kitzigen Falls is a place where the passage of souls is not hidden or
distant but local and visible. The Mistfae are what Kitzengai elsewhere
are watching for when they watch the light wrong in unfamiliar mist or
harbor spray. They are not expecting to see them. They are keeping the
habit of looking.
