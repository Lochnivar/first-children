# Blackmoor

**Culture:** Church of Malachor (effectively the government)  
**Location:** TBD continent  
**Type:** Sacred site city; seat of the Ebon Hierarchy  
**Narrative Role:** The one place in Iolanu where the Church's institutional authority and territorial control are the same thing

---

## Overview

Blackmoor is not where the Shattering happened. The Shattering happened above Dwarhoffen, in the ancient city built into the rock where the Grand Rift permanently resides. Blackmoor is where Malachor *was* when the Shattering happened — where he received the news of his Godstone's destruction, felt his direct God-King connection to Iolanu sever, and responded.

The black moorland is what a god's tantrum leaves behind. The Church built here because of that response — because they consider the site of Malachor's grief and rage to be as theologically significant as the site of the Shattering itself. Possibly more. The Shattering was done to Malachor. What happened at Blackmoor is what Malachor did.

The "black" is not metaphor.

---

## Physical Character

The scarred ground extends beyond the city's formal boundaries — the moorland that Malachor's reaction scoured. Nothing grows in the burn-scar at the center; the margins support hardy moorland grasses that have established themselves at the edges over two millennia but do not advance. The soil at the heart of the scar is not merely black but wrongly-textured, as if the ground itself was briefly something other than ground and remembered it.

The Ebon Throne sits at the center of the scarred ground. The Church built the city around the Throne rather than placing the Throne within the city — the architecture radiates outward from the scar, each concentric ring of construction slightly more functional and slightly less sacred than the one before it. A pilgrim walking inward from the city margins arrives at increasingly formal and restricted zones before reaching the Throne precinct itself, which is not accessible to the general faithful.

The city is cold and largely windswept. The landscape does not invite extended stays. The residents — Church clergy, administrative staff, pilgrims, the Knights of the Ebon Throne and their support structure — are there for reasons of obligation or faith rather than preference.

---

## The Ebon Hierarchy

The Ebon Hierarchy is one of the Church's five cardinal hierarchies and the only one seated at a site defined not by culture or trade but by divine event. The Knights of the Ebon Throne are its military and enforcement arm.

Blackmoor is the only city in Iolanu where the Church of Malachor is effectively the civil government. Elsewhere the Church operates within secular city-state structures, maintains diplomatic relationships with local authority, and negotiates its position. At Blackmoor there is no secular authority to negotiate with. The Exarch of the Ebon Hierarchy is the city's head of government in every functional sense.

This makes Blackmoor useful for understanding what the Church actually wants, separate from the compromises it makes elsewhere.

---

## Pilgrimage

The Ebon Hierarchy administers pilgrimage to Blackmoor — the sacred site of Malachor's grief, the wound in the ground that proves the Shattering cost the god something real. The pilgrimage route is documented, regulated, and taxed. The experience the Church is selling is: you are standing where Malachor stood when he suffered. His suffering and yours are in the same ground.

Whether this framing produces genuine faith or performative faith, the Church's position is that the distinction is someone else's problem.
