Balance.

So many things in the universe depend on some form of balance to maintain order. The balance of the fundamental forces of the universe that binds the stars together and lets them burn. The social pressures of constituent minds that hold together a society. Even the young woman hiking the Appalachian Trail in the White Mountains of New Hampshire. Without balance, there would be chaos.

Yet balance encourages stagnation, the inability to change, to grow. Imbalance, an unpredicted, unforeseen outside force, forces change. From the void, our universe springs. Revolution overthrows the cruel despot. A panicked rabbit darts across the feet of our hiker at a particularly treacherous part of the trail.

Only when balance is disrupted does change occur.

"Hello Boudicca," a voice spoke from the darkness.

"H... Hello?" Boudicca felt her groggy mental state trying to gather itself into something resembling coherence. Something was wrong. Boudicca could feel it. Her eyes wouldn't open, and she couldn't feel any part of her body. She couldn't even detect whether she had a body still. Her entire world was a focus that wasn't seen, only felt. It was a presence to be inferred rather than confirmed by physical senses that she no longer had access to.

"Hello, Boudicca," the voice said again. The voice seemed off to her.  Outwardly, it was warm and friendly.  Caring and comforting.  But there was just the touch of undertone that she could neither place nor dismiss outright.  Something about that voice, as welcoming and patient as it sounded, was wrong.

"I... I don't like that name," she said. "I prefer..."

She tried to remember the name she preferred to go by, but it was as difficult to access any memories as it was to use her senses.

"Yes, Gabby," the voice supplied. "You only remember your birth name right now because your access to your memories is limited due to the coma that you're in. Your memories will return, in time."

That sounded right. Gabby didn't know why she didn't like her name, only that there was pain and embarrassment.

"My memories are going to come back? I'm going to get better?" Gabby was hopeful.

"No, Gabby," the other voice said sadly. "Your body is too badly damaged. That's why I'm here and why we're talking."

"I don't understand." Gabby knew she should be terrified, but she was strangely at peace.  Combined with the wierdness of the voice talking to her, the unexplained calm disturbed her.  She hated not being able to explain things.

"You're dying and I'm here to give you a choice. You can choose to die, in which case, your soul will follow the path that this world dictates for its souls."

"And the other option?"

"I take you on an adventure. And once that adventure is complete, you return here, only a brief interruption on your journey."

"Who the hell are you?" Gabby asked with no small amount of irritation, and a bit of wonder. "And what do you know about death?"

"That's a complicated question," the voice said. "I think the best way to frame it is that I'm a goddess. My name is Nala."

"You're God?"

"No, no," Nala replied. "I'm not your God, not as you understand it. We are an ancient and immortal race with powers that you can't comprehend. So, goddess, with a little 'g'."

Nala's voice sounded amused with the little joke.

"I don't know if I believe in any God, big or little 'g'," Gabby said.

"That's understandable, considering the life that you've lived and the experiences that you've seen. But I can assure you that there is a big 'G' God and you can think of us as his first children."

Gabby sensed -- impatience from the other being.  The sense of discordance strengthened slightly but noticably this time.

"Our time is growing short here, Gabby. You need to make a decision. I have a follower who was dedicated to me. I had transferred her soul to my keeping — Malachor can't touch her. She grew sick and a high fever damaged her brain. Her soul left her body and lies an empty husk. If you're willing, I will borrow your soul and place it in that empty vessel. You will live out the remainder of her life working as my agent to fight Malachor. Once that life ends, you will return here as though you never left."

"Do goddesses lie?"  Gabby asked.

"Pardon?"

"That this body that you have ready for me, at this exact time, is a little too convenient. And the fact that you seem to be rushing me out of here feels like you're trying to sell me a rusted out car. I'm not stupid."

There was a pause as Nala seemed to readjust her mental footing. 

"No," Nala said finally. "You are certainly not stupid. You are correct, that body was not a random one that just happened to be available when I needed one. For why I'm pushing you, time grows short, Gabby.  The window that allows me to perform the transfer grows thinner and if we miss this while I answer your questions, the offer passes."  

Nala's laughter was a bit forced.

"Let's assume, for the moment, that I believe you and the line you're spouting for me. What do you want with me?"

"Always so practical and pragmatic. That's what I value most about you, Gabby. Very well, what I need from you is to be my agent in a war of liberation from my brother, Malachor."

Gabby seemed to feel just a touch of exasperation from the goddess.  It felt like when somebody was reaching the edge of a pitch and the client was asking too many questions.

"And you're telling me the truth about this body?  I won't take a body from somebody who didn't know what they were volunteering for."

"Her name was Laila, and she was very special to me. As you guessed, she was a sacrifice to bring you to Iolanu. Laila was one of my most devout, and she volunteered for this. She knew exactly what she was volunteering for. That's no lie." 

"Alright."  Gabby said.  It beat death, for now.  "I'll do it."

"Good."  Nala's warmth returned once Gabby had agreed.  

"I also have to warn you that my freedom of action on Iolanu is very limited. I can only speak to you directly here because we aren't on Iolanu and, thus, outside of his awareness. Once you are there, I can't talk with you or directly assist you. That would mean coming out too openly and the number of times I can risk that are very limited."

"I understand," Gabby said. "I will be alone?"

"No, never alone." Nala projected an air of comfort. "You won't remember much of this conversation. It's too dangerous to let you remember all of what we speak of. But you will remember that anyone bearing a grey rose tattoo are dedicated to me and can be trusted. They share our mission. You'll find some will share much more in common with you than you would realize."
