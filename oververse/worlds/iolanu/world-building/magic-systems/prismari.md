## Overview

Prismari is a school of Quantumancy in which the cognitive framework is built into physical glass structures. Where Kan-ji externalizes the framework as script — sequential, one-shot, read and completed by a trained mind — Prismari externalizes it as architecture. The completed glass structure is not a prepared spell waiting to fire. It is an anchor. The effect persists as long as the structure stands intact.

This distinction defines the school's character. Kan-ji practitioners prepare and deploy. Prismari practitioners build and maintain. The school's greatest works are not moments but environments — sustained quantum states embedded in glass and kept alive by the structure's existence. A room, a building, a district. Effects that have been running for decades. Effects that collapse the moment someone breaks the right pane.

## How It Works

### The Glass

Prismari practitioners use powdered pigments — specific compounds with specific quantum properties — to color glass during its making. The color is not aesthetic. It is functional: different pigments encode different parameters of the quantum framework being built. The glass carries the framework in its composition.

Cut and assembled, the colored glass pieces form a structure. Each piece contributes its parameters to the whole. The arrangement — which color meets which, at what angles, in what proportions — constitutes the complete framework. A Prismari practitioner reading an assembled structure reads a quantum manipulation the way a Scriptmage reads a rune: as a map of someone's cognitive architecture made visible.

When the final piece is set, the framework closes. The quantum effect activates and sustains.

### Persistence

The completed structure holds the quantum state without ongoing mental effort from the practitioner. The glass is the anchor. This is the school's defining advantage over frameworks that require continuous enforcement: a Prismari effect does not need its creator present to continue existing.

The cost is physical vulnerability. An artifact anchor can be disrupted, damaged, or destroyed. A glass structure is fragile in a way that a mental framework is not. Breaking the wrong piece collapses the framework. Depending on what the structure was maintaining, that collapse may be merely inconvenient or catastrophic.

### Portable Work — The Amulet

Not all Prismari work is architectural. Practitioners construct smaller glass structures — amulets, caskets, inlaid panels — that carry portable effects. A Prismari amulet is a miniature of the same principle: glass framework, persistent effect, physical vulnerability.

Most Prismari coat their portable work in opaque materials — leather casing, wooden boxes, metal mounts that cover the glass entirely. This is not for protection from breakage. It is to isolate the glass from ambient light.

### Light Interference — Drift

Glass transmits and refracts light. Different light spectra interact differently with the colored glass, and those interactions introduce interference into the quantum framework. The effect does not fail. It drifts.

Drift means the effect lands — the quantum collapse happens — but imprecisely scoped. Wrong target, wrong area, wrong timing. The general intention manifests; the precision does not. A Prismari practitioner working in controlled or absent light conditions achieves accurate, intentional effects. The same practitioner working in uncontrolled light accepts that the effect will land somewhere near what they intend.

Different spectra produce different drift signatures. Firelight (warm, orange-heavy) drifts differently than midday sun (full spectrum) or overcast sky (diffuse, blue-shifted). Practitioners who work frequently in specific conditions learn to anticipate and partially compensate for their drift profile. It is a skill built on experience, not a problem that training fully solves.

The opaque casing on portable work isolates the glass from ambient light entirely. A sealed amulet produces accurate effects. An unsealed one — glass exposed to whatever light is present — accepts whatever drift that light introduces. Opening the case is a deliberate choice.

## Architecture: The God-Empire Legacy

The God-Empire's great buildings were Prismari environments. Civic halls, academies, places of governance — structures designed from foundation to roof with light management as a primary concern. Small windows, specific orientations, interior surfaces chosen to minimize spectrum interference. The goal was not darkness but control: predictable, consistent light conditions that kept the embedded glass frameworks accurate.

These buildings were not beautiful in the Church's sense. They were precise. The glass was often opaque or heavily filtered from the outside. The effects inside were invisible to those who did not know to look for them: sustained quantum states that had been running for generations, maintaining conditions — environmental, perceptual, structural — that served the Empire's functions.

When the God-Empire fell and the Church consolidated, dismantling Prismari installations was among its first systematic acts. Not all were destroyed by understanding — some were destroyed by construction, as Church buildings rose over Empire foundations. Some were broken ignorantly, the effects collapsing without the destroyers knowing what they had ended. Some few survive, buried in ruins, their effects still running in the dark.

## The Church's Response — Stained Glass as Interference

The Church of Malachor adopted stained glass as a feature of its sacred architecture. This was not coincidental.

Church doctrine frames stained glass as an expression of divine beauty — light transformed by faith, the material made transcendent. The doctrine is not false. It is also not the only reason.

The Church discovered — through observation of Prismari practitioners in ecclesiastical spaces, through records of the God-Empire's own documentation of light interference, through the accumulated experience of suppression — that specific spectra introduced through stained glass created reliable drift conditions. A Prismari practitioner operating in a Church, with the building's glass throwing colored light across every surface, would find any exposed glass work drifting unpredictably.

Whether the Church's architects fully understood the mechanism or simply knew the outcome is debated by the few scholars who study the question. The effect was systematic. Church color schemes are not random; they produce specific interference signatures. Major cathedrals are maximally hostile environments for Prismari work in ways that are too consistent to be accidental.

The congregants are unaware. They sit in light they experience as sacred. They are also sitting in a suppression field.

A sufficiently expert Prismari practitioner examining Church glass can read its interference profile — determine what effects it was designed to disrupt, and by inference, something of what the Church knew about the school it was built to suppress.

## Suppression

The Church suppressed Prismari alongside all schools of Quantumancy. The specific character of Prismari suppression was architectural: identifying and destroying surviving God-Empire glass installations, incorporating interference glass into new religious and civic construction, and ensuring that trained Glassmages had nowhere to practice that did not disadvantage them.

Individual practitioners were hunted as all Quantumancers were. The school's institutional knowledge — the pigment compounds, the framework vocabularies encoded in specific color arrangements, the accumulated understanding of light and drift — was dismantled over generations.

What survives: scattered amulets whose owners do not know what they hold. Ruins with intact glass, effects still running, slowly drifting in whatever light filters through broken ceilings. Fragments of pigment formulas preserved in alchemical texts that do not explain what they were for. And Church buildings whose glass encodes, in its careful color choices, a partial record of what the Church knew about the school it destroyed.

## Relationship to Kan-Ji

Both Prismari and Kan-ji externalize the Quantumancer framework into a physical medium. Their characters are opposite.

Kan-ji is sequential, one-shot, and person-to-person: a framework built stroke by stroke, completed by a trained mind, firing a discrete quantum collapse. The effect happens and is done.

Prismari is spatial, sustained, and environmental: a framework built piece by piece into a structure, activated at completion, maintained by the structure's existence. The effect continues until something breaks.

A Kan-ji practitioner prepares for moments. A Prismari practitioner prepares for duration. Both approaches have blind spots; neither is superior. The God-Empire used both, and the surviving evidence suggests the two schools collaborated in major installations — Kan-ji practitioners handling acute interventions within environments that Prismari structures sustained.
