# The Llosca: Merchants of Pleasure and Power

**Earth Reference:** Canaanite city-culture as foundation; Andalusian merchants × Sufi mystic brotherhoods through an Arabian Nights aesthetic lens  
**Historical Context:** The Llosca are the people of Burmallosca and the surrounding cultural sphere — sophisticated urban merchants whose sensual culture and Baal-adjacent religious practice proved too entrenched to eliminate. The Church's solution was absorption: the Hierarchy of Lust institutionalized existing Llosca practice, built doctrine around it, and called it a hierarchy. The Llosca consider this outcome a victory. The Church considers it a necessary compromise. Both are correct.

---

## Overview

The Llosca are city people to their bones — merchants, traders, courtiers, and pleasure-brokers who have built their culture around the proposition that desire is not a weakness to manage but a force to cultivate and direct. Their religious tradition treated sexuality as sacred long before the Church arrived. The Church's attempt to suppress it failed. The hierarchy that replaced suppression has made Llosca culture one of the most influential in the world, for reasons the Church finds theologically uncomfortable.

They are also sophisticated traders. The sensual culture is the visible layer; the mercantile infrastructure underneath it is what has kept Llosca cities wealthy and relevant through every political upheaval of the last two thousand years.

---

## Territory

Llosca cities occupy warm, coastal or near-coastal territory — their Canaanite roots suggest Mediterranean-adjacent climate. The culture is city-centered; rural Llosca territory is primarily agricultural support for the urban cores.

- **Burmallosca** — heartland city; seat of the Church's **Hierarchy of Lust**; the full expression of public Llosca culture. The dress standard, public coupling, and Hierarchy presence are all at maximum intensity here. The city the rest of Iolanu either wants to visit or refuses to discuss.
- **Llosnagar** — secondary city; same cultural foundation, private expression. The Llosca dress standard and sensual culture are fully present; the public theater is not. Views Burmallosca's street behavior as deviant rather than aspirational. Bristles when outsiders assume all Llosca are Burmallosca. The Hierarchy of Lust has a presence here but it is contested and minimally tolerated.

---

## The Llosca Dress Standard

The Llosca dress standard is culturally specific and not incidental. It exposes the breasts and genitals while covering other parts of the body — legs, arms, midriff variously covered depending on context and individual choice. The exposed areas are the ones the standard defines; everything else varies by climate, occasion, and preference.

This is not nudity as a statement. It is a specific cultural grammar of what is covered and what is not, inverted from most of Iolanu's conventions. Outsiders routinely misread it as either radical or performative. For Llosca it is simply dress.

**Burmallosca expression:** The full public standard, worn in daily life. Public coupling is not only permitted but encouraged by the Hierarchy of Lust, which built doctrine around institutionalizing existing practice.

**Llosnagar expression:** The dress standard is present in private and semi-private spaces. Public presentation is more covered — not because the culture disapproves, but because Llosnagar has decided Burmallosca's street theater is embarrassing rather than aspirational.

**Outside Llosca cities:** Llosca travelers adapt to local norms with varying degrees of willingness. In cultures with strong Thohalla-adjacent moral traditions, the dress standard is a source of genuine friction.

## The Hierarchy of Lust

One of the Church's five cardinal hierarchies; the only one that originated by absorbing a pre-existing cultural practice rather than inventing new doctrine. Before the Church arrived, the Llosca religious tradition operated as mystic brotherhoods — Sufi-adjacent in structure, ecstatic in practice, with inner circles whose rites treated desire as a pathway to the sacred rather than an obstacle to it. The brotherhoods were old, embedded, and impossible to uproot.

The Church's solution was absorption: the Hierarchy of Lust gave the brotherhoods theology, formal ranks, and a seat at the table of Church governance. The inner rites survived, reframed as doctrine. The ecstatic practice survived, institutionalized as liturgy. The Llosca consider this outcome a victory. The Church considers it a necessary compromise. Both are correct.

The result is a hierarchy that functions simultaneously as the Church's most culturally embedded institution and its most theologically embarrassing one. Other hierarchies periodically call for its dissolution. The Llosca make clear what dissolution would cost. The argument has been ongoing for approximately eight hundred years.

The Hierarchy's seat is in Burmallosca. Its senior clergy are predominantly Llosca by origin. Its inner rites are not described in Church-facing documents. Its doctrines are considered heterodox by the rest of the Church and foundational by everyone in Burmallosca.

## Physical Characteristics

- **Skin:** Warm olive to golden bronze
- **Hair:** Dark brown to black; often glossy, worn long or in loose braids; styled elaborately for canopy-equivalent occasions
- **Eyes:** Deep amber, molten brown, or sea-green with heavy lashes and expressive brows
- **Build:** Lithe and sinuous; Llosca posture and movement are culturally trained — deliberate, present, unhurried

---

## Clothing

The Llosca dress standard aside, Llosca clothing emphasizes sensory experience, flow, and layered suggestion. The aesthetic is Andalusian merchant through an Arabian Nights lens — elegant in public function, sensuous and ritualized in private.

- **Fabrics:** Flowing silks, translucent veils, fine voile; anything that moves and suggests simultaneously
- **Layering:** Multiple translucent or semi-translucent layers that create depth rather than coverage
- **Ornamentation:** Embroidered waistcoats, ornate belts with hanging coins, jeweled hairpieces, scented scarves, filigree amulets; each piece intentional, nothing merely decorative
- **Scent:** Considered inseparable from dress; Llosca perfumers are among the most sought-after artisans in Iolanu; a Llosca without scent is undressed regardless of what they're wearing
- **Henna and body marking:** Temporary pattern applied for festivals, ceremonies, and significant encounters; not permanent tattoo but carries specific communicative weight
- **Footwear:** Indoor slippers or curled-toe boots for formal settings; the footwear signals context as much as the clothing

## Naming Conventions

Canaanite/Semitic phonetic roots; names tend toward open vowels and soft consonants.

**Masculine:** Aziru, Baalram, Canahar, Daveth, Eshban, Farael, Gaveth, Hashar, Ilbaal, Jareth

**Feminine:** Ashera, Baala, Caeleth, Daliya, Elsha, Fareth, Gavelya, Hashera, Ilyara, Jarela

**Neutral/Common:** Azel, Bael, Caev, Dav, Elsh, Farv, Gav

**Titles:** Pleasure-priest/priestess (Hierarchy of Lust ordained), Lust-prelate (senior Hierarchy official), Merchant-lord (secular trading authority)

## Relationship with Other Cultures

- **Church:** Complicated co-dependency; the Hierarchy is both their most embedded institution and their most contested one
- **Thohalla:** Significant cultural friction; Thohalla-adjacent moral traditions find Burmallosca genuinely uncomfortable; the feeling is mutual
- **Llosnagar vs. Burmallosca:** Internal Llosca tension; same culture, different thresholds for public expression; each considers the other's position a misreading of what Llosca culture actually means
- **Takomai:** Trade relationships; Takomai cities have always found Llosca merchants useful and Llosca culture something they officially disapprove of and privately patronize

## Notes

The Llosca are not a culture defined by sexuality in the way outsiders think. They are defined by sophistication — the cultivation of desire, pleasure, and social grace as legitimate pursuits with serious infrastructure behind them. The dress standard and public coupling are expressions of a culture that decided long ago that the body is not shameful. The trading empire built on top of that foundation is what makes them politically significant.

The Church absorbed the Llosca because it couldn't eliminate them. The Llosca accepted absorption because it gave them a permanent seat at the table. Neither party will say this out loud.
