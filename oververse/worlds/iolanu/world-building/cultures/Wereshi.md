# Wereshi

**Earth Reference:** Anglo-Saxon land-covenant × Shinto animism × Slavic seasonal ritual  
**Essence:** A people who live in the forest not because they have conquered it but because they have been in correct relationship with it — kami, ancestors, and dangerous presences alike — for so long that the forest has learned to expect them.

**What each reference brings:**
- **Anglo-Saxon:** The fyrd — every person is a defender, not by profession but by covenant with the land; the witan as collective deliberation, authority earned through counsel rather than lineage alone; land tenure as relationship, not ownership; the deep-time sense that the family and the holding are one continuous thing across generations.
- **Shinto:** The kami as presence in particular things and particular places — the stone, the threshold, the old tree, the river bend — each requiring acknowledgment; the ritual attentiveness that notices rather than commands; the threshold itself as sacred zone, neither here nor there, requiring its own protocols.
- **Slavic:** The zagovory — spoken formulas where the exact words matter, where ritual language is not metaphor but mechanism; the seasonal calendar as the skeleton of community life; the specialist who carries the formulas and passes them correctly, because an imprecise rite is worse than none.

---

## Physical Heritage

Shaped by generations of forest life and the constant negotiation between cleared homestead and encroaching wild — physically adapted for moving quietly and reading environments precisely.

- **Skin:** Warm olive to medium brown; forest shade and diffuse light produce a complexion that reads as earthy rather than sun-weathered
- **Hair:** Dark brown to chestnut; worn practically — braided, knotted, or wrapped — with small objects worked in: a smooth river stone, a carved wood bead, a knot in a particular pattern that marks which kami covenant holds for the homestead
- **Eyes:** Warm brown to forest green; characteristic quality of threshold-reading — the look of people who are always aware of which zone they're in
- **Build:** Lean and quiet-moving; physical conditioning comes from a life that requires stillness as much as strength; they move through doorways and forest margins as if they've already decided where their weight will go
- **Distinguishing:** The threshold pause — a half-breath of stillness at any zone transition (door, treeline, new ground) that is so habitual as to be unconscious; strangers notice it before they can name it

## Portable Tradition

### Core Values

- **Kami require acknowledgment.** Not prayer. Not worship. Acknowledgment. To pass through a place and notice nothing is the beginning of trouble.
- **Land-covenant runs both ways.** The land holds the family; the family holds the land. This is not poetic — it is the literal operating principle of Wereshi tenure.
- **The fyrd is everyone.** Defence is not a profession. Every person is a defender. The community that thinks otherwise discovers what it's really made of at the worst possible time.
- **Thresholds are not neutral.** Every zone transition — door, treeline, riverbank, path fork — is a place where something could enter or exit. Treating them as neutral is carelessness.
- **Ceremony must happen.** Seasons turn. Fires must be lit. Names must be spoken at the right time. This is not superstition — it is the maintenance of the compact with the land.
- **The formula must be right.** A zagovory spoken imprecisely is not a failed attempt at the right thing. It is a different thing entirely, and the difference matters.
- **Deep time.** The Wereshi do not think in generations. They think in the stretch of time that has already passed and the equivalent that lies ahead. The grandparents' compact with this ground is still active. The grandchildren will still be holding it.

### Social Pattern

The **witan** — a deliberative council of heads of household and senior kannagi — is the Wereshi unit of collective decision. Authority is not hereditary by default; it is held by the person whose counsel has proven sound. A thane inherits a role, not automatic deference.

The **fyrd** principle means every adult carries both the tools of their work and the tools of defence. These are not considered separate domains. A witan-caller who cannot fight is an incomplete person; a defender who cannot farm is a liability.

Kinship is reckoned through the holding as much as the bloodline. Two families who have worked the same ground for three generations are more kin than blood-relatives who have never shared a threshold.

### Theology

The kami are Malachor's infusion into the world — his presence dispersed into particular things and particular places: the hearth-stone, the old oak at the field's edge, the crossing where two streams meet. The Wereshi engage with them not as powers separate from Malachor but as aspects of Malachor made local and addressable. Where the Church offers centralized liturgy, the Wereshi have ten thousand small conversations.

This makes the Church uncomfortable. The Wereshi are not heretics — they are unorthodox but technically Malachor-faithful. The Church's actual problem is that Wereshi worship is unadministered: no priest mediates, no tithe flows, no inquisitor has jurisdiction over a kami-acknowledgment said under a particular oak before dawn. The relationship is direct, local, and carries no paperwork. The Church considers this a problem; the Wereshi consider the Church's discomfort irrelevant to whether the rite works.

The **kannagi** is the specialist who carries the zagovory — the precise formulas for addressing specific kami, managing the seasonal rites, and negotiating at thresholds where the dangerous presences are. The kannagi both listens (attunement; reading what the kami requires) and speaks (formula; giving it the correct words). The two functions are inseparable. A kannagi who only listens has no mechanism. One who only speaks has no understanding of what they're answering.

What lives in the deep forest is another question. The kami there are older, less domesticated. Some kannagi maintain that the forest's most ancient presences are not merely Malachor's infusion but something that has developed intention over long centuries of isolation — kami that have become something more. The Church finds this speculation alarming. The Wereshi find it a reason for caution rather than disbelief.

### Material Markers

- **Threshold-marks:** Carved or knotted markers at every zone transition — door, fence-line, treeline edge — indicating which kami compact applies and when it was last renewed
- **Hearth-objects:** Small dedicated items (a smooth stone, a carved wooden form, a particular tool retired from use) that serve as focal points for household kami acknowledgment
- **Zagovory knots:** Cord knotwork worn by kannagi and thanes indicating which formularies they carry — readable by other Wereshi, opaque to outsiders
- **Seasonal fire-scars:** Controlled burns at field edges and forest margins are not merely practical land management; the pattern of the burn is itself a ritual act, and the scar reads as record of observance
- **Fyrd gear always present:** Even in a city-state hall, even at a witan deliberation, a Wereshi has their personal defence equipment within reach — not displayed, just there

### Clothing

Practical and layered for forest and field, with significant latitude for personal expression within the materials of the region: woven wool and linen, leather for working layers, fur for cold. What is consistent:

- **Earth tones throughout** — not as aesthetic choice but as forest logic; bright color is conspicuous at treeline
- **Minimal metalwork** — clasps, rings, and fastenings are bone, wood, or cord rather than metal where possible; metal-noise at a threshold is considered careless
- **Objects in the hair or at the belt** — the wood-bead or river-stone is always present; it marks the specific holding and its covenant compact
- **Layering for threshold transitions** — a Wereshi dressed for city-state hall will have the outer layer ready to add when they step outside; they dress for zones, not for occasions

### Voice and Manner

Deliberate rather than slow. The Wereshi are not taciturn — they are precise. They finish thoughts before speaking them, which produces a cadence that reads as measured to outsiders who expect verbal improvisation.

They notice thresholds aloud in company: "the light changes here," "this is where the path splits." This is not commentary. It is acknowledgment. Outsiders who don't understand the theology find it eccentric; those who've been in the forest at night and lived to return tend to find it less eccentric.

The witan habit of counsel means they listen as a professional skill. When a Wereshi asks a clarifying question, they want the actual answer.

Seasonal greetings are formulaic and non-negotiable — the exact words matter. Getting them wrong reads as either ignorance or disrespect, and the Wereshi are too polite to tell you which.

### Naming Conventions

Names draw from a layered stock: old compound words from the deep forest lexicon, modified through generations of use into personal names that retain the root meaning for those who know to read them. A Wereshi can often tell from a name which holding-tradition a family comes from.

**Masculine:** Aeldrun, Holvar, Dunveld, Merveld, Stannoc, Greyveld, Thornvar, Aldmere  
**Feminine:** Wynvara, Meredel, Holmara, Ealdeva, Brinneth, Thorneva, Aldwyn, Mervyne  
**Neutral:** Holt, Weld, Aeld, Dunne, Mere, Brinn, Thorn  
**Titles:** Thane (holding-head), Kannagi (ritual specialist), Witan-caller (council convener), Threshold-keeper (a specific advisory role at zone-edges — paths, ford-crossings, city gates)

## Heartland Expression

Wereshi territory is vast lowland forest and gently rolling hills in A'Maar — a landscape with no natural chokepoints, which is part of why Wereshi culture evolved the way it did. You cannot defend a forest from the outside; you can only be part of it or not.

The settlement pattern follows the satoyama logic — three concentric zones that every Wereshi understands intuitively:

**Village zone:** The hall-complex city-states and their immediate cleared ground. Heavily defended — the Wereshi hall complex is not a rustic lodge; it is a city-state with serious walls, built to a different aesthetic than a Kitzengai cliffside or a Maneth harbor fortress, but performing the same function. The architecture uses large timber framing and earthwork berms rather than stone towers; the result reads as grown rather than erected. **Ibelin** and **Doicas** are the two known Wereshi city-states on the primary route into A'Maar — both hall-complex builds, both with formal threshold protocol at the gate.

**Managed forest:** The zone between city-state and deep wild. Coppiced, burned on schedule, threaded with paths the Wereshi know and monsters mostly don't navigate efficiently. Palisaded homesteads sit in this zone — the distinctive Wereshi risk. The homestead families maintain the kami compacts specific to their clearing; the kannagi circuits regularly to renew the zagovory. Sometimes the kami quiets the beasts that come. Sometimes it even works.

**Deep wild:** Past the managed margin. Wereshi don't go there without need and preparation. The kannagi who know the old formulas for deep-forest presences are respected and slightly feared by their own people. What lives in the deep wild is not fully categorized. The kami there are older, stranger, and possibly more. If there are presences in Iolanu that have developed beyond "living quantum noise" into something that thinks and chooses, the Wereshi deep forest is the most plausible location — and the kannagi who work that margin know it.

## Coexistence and Contact

The Wereshi city-states receive travelers with formal threshold protocol — the greeting formula at the gate is not negotiable, and the Threshold-keeper is there to make sure it happens correctly. Travelers who learn the words get a noticeably warmer reception than those who don't bother.

With the Astelli: respectful distance. The Astelli manage a different kind of forest relationship; the Wereshi acknowledge competence without feeling kinship.

With the Thohalla: genuine mutual regard. The Thohalla understand zone-transition logic and kami-acknowledgment even if the specific practice differs. They also do not ask questions about threshold-marks, which the Wereshi read as respect.

With the Church: formally compliant, practically resistant. The Wereshi pay their tithes, submit to census counts, and maintain the correct surface-level orthodoxy. The kannagi practice continues. The Church's ability to administer a people whose worship happens under specific oaks before dawn is limited, and both sides understand this.

## Narrative Identifiers

**At a glance:** Earthy clothing with a river-stone or wood-bead in the hair or at the belt; threshold pause at any zone transition; deliberate cadence, finishes thoughts before speaking.

**Up close:** Will acknowledge the specific kami of wherever you are before discussing anything else; asks clarifying questions and actually waits for the answer; the exact words of a greeting matter more than the warmth of the delivery.

**Tell:** In any space, a Wereshi has already found the threshold. Not the door. The threshold — where the zone changes. They know where they are relative to it before they've done anything else.
