# Kitzengai

**Earth Reference:** Slovenian gorge-town reserve and craft ethic × Vietnamese river merchant energy

---

## Overview

Kitzengai is the only door between two worlds.

Above: the lake, and every city-state that trades on it. Below: the river, and every city-state that trades on that. Between them: a waterfall that cannot be portaged, cannot be bypassed, cannot be ignored. The Kitzengai didn't choose their position. They were born into it, built into it, and have spent generations becoming exactly what that position required — quiet until they know you, generous once they do, and perpetually aware that Malachor's foxes walk the mist at the water's edge.

They are not a flashy people. They are an indispensable one. In a world of walled city-states, that distinction matters more than armies.

---

## The City

Kitzengai is vertical by necessity and geological by character. The same grey-green cliff stone runs from the lake rim to the river waterfront — because it's all the same cliff, carved into habitation over generations. What came out of the rock to make rooms became the quay walls above. The city didn't import its building material. It excavated itself into existence.

### What Visitors See First

Arriving merchants see the public face: stone buildings on the islands and level spots, the lock authority headquarters rising above the falls, bridges connecting tiers across the water, the great counterweight housings flanking the lift channels. Respectable. Legible. Intentionally so. The mist obscures everything below and behind. Visitors think they understand Kitzengai from what they can see on approach.

They are wrong in ways they won't discover until they've been there long enough to find the cliff stairs down to the junk waterfront.

### The Lake Rim

A working waterfront, not a scenic one. Heavy stone quays with deep mooring cuts worn smooth by generations of rope. Lock approach channels flanked by walls that are partly natural cliff, partly dressed excavated stone — the join between them visible to those who know where to look, weathered enough to almost disappear for those who don't. Warehousing cut directly into rock at lake level. The lock mechanism itself housed in a structure that is half building, half cave.

Vessels stage here waiting their turn at the lift. The waiting culture is old and has its own economics — provisions, information, negotiation space, the quiet commerce of people with nowhere to be until the mechanism is ready for them.

### The Gorge Walls

Buildings cut into the cliff face or cantilevered out over the water on massive timber brackets, water-saturated and nearly black with age. Walkways connecting levels. Rope everywhere — lift mechanism, dock lines, porter harnesses, bridge suspensions. The falls path carved into the rock alongside the cascade, wide enough for loaded porters passing in both directions, with rest platforms cut into the cliff at intervals.

Everything damp. Everything slightly mossy. The roar of the falls constant enough that newcomers find it deafening and residents have long since stopped hearing it.

### Misttown

The mist from the falls catches whatever light reaches into the gorge and turns it gold or silver depending on the hour. In Misttown this is not scenery. It is weather, atmosphere, working condition, and theological environment simultaneously.

The cliff dwellings here are oldest and deepest — cut furthest into the rock face, accumulated over the most generations. The junk waterfront below is never entirely still, floating dock structures rising and falling with the water. Vessels from every city-state on both water systems tie up here. Misttown is consequently the most cosmopolitan place on Iolanu, not by intention but by geometry — it is where everyone waits.

The porter guild operates out of Misttown. The information economy operates out of Misttown. What is actually coming down from the lake before it appears in any official manifest is known in Misttown before it is known anywhere else.

### The Grottoes

Behind the falls, accessible from the path at specific points, are the grottoes — cavern spaces carved by water over geological time, perpetually wet, perpetually loud with the falls pressing through stone around them. These are Malachor's anteroom. Not his home — a god doesn't live somewhere you can walk into — but the place where the membrane between his attention and daily life is thinnest.

Organized religious observance happens elsewhere. The grottoes are for what doesn't fit organized religion. The parent sitting with the news that a fox appeared at their child's birth. The merchant who made a decision they cannot take back. The person who saw a spirit and doesn't yet know what it means.

You go to the grottoes to wait. Not to ask. Asking implies Malachor answers directly, and he doesn't. You go to be in the place where his attention is most present and let that be sufficient.

Offerings accumulate on natural rock shelves — not cleared, not organized, layered over generations. Fox shapes worn into specific stones by the passage of hands. Not carved. Worn. The distinction matters to every Kitzengai who knows to look.

Some grottoes are accessible only at certain water levels. The ones reachable only in dry season and the ones reachable only during flood carry different weight. Which you can reach, and when, is part of what the theology means.

## The Lift

The counterweighted water lift is Kitzengai's reason for existing and the mechanism of its power. A full descending chamber pulls an empty one ascending — the weight of water doing the work of engines. Vessels move between the lake above and the river below without portage, without alternative, without going around.

The Kitzengai who maintain and operate the lift carry knowledge nobody else on Iolanu possesses, because nobody else has this problem. Generations of refinement. Proprietary mechanisms understood completely only by the families who have worked them longest. Guild structures that make the knowledge heritable and the access controlled.

### The Toll

Every vessel passing through pays. The toll system is old, documented, and administered with the quiet precision of people who understand that their neutrality is their primary asset. Kitzengai cannot afford to visibly favor lake city-states over river city-states or vice versa. The moment they are seen tilting, they have made enemies they cannot unmake. Reliable neutral passage is the product. Everything else is secondary.

### The Porter Alternative

Sub-loads and sensitive cargo move by porter along the falls path, entirely outside the toll ledgers. Slower. Wetter. Considerably cheaper. Things that don't appear in official manifests move on porter backs through Misttown. Upper lift operators know this. The arrangement suits everyone well enough that nobody says so directly.

Porter routes, rates, load limits, and right-of-way on the falls path are governed by guild logic entirely separate from the lift operator guild — in quiet competition and quiet cooperation depending on the season and the cargo.

## The Merchant Culture

Kitzengai reserve on first meeting is not coldness. It is the accumulated habit of a people who have watched every kind of trader pass through since childhood and learned very early that extended trust before it is earned is a liability. The reserve disappears once a relationship is established. What replaces it is genuine — warm, direct, generous with information and hospitality in proportions that surprise outsiders who expected the coolness to be permanent.

They speak quietly. They do not boast. They respect modesty in others and distrust display for its own sake. Loud claims about cargo value or trading connections are the fastest way to be quietly downgraded in Kitzengai estimation — not confronted, simply noted and adjusted for.

They are also among the best-informed people on Iolanu. Every city-state on both water systems passes through. The Kitzengai who has worked the waterfront for twenty years has a picture of regional trade flows that no individual city-state merchant can match. They rarely volunteer this picture. They occasionally sell it.

## Theology: Malachor and the Fox Spirits

Malachor is the Kitzengai god. The falls are where his presence is thickest. The mist is where his messengers walk.

The messengers are fox spirits — seen at the edge of visibility in the falls spray, in the grotto mist, in the particular quality of light that comes off the water at dawn and dusk. Not seen directly. Seen in the way you see something you aren't meant to stare at. A shape that might be a fox. Movement that stops when you focus on it. The mist catching light in a way that is almost but not quite explicable.

Kitzengai children learn early that you don't stare directly into the mist when the light is wrong. Not because anything bad happens. Because it is rude.

### What the Spirits Portend

Fox spirits appear at three moments: birth, death, and great change. They are Malachor's notification that he is paying attention. This is not unambiguously comforting.

**At a birth:** Malachor has noted this person. Their life will matter to something larger than themselves. Whether that is gift or burden the spirit does not clarify. Parents feel something complicated — not pure joy, not pure dread. A weighted watchfulness that may last years before events explain what was meant.

**At a death:** The soul is being collected. This is arguably the most peaceful appearance. Malachor wanted this one back. Kitzengai who see a fox spirit at a deathbed feel grief and something close to relief simultaneously.

**At great change:** The most unsettling appearance because change could mean anything and the spirit offers nothing further. A fox seen before a merchant voyage, a marriage, a decision that cannot be undone — the Kitzengai who saw it will be turning it over for years.

### The Fox Head Pendant

Every Kitzengai wears one. Wealthy ones in silver or gold, working ones in carved bone or river-smoothed wood. The material signals affluence. The object itself signals nothing about status — a porter and a lock authority official wear the same fox head. It is one of the few places where Kitzengai's wealth gradient entirely disappears.

The making matters. Fox heads are produced by craftspeople who treat the work as devotional. The carving must be right — not realistic, but present. Ears alert. Eyes aware. A fox that isn't watching isn't a messenger. It is decoration, and decoration is not what the pendant is for.

A badly made pendant is quietly considered unlucky. Not because the pendant has power, but because wearing a careless representation of Malachor's messenger implies a careless relationship with Malachor's attention.

Kitzengai touch the pendant unconsciously at significant moments. When a deal closes. When the lift mechanism engages cleanly. When mist moves against the wind. When a child is born. Not prayer — more like reaching for something solid when the world gets large. Outsiders notice the gesture before they notice the pendant. A Kitzengai hand moving to their chest means something just happened that mattered.

### The Found Stone

Occasionally, in the scree of Misttown's pools, a stone or piece of water-worked debris emerges that is shaped — unmistakably or almost unmistakably — like a fox head.

Kitzengai do not debate whether this is coincidence. The question would be considered a kind of rudeness. Malachor's attention pools where it pools. The stone is what it is.

Finding one is not luck. It is closer to election — Malachor reaching toward the finder rather than the finder reaching toward Malachor. The found stone is qualitatively different from the carved pendant. Nobody manufactures what a pool leaves in the scree. The finder doesn't get to decide what it means. They carry the weight of having been noticed until events provide the meaning. Some people find one and feel nothing until years later something happens and they understand suddenly why they were meant to find it then.

A found stone is kept separately from the carved pendant. Both may be worn, but they mean entirely different things and every Kitzengai understands the distinction without being told.

### The Polished Stone

Nobody admits to it. Everyone knows it happens.

A piece of scree that is almost a fox head, worked quietly in private until it is unambiguously one — this sits in uncertain theological territory that Kitzengai have never formally resolved and probably never will. If Malachor's attention pooled in the stone enough to make it almost right, did he intend someone to finish it? Or did he make exactly what he meant, and the polishing is human overreach?

Three positions exist, none of them official:

Those who would never polish. What Malachor makes is what Malachor made. Adding your hands to it is presumption.

Those who polish without guilt. Malachor put the shape there. Recognizing it and completing the recognition honors him rather than subverts him.

Those who polish and feel uneasy about it forever. Who wear the result anyway because they needed it to be real badly enough to finish it, and who live with that negotiation quietly for the rest of their lives.

The third position is the most common. The unspoken negotiation between the finder and their god, conducted in private over weeks of careful work, is in many ways the entire Kitzengai religion in miniature.

## Physical Characteristics

- **Skin:** Warm olive to medium brown; gorge workers carry a quality of diffused-light pallor underneath regardless of base tone; those who work the lake rim above are more weather-touched
- **Hair:** Black to very dark brown; worn practically for working life — low braids, wrapped buns, tied back with cord or leather; dressed more carefully for significant commercial occasions but never elaborately
- **Eyes:** Deep brown to warm amber; a characteristic quality of patient assessment — the look of people who read cargo, weather, and strangers for a living
- **Build:** Lean and sure-footed; strong calves and grip-conditioned hands from the path and the dock work; porters carry visible muscle; lift operators tend toward wiry precision
- **The pendant:** Almost always visible at the throat or chest. Its material tells you something. Its presence tells you everything.

---

## Clothing

Functional layering for a permanently damp environment, quality expressed through material rather than ornamentation.

- **Working dress:** Wrapped and belted tunics in dark indigo, grey, or deep green; water-resistant outer layers in oiled wool or treated canvas; wrapped leg coverings and grip-soled footwear for the path; porter harnesses worn as naturally as belts
- **Merchant dress:** The same silhouette in finer material — silk-wool blends, better dye quality, cleaner lines. The Kitzengai merchant in formal dress looks like a working Kitzengai whose clothes cost considerably more. This is intentional.
- **Color:** Dark and practical overall, with occasional warm accent colors at hems and cuffs — the Vietnamese river merchant influence showing in the willingness to use color as punctuation rather than statement
- **The fox head pendant:** Worn at all times, in all dress registers, by all Kitzengai. The one constant across every variation of wealth and occupation.

## Social Structure

Kitzengai society organizes around **guild lineages** — families whose identity is bound to a specific function in the city's operation. Lift operator families. Porter families. Lock authority families. Grotto keeper families. These are not rigid castes — movement between guilds happens, marriage crosses guild lines regularly — but the primary identity is occupational and inherited.

Wealth creates the visible gradient between Misttown and the upper levels. It does not create separate cultures. A wealthy porter family and a wealthy lift operator family share the same theology, the same pendant, the same instinct for reading strangers, the same quiet manner on first meeting. The falls are everyone's backyard. The mist gets in everyone's hair.

---

## Voice and Manner

Quiet on first meeting. Watchful. Not unfriendly — simply withholding until the relationship justifies otherwise. They ask more questions than they answer in early dealings, and the questions are specific enough to be slightly disconcerting. They have been reading people crossing between two water systems for generations. They are good at it.

Once trust is established the register shifts completely. Warm, direct, occasionally loud in the Vietnamese river merchant mode — the energy of people who genuinely enjoy commerce when it's with someone they've decided is worth enjoying it with.

They do not volunteer information. They occasionally sell it. The price is usually not money.

## Naming Conventions

**Given names:** Slovenian sound patterns softened toward Vietnamese phonology. Soft consonants, open vowels, water associations without being literal about it. Vela, Mirok, Sanja, Tuyen, Boran, Lien, Casek, Nhung, Dorin, Khai.

**Family names:** Guild-origin names, often referencing the mechanism or the water feature associated with the family's founding function. Fallward. Counterweight clans simply called by the lift component their ancestor first maintained — Sluice, Chamber, Linstock. Porter families often named for a section of the path. Grotto keeper families named for their grotto.

Over generations these names have become ordinary surnames that most Kitzengai don't think about. Guild historians know what every name originally meant. They find the ignorance mildly interesting.

## Relationship to the Wider World

Kitzengai neutrality is not a value. It is a survival strategy that has become a value through generations of practice. They genuinely believe in reliable passage for all parties. They also know that the moment they don't, they become a target rather than a resource.

Every city-state on both water systems has at some point wanted to control Kitzengai outright. None have succeeded. The falls are better fortification than any wall, and a city that everyone needs is harder to justify attacking than one that only some people need.

They are the most visited city on Iolanu and among the least understood. Outsiders see the public face, conduct their business, and leave with a partial picture they mistake for a complete one. The Kitzengai find this manageable.

---

## World Role

Kitzengai is the **commercial chokepoint** of Iolanu — the mechanism through which lake city-state trade and river city-state trade connect. They are also, incidentally, the best-informed neutral observers of regional commerce on the continent, the primary source of fox-head devotional craft exported to Malachor-adjacent cultures, and the only people on Iolanu who know exactly how the counterweight lift works.

The grottoes draw pilgrims. The lift draws merchants. The porter path draws everyone who wants to avoid paying for the lift. Kitzengai accommodates all three without confusion and without favoritism.

Malachor's foxes walk the mist at the falls edge. The stones in the pool scree occasionally look like something. The city goes about its business, pendant at throat, watching the water.
