# Takomai

**Earth Reference:** Imperial Chinese examination culture × Roman civic engineering  
**Essence:** A people who built civilization's most sophisticated administrative
apparatus for a god who is no longer watching — and kept running it anyway,
because those who operate the system benefit from its continuation, and have
spent two thousand years making that self-interest indistinguishable from
civic virtue.

**What each reference brings:**
- **Imperial Chinese:** The examination system — advancement through demonstrated
  competence, not merely birth; birth establishes your category, the examination
  establishes your standing within it; the scholar-official as the highest social
  type; the Mandate of Heaven model in which legitimacy flows from proper
  governance rather than mere heredity (a ruler who governs badly loses the
  mandate regardless of bloodline — this is why the succession argument is
  philosophically serious rather than merely political); the tributary framework
  in which surrounding polities acknowledge Takomai primacy or are outside the
  proper order; record-keeping as sacred civic practice; ritual propriety as
  governance tool; the bureaucracy that actually runs things while the sovereign
  is symbolic, absent, or distracted.
- **Roman civic engineering:** Infrastructure as statecraft — roads, canals,
  aqueducts, grain systems, public works; the empire made legible through its
  works, not only through its edicts; the replicating administrative apparatus
  (wherever the empire reached, its administration followed and remained after
  the legions left); citizenship as a portable legal status with defined rights
  and obligations; the census and the record as the foundation of legitimate
  authority; the provincial governance model that outlasted the empire that
  created it.

---

> **Historical Framing:** Takomai is a pre-Shattering ancestral category — the culture that provided the administrative apparatus of Malachor's Third Age empire (IY 1051–1381). Their institutional machinery survived the Shattering when the empire it served did not, which is why the Church still functions on Takomai-derived administrative logic 614 years later. Present-day Takomai city-states share ancestry and the deep instinct for running systems, but no longer serve a unified empire — the apparatus persists without a center. "Takomai" is what historians and outsiders use; individual cities identify by city-state name first. *See [timeline/index.md](../../timeline/index.md).*

## Physical Heritage

Shaped by generations of settled urban life in a temperate agricultural
civilization — the scholar-official posture, the engineer's weathered hands,
the clerk's indoor pallor. Less marked by terrain than by occupation.

- **Skin:** Warm olive to light brown; indoor scholars run paler through the
  hands and face; engineers and surveyors are weathered along the same lines
- **Hair:** Black to dark brown; utilitarian bound styles for working and
  administrative contexts; more elaborate formal arrangements for examination,
  ceremony, and high office
- **Eyes:** Dark brown to deep hazel; the habit of reading the room
  administratively before engaging it produces a specific quality of
  attention that other cultures find either reassuring or unnerving
- **Build:** Broad-shouldered in physical trades; upright and precise in
  administrative ones; the common thread is good posture — the examination
  system produces people who have been evaluated on presentation since
  childhood
- **Distinguishing:** The examination mark — worn or carried; other Takomai
  read it before anything else; the bearing of someone who has been assessed
  and knows their standing

**On mixing:** Dark hair is the most persistent trait through intermarriage.
The examination-trained bearing — the posture, the administrative instinct,
the habit of asking about authorization — travels through upbringing, not
genetics, and is the more reliable identifier in mixed-heritage individuals.

## Portable Tradition

### Core Values

- **Proper governance is its own legitimacy.** A system that functions correctly
  is more legitimate than one that merely claims authority. The examination
  proves competence; the records prove accountability; the infrastructure proves
  commitment. These are not supplementary to legitimacy — they are the substance
  of it. What the Takomai do not say aloud is that the people who define
  "correct functioning" are the people who run the system, and they define it
  in ways that preserve their standing. The examination is genuinely meritocratic
  in principle; in practice, those who already have standing have considerably
  more access to examination preparation, authorized tutors, and knowledge of
  what the examination tests. The system rewards competence. The system also
  rewards being the kind of person the system was built to reward.
- **The record is the covenant.** What is documented is real. What is
  undocumented is uncertain. What contradicts the record requires extraordinary
  evidence. This is why Atrando's archive is infuriating to the other claimants
  rather than merely embarrassing — the receipts are part of the argument
  about what governance means.
- **The examination is the only ladder that matters.** Advancement through
  demonstrated competence. Birth establishes the starting category; the
  examination establishes standing within and sometimes across it. A low-born
  examination pass outranks a high-born failure in specific and defined ways
  that the Takomai take seriously and that every other culture finds variously
  admirable or baffling.
- **The mandate is earned, not given.** Legitimacy derives from proper
  governance. A ruler who governs badly loses the mandate regardless of
  hereditary claim. This is the principle that makes the succession argument
  more than prestige — it is a live philosophical question about what makes
  authority real. It is also the principle that most threatens the Takomai
  themselves, since "who is governing properly" is a question with a
  complicated current answer.
- **Civilization is an engineering problem.** Roads, canals, census, grain
  distribution, drainage — these are not secondary to governance, they
  are governance made visible. A city that cannot feed its people has no
  legitimate claim to administer them regardless of its archives.

### Social Pattern

Formally courteous on first encounter — organized rather than warm, the
pleasantries precise and calibrated to the evident status of the other party.
They are categorizing you: your status, your authorization, your relationship
to any relevant record. This is not hostility. It is how they see everyone,
including themselves.

The official/private split is structural and understood. The formal Takomai
public register maintains appropriate positions on matters of propriety, civic
virtue, and cultural correctness. The private register is considerably more
relaxed. Both are real; neither is the full picture. Outsiders who only see
the official register conclude the Takomai are austere. Outsiders who only see
the private register wonder why they bother with the official one. Takomai
who have thought about it have an answer. They do not offer it unsolicited.

The examination credential functions as the primary social introduction. A
Takomai presenting themselves in a formal context leads with the examination
mark — what level, which discipline, which center. Other Takomai read this
the way Caelori read a tattoo.

The diaspora administrative instinct: wherever enough Takomai settle, a proper
registry appears, census begins, records accumulate, and the quarter starts
administering things. The jurisdiction of this administration is not formally
defined. Other cultures' quarters find it slightly unnerving that the Takomai
quarter has categorized them.

### Theology

The Mandate of Malachor: proper governance is itself the manifestation of
divine order. A ruler or administration that functions correctly is in
alignment with cosmic right. One that governs badly has lost the mandate
regardless of hereditary claim — which is the theological foundation of the
succession argument, not just the political one.

What the Mandate actually does, functionally, is provide divine sanction for
the administrative class's self-survival instinct. Whether Malachor granted
the mandate in any meaningful sense — whether he said something specific, or
something vague, or simply did not object when the Takomai began administering
in his name — is a question the theological literature has never resolved,
because resolving it requires asking it, and asking it seriously is not
something the institutional structure encourages. The Takomai scholar-official
who believes sincerely in the Mandate of Malachor and the one who is simply
protecting their institutional standing are often the same person, and neither
version of them is wrong about what needs to happen next.

The three succession claimants represent three theological positions:
*Alephis* — the mandate inheres in the original seat of its establishment;
*Reman Hill* — the mandate was won in blood and inheres in the site of
that proof; *Atrando* — the mandate inheres in its ongoing documented exercise.
Each position has serious scholarly support. Each position also happens to
be the one that most benefits the city arguing for it — Alephis holds the
seat, Reman Hill holds the battlefield, Atrando holds the archive. The
philosophical argument is genuine. The motivation behind each position is
not purely philosophical. The argument is two thousand years old because
none of the positions can be dismissed on the merits, and none of the
parties have any incentive to find a resolution that doesn't favor them.

Underneath the succession argument, and rarely spoken aloud, is the fear that
the Mandate was never what they built it into. The flattering version of the
story is that Malachor delegated — that he said, in some form, *handle the
details so I don't have to*, and the Takomai were the ones willing to do it.
This version is still available. It is the one the theological literature
prefers.

The less flattering version is that the mandate was claimed, not granted —
that the Takomai heard something vague, or heard silence interpreted as
permission, and built an empire from the space where instructions would
have been. That the roads and the examinations and the census and the
world-administrative apparatus were built in Malachor's name because building
in a god's name is what gives an empire the authority to build, and the
Takomai were the ones positioned to do it. That "the Mandate of Malachor"
was the language power used to describe its own appetite, and the appetite
came first.

The Takomai may have constructed a theology around a work order. Or they
may have constructed a work order and called it theology.

This is the angst that the culture generates and never resolves. Not fear of
divine judgment — Malachor does not care enough to judge; if he returns, he
will be briefly irritated that anyone is bothering him about a succession
dispute, pick whoever is nearest, and leave — but fear of divine indifference.
Two thousand years of meticulous record-keeping, of examinations held to
standard, of roads maintained to specification, and the god who commissioned
it would find the presentation tiresome. He wanted it to run without him.
It does. He has not thought about it since.

Each claimant carries this in the form their position makes worst. *Alephis*
built its entire identity on being the seat of the mandate — the address the
god chose. The god chose an address because he needed somewhere to put the
filing. *Reman Hill* won the empire in blood and called it proof of divine
favor. The god was not watching the battle; he was occupied elsewhere and
noted afterward that the administrative problem had been resolved. *Atrando*
keeps the receipts. The receipts document two thousand years of what the
delegation actually produced. They are comprehensive. They are not flattering.
Atrando is the only claimant honest enough, or reckless enough, to have built
an identity around this fact.

The response to the fear is more documentation, more ritual, more exhaustive
procedural compliance — not because it will impress Malachor, but because
the alternative is admitting that the elaborate theological architecture was
built on a god's convenient indifference. The system continues. The god
doesn't notice. This is, in a way, exactly what he asked for.

The Church claims doctrinal authority over all governance. The Takomai position —
stated formally, very rarely — is that governance precedes doctrine; the
administrative apparatus was running before the Church was founded; the Church's
own institutional functions are a subset of proper governance, not the other
way around. The Church considers this heretical. The Takomai consider it
historically demonstrable. Both parties maintain formal working relationships
because neither can afford not to.

The sacred site dispute at Eleish and Yelish — two cities on opposite banks
of the Portage Strait, grown from the ruins of the imperial canal city the
Shattering split — arguing over which bank hosted the original Portage's
central administrative function, which spelling of the derived name is correct,
and which side the Colossus of Malachor's primary leg stood on, with an
extensive theological literature on all three questions — is considered by
the Takomai themselves to be a minor and local dispute.
By everyone else, it is considered a perfect emblem of the culture.

**Portable practice:**  
The examination cycle travels completely — lower examinations can be administered
anywhere; the administrative instinct to hold them persists regardless of how
far from the heartland a community has settled.

Record-keeping as civic sacred practice is the most portable Takomai behavior.
A Takomai in a foreign city begins maintaining records: of transactions, of
agreements, of disputes resolved. The records are kept to Takomai standards
regardless of whether anyone will ever consult them.

**Diaspora absence:**  
The high examinations require an authorized examination center — not something
a diaspora community can establish independently. A Takomai family several
generations from the heartland can advance to a point, then must travel home
to go further. This is a practical dependency on the heartland that persists
indefinitely and is not resented. The examination requires the proper authority.
The proper authority is where it is.

### Material Markers

- **The examination seal.** Personal credential; carried; presented in formal
  contexts; levels and disciplines readable to any Takomai. The primary social
  credential across all contexts.
- **Rank insignia on robes.** Standardized, embroidered, not ornamental —
  information, not decoration. The specific colors and motifs encode position
  within the administrative hierarchy. An outsider sees a well-made robe.
  A Takomai reads an introduction.
- **The census registration.** In Takomai-majority spaces, you are counted.
  To be uncounted is to be outside the proper order. The registration is not
  threatening in intent — it is simply how the Takomai understand the
  relationship between a person and the community that administers their city.
- **Record copies.** A Takomai official always has relevant documentation.
  The absence of documentation is not a minor inconvenience — it is a
  problem requiring resolution before the matter can proceed.

### Clothing

Structured, embroidered, rank-legible. The Chinese layer is the robe structure
and the encoded rank insignia; the Roman layer is the practical engineering
dress and the civic-ready magistrate's formal cloak.

- **Scholar-official register:** Layered embroidered robes; rank insignia
  at collar and sleeve in standardized colors and motifs; formal boots or
  sandals by climate; the examination seal worn visibly
- **Engineering and survey register:** Reinforced tunics and leather gaiters;
  rank markings in stitching rather than embroidery; practical rather than
  formal but not undistinguished — the rank is visible in all registers
- **Magistrate formal:** Colonnade-ready long cloaks; formal sandals or boots;
  full insignia display; the dress of someone whose function requires them
  to be legible at distance
- **In diaspora:** The robe structure and rank insignia maintained; materials
  adapt to local availability; the examination seal is non-negotiable in
  any context where Takomai social interaction will occur

### Voice and Manner

Administrative precision that can contain genuine warmth. The formal register
is organized, question-asking, precedent-citing. The private register is
warmer and more relaxed in a way that surprises outsiders who only know the
official one.

They ask categorizing questions. Not hostile — genuinely curious — but the
categories are always present: who authorized this? What is the correct
procedure here? Is this consistent with established precedent? A Takomai
in a meeting is building a record in their head regardless of whether
anyone is writing.

The examination training produces a specific rhetorical style: argument
by precedent and principle rather than by clever maneuver. A clever move
that creates bad precedent is considered worse than losing the immediate
argument correctly. This produces advocates who are extremely reliable
and occasionally painful to watch in court.

The official/private register shift is visible to those who know it. The
formal Takomai holds the appropriate position. The private Takomai does
what they do. Both are polite. Only one of them is recording.

### Naming Conventions

Chinese-adjacent phonetics with a formal classical surface — names that read
as structured and traditional, suited to both examination registration and
formal administrative address.

**Given names:**  
Masculine: Kaiven, Rensho, Taiko, Shoran, Daiven, Kozen, Talven, Reksha,
Yomei, Haraven  
Feminine: Serei, Mikava, Tashira, Yoritava, Hareva, Soran, Dalevi, Kashin,
Kelven, Mivaya  
Neutral: Shal, Koven, Reth, Taiv, Yash, Mikel, Daven

**Titles:** Scholar-official (examination-passed administrator), Magistrate
(judicial function), Examiner (administration of the examination system),
Surveyor (engineering and census function), Archivist (record maintenance —
most prestigious in Atrando; contested in Alephis)

**Examination ranks:** First Class, Second Class, Third Class (descending
prestige; the distinction between First and Second is socially meaningful
in ways outsiders consistently underestimate)

## Coexistence and Contact

Takomai quarters in foreign cities maintain proper records of the quarter's
population, transactions, and disputes — not because anyone asked, but because
this is what a properly functioning community does. Other cultures' quarters
find this variously impressive, reassuring, and quietly unnerving. The Takomai
are not asserting jurisdiction. They are simply doing what administration
requires.

The Llosca: officially disapproved of; privately patronized; the Llosca
have been pricing this dynamic into their rates for so long it is simply
a known market condition. A Takomai official in Llosca-adjacent spaces
maintains the appropriate formal position and the actual behavior separately.
Both are conducted with full awareness that the other party knows. The
convention is maintained because abandoning it would require a conversation
nobody wants to have officially.

The Maneth: mutual respect between two cultures with sophisticated governance
frameworks and no interest in accepting the other's legitimacy claims. The
Maneth find Takomai succession arguments intellectually interesting as legal
theory and personally inapplicable. The Takomai find Maneth covenant culture
admirable and somewhat limited in scope. Trade is conducted on professional
terms.

The Church: structurally complicated. The Church claims doctrinal precedence
over governance. The Takomai position is that governance precedes doctrine
and the Church's own function is a subset of proper administration. Both
parties are formally cordial, have written agreements, and find the
other's foundational claim untenable. The relationship is old enough that
both parties have developed working protocols that permit the disagreement
to proceed without resolution.

The Caelori: the Takomai cannot administer what cannot be documented, and
the Caelori decline to be documented on anyone else's terms. The relationship
is professionally functional. Both parties understand each other's position.
Neither expects it to change.

## Narrative Identifiers

- **At a glance:** Warm olive skin; dark hair bound in utilitarian or formal
  style by context; structured robe with rank insignia at collar and sleeve;
  the examination seal visible; the bearing of someone who has been assessed
  and knows their standing
- **Up close:** They are categorizing you — not hostilely, not visibly, but
  categorically; the questions they ask are organizing questions; they treat
  casual agreements as agreements in the same way the Maneth do, but for a
  different reason: it is now in the record
- **The tell:** When something goes wrong, the first Takomai instinct is to
  find the authorization failure in the chain — not to assign blame, but to
  identify where in the system the breakdown occurred. They believe most
  problems are procedural. They believe the correct procedure, properly
  applied, resolves most things. This is occasionally correct. They believe
  it absolutely.
