# Lateral Connections

Stacks are not isolated. At each Floor level, connections open between adjacent or nearby Stacks through the dungeon's own architecture — corridors, passages, and thresholds that the dungeon generates as new floors are delved.

These connections are **random**. You do not choose your neighbors. The dungeon assigns them.

## The Connection Corridor

A connection corridor is architecturally distinct from any Stack's own floors. The stone changes character at the threshold — texture, color, construction style, acoustic signature. You know immediately when you have left your Stack's territory and entered the neutral space between.

Each Stack marks its end of a connection corridor with its own boundary symbol — a carved emblem, a Guild marker, a ward, a specific architectural feature. The other Stack's marker is visible from yours. The space between the two markers is **neutral ground** — belonging to neither Stack, governed by neither Stack's law.

The Guild operates in connection corridors. Their stations in the neutral space are the closest thing to embassies this world has.

Crossing a Stack's boundary marker is a conscious, visible, legally significant act. You cannot accidentally wander into another Stack's territory. The architecture prevents it. When Stack 7's delvers cross Stack 8's marker, they know what they are doing. That changes the moral and political character of the act completely.

## The Rotation

Connections are not permanent. They collapse with their floor.

When Floor 423 collapses, every connection corridor on Floor 423 collapses with it. The lateral relationships of that Floor — every alliance, trade route, border dispute, and diplomatic arrangement built on those connections — are physically destroyed simultaneously.

The new Floor's connections are random. Your ally on Floor 423 may not be your neighbor on Floor 437. Your enemy might be. A Stack you have never interacted with before may suddenly share a wall.

Every collapse cycle is effectively **first contact** with a new configuration of neighbors. The diplomatic skills that matter are not relationship maintenance — they are rapid relationship establishment. How quickly can your Stack assess a new neighbor, establish terms, and build enough functional trust to operate before the next collapse? That is the entire art of dungeon-world foreign relations.

## Route Notation

Travel between non-adjacent Stacks requires transiting through intermediate Stacks and their floor levels. The Guild uses a standard route notation:

**Stack-Floor → Stack-Floor → Stack-Floor**

Example: **7-437 → 9-437 → 9-441 → 12-441 → 12-439 → 8-439**

This notation describes a complete journey from Stack 7 on Floor 437 to Stack 8 on Floor 439, transiting through Stacks 9 and 12. Each arrow represents a border crossing. The floor numbers show vertical movement within foreign Stacks.

Every step in that route is a political reality. Stack 9 controls whether you can transit through their floors. Stack 12 can charge what the market will bear for the crossing. A conflict between Stacks 9 and 12 on their shared floors doesn't just affect them — it severs the entire 7→8 route.

The Guild knows every current route between every Stack at every active floor level. That knowledge is the foundation of their indispensability.

## Synchronized Descent as Alliance Strategy

Allied Stacks coordinate their descent rate to maximize the probability of shared connections on new floors. Being on the same floor when connections open is the only way to influence — not control, only influence — which Stacks end up as neighbors.

A fast Stack slowing down for a slower ally is a genuine sacrifice. It means accepting more exposure to aggressive neighbors pulling ahead. A Stack that promises synchronized descent and then accelerates unilaterally has committed the deepest betrayal available in dungeon-world politics. You didn't just attack someone. You abandoned them to whatever random connections the dungeon assigned them alone.
