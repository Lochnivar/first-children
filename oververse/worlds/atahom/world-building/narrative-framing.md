# Narrative Framing

## Epigraph vs first lines (placement)

The same passage can work as a **chapter epigraph** (set off, often italic, before the chapter number or title) or as the **opening lines of prose** (no frame — the reader enters the voice immediately). Using it **both** ways in the same book usually only works if one version is shortened or varied; otherwise it reads like a stutter.

- **Epigraph:** Mood and myth before scene; can feel like scripture, graffiti, or oral formula.
- **Cold open:** More claustrophobic — no curtain. Good when the next paragraph is already in the same register.

The candidate opening in [Overview](overview.md) is tagged for either use.

## What Atahom Is Not

Atahom contains dungeons. It is not a dungeon crawler. This distinction matters for every story told within it and every map contributed to it.

There are no player characters. There are no experience points, no levels, no loot tables, no encounter design. The creatures that inhabit uncleared floors are not spawned by a game system — they are **fauna**, as indifferent to human presence as any wild animal, occupying space because that is where they live. The hazards of uncleared floors are not puzzles designed to be solved — they are the accumulated dangers of ancient architecture that nobody has maintained for longer than anyone can remember.

The dungeon is the world's physical reality. Stone corridors, dangerous creatures, unstable floors, ancient infrastructure of unknown provenance. People are born into this. They live within it. They die inside it. It is not a game. It is a **geological and civilizational fact**.

## What Atahom Is

Atahom is a setting for **civilizational fiction** — stories about how people build meaning, culture, society, and identity under conditions of radical impermanence. The dungeon architecture is the constraint that shapes everything, the way climate shapes surface-world cultures, but it is not the subject. The subject is always the people.

The narrative registers Atahom supports:

**Political drama.** Stack rivalries, Guild intrigue, theological conflict, the depth race, the moral weight of letting a neighboring Stack fail. Who holds power, how they got it, what they do with it, and what it costs them.

**Generational saga.** Stories that span collapse cycles. Families measured in floors descended. What gets carried down and what gets left behind. The Lasters who inherit a world their parents built and their grandparents lost.

**Exploration narrative.** Not dungeon crawling for treasure but the genuine uncertainty of not knowing what is on the other side of the wall. First contact with an unknown Stack. A connection corridor that opens somewhere the maps say shouldn't exist. A floor that doesn't behave the way floors are supposed to behave.

**Institutional drama.** The Guild navigating its neutrality across increasingly hostile Stack politics. A cistern clock that has been running since founding day finally failing. A Year-Turn report that contains information nobody is supposed to have.

**Theological tension.** Reclaimists and Descenders disagreeing about the same observable facts. An Ascender who found something near the sealed top and came back changed. A deep delver who went far enough to have opinions about all three traditions and trusts none of them.

## The Guild as Protagonist Institution

The Adventurers Guild in Atahom is not a fantasy adventuring party. It is a **professional institution** — surveyors, cartographers, diplomats, route runners, and floor certifiers. Guild work is dangerous because the work itself is dangerous, not because the Guild seeks danger.

A Guild story is not *we cleared the dungeon and leveled up*. It is:

*We were contracted to certify Floor 424 for settlement. We lost two people doing it. We discovered a connection corridor to a Stack that isn't in our records. We have three Fists to decide what to report, to whom, and what we're not going to say in writing.*

Guild members are professionals doing a job that civilization depends on. They have politics, rivalries, and institutional loyalties. They have opinions about the Stacks they serve. They have access to information that powerful people want and other powerful people want suppressed. They are not heroes. They are not mercenaries. They are, in the most precise sense, **indispensable**.

## Tone and Register

The emotional register of Atahom is not post-apocalyptic grief. The world is not broken. The Collapse is not a catastrophe that happened — it is a cycle that keeps happening, and civilization has integrated it so thoroughly that the grief framing would feel imported and false.

The register is closer to **radical present-tense pragmatism**. You build well because craft has meaning, not because you expect permanence. You invest in relationships because they matter now, not because they will last. You go deep because the frontier is real and the frontier rewards the people willing to find it.

The closest tonal comparisons:

- The anthropological weight of Ursula K. Le Guin — culture as the subject, not the backdrop
- The willingness to let architecture be genuinely alien that runs through China Miéville
- The political texture of a Ken Liu story — moral complexity without resolution
- The generational scope of a family saga where the family is a civilization

Stories in Atahom should feel like they could only happen here. If the story would work equally well in a generic fantasy setting, it probably isn't using the world yet.
