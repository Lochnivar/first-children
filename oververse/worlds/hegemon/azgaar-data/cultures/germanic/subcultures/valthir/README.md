## Narrative Description

**"The Steadfast Holders"**

The Valthir occupy the dense forestlands and river valleys of the northern interior, a people defined by their deliberate pace and profound suspicion of change. Unlike their more ambitious Germanic cousins, the Valthir measure progress in generations rather than years, building stone halls meant to stand for centuries and planting orchards their grandchildren will harvest.

Their settlements follow ancient patterns: a central mead hall surrounded by family steadings, each marked by boundary stones that have not moved in living memory. The Valthir hold that land belongs not to individuals but to lineages—a family's claim to a valley or forest tract is validated by the bones of ancestors buried there. This creates a society simultaneously egalitarian and rigid; a thrall's grandson may own land, but no amount of wealth can buy a claim where your dead do not rest.

Valthir expansionism is limited but inexorable. They do not conquer; they **settle**. When population pressure builds, younger sons are given axes, seed grain, and their grandfather's blessing to claim new lands. These settlements advance like a slow tide, each new steading establishing its own boundary stones, its own grove of ancestor trees. The Valthir have been expanding for four centuries, and in four more centuries they will have moved perhaps fifty miles.

**Military Character**: The Valthir fight as they live—with grim patience. Their warriors carry heater shields and broad-bladed axes, forming shield walls that advance at walking pace. They do not raid; they do not pursue broken enemies. When Valthir go to war, it is to hold ground, to make a stand at a river crossing or forest path, to demonstrate that **this far, no further**. Their enemies often win battles against them but rarely win wars—the Valthir simply return, rebuild, replant, and wait.

**Relationship to the Strategists**: The Valthir seem peculiarly resistant to First Children influence, though whether this is strength or blindness remains debated. Their commitment to tradition and ancestral lands makes them poor pieces for the game—moving them requires generation-long pressure. Some Strategists ignore them entirely; others see their stability as a useful anchor around which more volatile cultures orbit.
