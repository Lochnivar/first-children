## Narrative Description

**"The River Lords"**

The Meridian occupy the lush river valleys and wine-dark forests of the western Latin lands—territory so rich that it sustained sophisticated civilization before the old empire reached it, and continues to sustain sophisticated civilization now that the empire has fallen. Meridian culture is older than Veridian imperialism, deeper than Solemnium commerce, more refined than Aurelian independence—or so the Meridian themselves insist.

The Meridian are organized around **cours nobles**—noble courts that function as centers of culture, politics, and economic power. A cours might be based in a single castle or dispersed across several estates, but it always includes a lord, a council of vassals, a household of administrators, and a salon of artists and intellectuals. These courts compete for prestige through patronage, hosting elaborate festivals, sponsoring poets and painters, and demonstrating refined taste in everything from architecture to wine.

This creates a culture obsessed with **aesthetics and manners**. Meridian nobles learn to compose poetry, appreciate music, debate philosophy, and evaluate wine vintages with the same dedication other cultures devote to warfare. A Meridian court session might spend hours debating the proper interpretation of a classical text or the superior vintage of a particular vineyard. Foreigners mock this as decadence; the Meridian respond that civilization is precisely the refinement of such matters—anyone can swing a sword, but properly appreciating a metaphor requires education.

**Political Structure**: The Meridian King is elected from among the high nobility by the cours nobles—a system that ensures the monarch has political support but also means monarchs spend their reigns managing the courts that elevated them. Meridian politics is a endless dance of alliance and betrayal, conducted through marriages, appointments, and carefully worded letters. Actual warfare between Meridian lords is rare (though dueling is common); political warfare is constant.

This political culture produces remarkable stability through controlled instability. No single court can dominate, so coalitions form and dissolve continuously. The king balances competing interests, granting favors here and withdrawing them there. Ambitious nobles rise through skill and patronage; incompetent nobles fall through scandal and bankruptcy. The system is inefficient but resilient—three centuries of this has produced no civil wars, though countless political careers have ended in disgrace.

**Economic Power**: Meridian lands produce wine, grain, fine textiles, and luxury goods. Their workshops employ skilled artisans whose products command premium prices across the Latin world and beyond. But the Meridian's real wealth is **cultural capital**—Meridian language, Meridian aesthetics, Meridian philosophy set trends across multiple cultures. A Sturmgaard merchant might grow rich, but his daughter will learn Meridian poetry to prove she's cultured.

**Military Character**: The Meridian maintain professional armies, but their real military innovation is the **chevalier tradition**—heavy cavalry whose training emphasizes individual combat excellence over formation discipline. Meridian knights train from childhood in horsemanship, swordsmanship, and the code of chivalry that governs their conduct. They fight in tournaments as often as battles, and the distinction between the two is sometimes unclear.

This produces warriors who are individually formidable but difficult to coordinate. Meridian armies can be brilliant or disastrous depending on whether their nobles cooperate or compete for glory. Wise Meridian commanders structure battles to channel competitive instincts productively; foolish ones try to impose discipline and watch their knights ignore orders to pursue personal valor.

**Cultural Philosophy**: The Meridian believe civilization's purpose is not mere survival or conquest but the cultivation of **excellence**—in art, in thought, in conversation, in living itself. They see themselves as preserving what's best about Latin culture while shedding imperial arrogance. They value subtlety over force, persuasion over command, beauty over utility. Whether this represents wisdom or decadence is, naturally, a matter of perspective.

**Relationship to the Strategists**: The Meridian's emphasis on individual excellence and court politics makes them ideal ground for Strategist manipulation. A whispered suggestion to the right poet, a strategic marriage alliance, a carefully planted rumor—these can reshape Meridian politics without anyone realizing external forces were involved. Some Strategists treat Meridian courts as their personal entertainment, staging elaborate political dramas for their own amusement. Others find the Meridian's obsession with aesthetics tiresome and focus elsewhere. A few Meridian philosophers suspect Strategist influence and write treatises on "invisible hands in history," but such works are typically dismissed as paranoid speculation.
