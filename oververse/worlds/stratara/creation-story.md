# Creation Story: Stratara

**Status:** World-Specific Expansion  
**Category:** World Creation / History  
**Related:** [World Creation](../../mechanics/world-creation.md), [First Children](../../cosmology/first-children.md), [../README.md](README.md)

## Overview

Stratara was created as a collaborative experiment by 4-6 First Children siblings who decided to work together on a new world. What began as an amicable experiment in "controlled imbalance" quickly devolved into a conquest game as ownership questions emerged.

## The Collaboration

### Initial Concept

**Purpose:** An experiment in "controlled imbalance"
- The First Children wanted to explore how collaborative creation would work
- They aimed to create a world that demonstrated dynamic equilibrium
- The concept was to pool their Quantumancy to shape a planet-sized "board"

### The Creation Process

**Joint Authorship:**
- Multiple First Children pooled their Quantumancy
- They shaped Stratara as a living world divided into territories
- Territories weren't arbitrary—they were natural divisions created by quantum fluxes
- Unstable rift zones created natural borders (chokepoints) that functioned like game boundaries

**Quantum Foundation:**
- The world's "rules" were baked into its quantum substrate
- Territories are coherent energy patterns that First Children can subtly influence
- Mortals perceive this as "fickle fates" or magical boons/curses
- Everything operates within the universal quantum foundation—no separate realms

## The Devolution

### Ownership Questions

**The Conflict:**
- One First Child claimed primacy as the "lead author"
- Others challenged this claim, sparking proxy wars
- The question "whose world is it?" became central to the conflict
- This demonstrates how collaborative creations can become contested

**Framework Alignment:**
- Per framework: "Worlds may be shaped by one sibling alone (most common), by several in collaboration (possible but not necessarily common—raises questions of ownership/control, 'whose world is it?'—potential fuel for conflicts)"
- Stratara demonstrates this exact dynamic

### The Game Emerges

**From Experiment to Competition:**
- What was meant to be educational became competitive
- First Children began using mortals as proxies in their conflicts
- The world became a "board" where they competed for control
- Conquest replaced collaboration

## Peer Judgment

### External Observation

**Other First Children:**
- First Children not involved in Stratara's creation observe from afar
- They debate whether this "game" violates moralistic rules
- Some argue it's fine if balanced and educational
- Others see it as immature sibling squabbling

**Moralistic Gray Areas:**
- Is it abusive to mortals to use them as game pieces?
- Or is it educational, teaching mortals about dynamic equilibrium?
- Framework allows for these gray areas—peer judgment determines legality

### Potential Liberation

**If Things Escalate:**
- If the game becomes too destructive, it could trigger a liberation operation
- An external First Child might sneak in souls from another world (via greyrose-like anchors)
- These transferred souls could tip the scales and "reset" the board
- This demonstrates liberation operation patterns from the framework

## Framework Conformance

**Conforms to Framework:**
- Collaborative creation is possible (framework allows it)
- Ownership questions are expected (framework acknowledges this)
- Peer judgment determines legality (framework rule)
- Liberation operations can occur (framework pattern)
- All mechanics operate through quantum manipulation (framework requirement)

**World-Specific Expansions:**
- The "game" concept is Stratara-specific
- Territory system as quantum nodes is world-specific
- The devolution from collaboration to competition is Stratara's unique story
